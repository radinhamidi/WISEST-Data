We calculated pooled odds ratios (OR) and 95% CIs.Results: In total, 17 studies comprising 68,448 participants were eligible for the meta-analysis and showed that MPB is associated withan increased risk of aggressive PC (OR  1/4 1.59; 95% CI: 1.36-1.86; P o 0.001) as well as BPH (OR  1/4 1.26; 95% CI: 1.05-1.51; P  1/40.01). IntroductionMale pattern baldness (MPB) is the most common causeof hair loss mediated by systemic androgens and geneticfactors, which affects up to 70% of men and increases withage [1]. Androgens and the androgen receptor are requiredfor expression of the male phenotype. However, MPB is anandrogen-independent genetic disorder which is induced viathe activation of androgen receptor in hair follicles bydihydrotestosterone. Inhibition of dihydrotestosterone pro-duction can suppress the progression of MPB which canexplain the phenomenon that eunuchs do not developbaldness if castrated before the age of 25 [2]. Androgenshave also been strongly implicated in the carcinogenesis ofprostate cancer (PC) and the development and maintenanceof benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH) [3,4]. In addition,previous studies identified that finasteride, a 5a-reductaseinhibitor, is the main agents for MPB and BPH because ofthey respond to the inhibition of 5a-reductase. Therefore,MPB seems to share similarly pathologic mechanisms withPC and BPH for aging and androgen influence.PC is one of the most commonly diagnosed cancers andone of the most common causes of cancer related death indeveloped countries. Although some risk factors have beenestablished for PC, they collectively explain only a fractionof the disease occurrence. Therefore, additional research toimprove our understanding of the etiology of PC is needed.Several studies have investigated the relationship betweenMPB and PC and have yielded inconsistent results [5-9].

Therefore, the aim of this study is to conduct acomprehensive meta-analysis of existing studies to confirmthe association of MPB with PC and BPH.2. MethodsThis study protocol was approved by the institutionalreview board at the West China School of Medicine beforeinitiation and it did not need ethical standard statement.2.1. Data sources and searchesAsystematicliteraturesearchintheMEDLINE,EMBASE, and Cochrane databases was conducted toidentify relevant studies which reported the association ofMPB with the risk of PC and BPH published up to January2017. Additional manual searches were made using thereference lists from relevant studies to retrieve other papersrelevant to our topic.
2.2. Data extraction and study qualityTwo reviewers reviewed the full texts of the includedstudies which met the following inclusion criteria wereincluded in the meta-analysis: (1) The risk point estimatewas reported as an odds ratio (OR) with the 95% CI, or thedata were presented such that a OR and 95% CI could becalculated; (2) the study had a cohort design or retrospectivecase-control design or randomized control design; (3) thestudy evaluated MPB with PC or BPH; and (4) the studylanguage was published in English. Studies that did notmeet the earlier criteria were excluded. Data were extractedvia a standardized data extraction form, collecting informa-tion on the year of publication, country, study design,number of cases and controls, type of alopecia, severity ofalopecia, and PC stage, when available. The methodologicalquality of included studies was assessed by 2 authors usingthe Downs and Black tool for both randomized controlledtrials and nonrandomized controlled trial [11]. In general,the score ranges were grouped into the following 4 qualitylevels: excellent (26-28), good (20-25), fair (15-19), andpoor (o14).2.3. Statistical analysis and meta-analysisHeterogeneity, that is, the proportion of variability acrossstudies was assessed using Cochran's Q statistic andquantified using the I2 statistic. If high heterogeneity(I2 4 50) was found, influence analysis (one kind methodof sensitivity analysis) would be performed. We would takeout included studies one by one ("leave one out") to see
which study influenced the heterogeneity of the meta-analysis. Pooled estimates were calculated omitting onestudy at a time. For all statistical analyses, a 2-sidedP o 0.05 was considered statistically significant. Dataanalysis were performed with Review Manager Software(RevMan v.5.2, Cochrane Collaboration, Oxford, UK).3. DiscussionThe main finding of our meta-analysis is that baldness ispositively associated with an increased risk of aggressivePC as well as BPH, compared to those without baldness.Our study is the most comprehensive meta-analysis to datethat examines the association between MPB and prostatedisease with 68,448 participants. It is the first meta-analysis
to examine the relationship between MPB and aggressivePC and BPH. In the literature, it has been one previousmeta-analysis on the effect of androgenetic alopecia on PC[10]. That meta-analysis assessed 7 case-control studies(n 1/48,994) and reported that vertex baldness wasassociated with an increased risk of PC but that frontalbaldness was not. However, that study had some limita-tions, including that it only considered the relationshipbetween alopecia and PC, subtype-specific analyses by PCaggressiveness were not presented. Our meta-analysis over-comes these limitations by incorporating 17 studies and byincluding analyses of the relationships between MPB,aggressive PC, and BPH.The pathological mechanisms underlying the relationshipbetween MPB and prostate disease have yet to be clearlyestablished. The leading hypothesis is that androgens playan extremely important role in the pathophysiology of bothPC and baldness, given that androgens profoundly affect thedevelopment of PC as well as the physiological growth ofscalp and body hair. A lot of evidence from basic scienceand observational studies supports the hypothesis. Eunuchsrarely diagnosed with PC and did not go bald. Castrationhas a palliative effect on PC and castrated men also do notgo bald [25]. Testosterone alone can produce prostaticadenocarcinoma [25]. Bald men have been shown to have
a greater number of androgen receptors in the scalp andhigher serum levels of testosterone [17]. As well as menwith androgen insensitivity syndrome or testicular femini-zation, in whom functional androgen receptors are lacking,also do not go bald [25]. In addition to androgens, there aresome other similarities between the 2 disorders such asaging and hereditability. PC is age-dependent disease,which its incidence increases that come with age, as wellas going bald.BPH is a disease that impairs the quality of life in manyaging men. Previous studies have suggested an associationbetween MPB and BPH with respect to aging and endog-enous hormones. Epidemiologic associations between MPBand BPH have been studied in face of a possible commonunderlying mechanism for both conditions. MPB preva-lence increases with age, as well as BPH which is age-related prevalence of 50% for men in the 51 to 60 years agegroup and up to 70% in the 61 to 70 years age group. LikeMPB, BPH is also androgen-dependent entity, whichandrogens play a permissive role in the development ofprostatic growth [4]. Previous evidence showed that indi-viduals with innate 5a-reductase deficiency did not go MPBor BPH during their lifetime [26]. These findings mayunderlie a basic mechanism that could potentially explainthe results we observed in present study. However, further
researches are necessary to better understand the potentialassociation between PC, BPH, and MPB.Although the association between MPB and PC has beeninconsistent, recent cohort studies suggested a positiverelationship [6,10,18]. Our findings suggest that bald menat any age is significantly associated with aggressive PCrisk relative no baldness, regardless of the classification(any, frontal, vertex, and frontal plus vertex baldness). Onesuch cohort study [18] comprised 39,070 men, 1,138 ofwhom were diagnosed with PC during 2.78 years of follow-up. This study predicted a 1.19 times higher risk ofaggressive PC among men with frontal plus moderatevertex baldness at age 45 years than those without baldness.Another cohort study, which assessed androgenetic alopeciaat ages 20 and 40 years, found a 1.81 times higher risk ofPC by age 55 years among men with vertex baldness at age40 years than those without baldness. In addition, recentcase-control study found frontal baldness was associatedwith high-stage (OR  1/4 2.61, 95% CI: 1.10-6.18) and high-grade (OR  1/4 2.20, 95% CI: 1.05-4.61) PC. The earlierresults are consistent with our findings. However, a numberof studies had found no association between MPB and PC[15,17], others had found MPB to be associated with adecreased risk of PC [8,9]. Although differences in samplesize, study design, and exposure assessment might plausiblyexplain much of the inconsistency across studies. However,there was not uniformity in the specific ages studied andexist data heterogeneity precluded dose-respond meta-anal-ysis. In a smaller case-control study, investigating apossible relation between early-onset vertex baldness atages 30 and 40 years and PC, early frontal baldness wasassociated with a decreased risk of PC (at age 30 years: OR= 0.66, 95% CI: 0.19-2.35; at age 40 years: OR = 0.88,95% CI: 0.40-1.94). Muller et al. [6] finished the cohortstudy found that the hazard of PC is 1.81 (95% CI: 1.13-2.90) times higher for those with vertex baldness than thosewith no baldness at age 55 years, however, the vertexbaldness had a 44% lower hazard than those with nobaldness at age 75 years. Other case-control study [13]observed significant associations between early-onset bald-ness and PC in African-American men. Found PC diag-nosed less than the age of 60 years, frontal baldness wasassociated with high stage (OR  1/4 6.51, 95% CI: 2.11-20.06) and high grade (OR  1/4 4.23, 95% CI: 1.47-12.14).No significant associations between baldness and PCseverity were observed for older men (age 4 60 years).With regard to the age-dependent association between MPBand risk of PC, further studies are warranted.The association between MPB phenotype (vertex, fron-tal, or vertex plus frontal baldness) and PC was alsoexamined. In the 17 included studies, they all evaluatedthe associations between different MPB and PC. Forexample, in Zhou et al's research [18], they observed apositive and statistically significant association betweenfrontal with moderate vertex baldness and aggressive PC(hazard ratio 1/4  1/4 1.39, 95% CI 1/4: 1.07-1.80). In the study
of Thomas et al. [7], they found that the increased overallrisk was more strongly linked to frontal baldness thanvertex baldness as reported by prior studies. Our poolresults of association between different MPB phenotype andPC were diverse. Meta-analysis of 10 studies revealed nostatistically significant association between frontal baldnessand PC. However, meta-analysis of 8 other studies revealedstatistically significant association between vertex baldnessand PC. For frontal plus vertex baldness, there was nosignificant association between it and PC risk. The patho-physiological differences between frontal and vertex bald-ness are unknown. In the future, as the pathophysiologies ofdifferent type of MPB become better understood, thesedistinguishing characteristics may shed additional light onpotential mechanisms linking MPB and PC.Although hormonal factors are thought to be possiblecausal factors of BPH, the association between MPB andBPH has been inconsistent. A case-control study found noassociation between baldness patterns and BPH [22]. Studyconducted by Chen et al. [19] found the presence ofbaldness was an independent risk factor for the presenceof BPH after adjusting for age, IPSS, urination time, urinaryvolume, abdominal obesity, glucose levels, insulin levels,and fibrinogen (OR  1/4 5.14; 95% CI: 1.23-47.36; P  1/40.041). Although our study has revealed a statisticallysignificant association between MPB and BPH, furtherresearches are necessary to better understand the potentialassociation between BPH and MPB.Several limitations of our meta-analysis merit discussion.First, our analysis includes 3 cohort studies and 14 case-control studies. Therefore, the confounding bias fromincluded study per se cannot be excluded. Second, althoughwe included only studies that used the Hamilton scale toevaluate baldness patterns, most studies included in thismeta-analysis assessed baldness relied on self-reportedwhich may introduce confounding factors.5. Conclusions
We found baldness is positively associated with anincreased risk of aggressive PC as well as BPH, comparedto those without baldness. Despite our findings, furtherstudies, preferably prospective cohort studies, are requiredto better elucidate these relationships and to advanceknowledge in this field.
