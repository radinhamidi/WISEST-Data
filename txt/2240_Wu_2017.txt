Zhang. E-mail: AbstractBackground The interleukin-17 (IL-17) cytokine pathway plays a key role in the development of psoriasis. Antibodiestargeting IL-17 or blocking its receptor may be a new therapeutic approach for psoriasis. To assist treatment selection indaily practice, it is essential to understand the benefit and risk profile of IL-17 antagonists.Objective We performed a meta-analysis to evaluate the efficacy and safety of IL-17 antagonists in patients withMethods We searched a number of databases for relevant randomized controlled trials (RCTs) published before May2016. The following outcomes were evaluated: Psoriasis Area and Severity Index (PASI) 75, 90, 100 response, Investiga-tor's Global Assessment (IGA) score of 0 or 1 response, adverse events (AEs) and withdrawals. The meta-analysis wasperformed using Review Manager 5.2 software.Results Nine RCTs with 5951 patients were included. IL-17 antagonists achieved higher PASI 75, 90, 100 responserates and Dermatology Life Quality Index 0 or 1 response rates than placebo and a lower incidence of discontinuationsdue to lack of efficacy. In the safety analysis, no significant differences were found between the IL-17 antagonists andplacebo in the proportion of patients with serious AEs, cardiovascular disease and discontinuations due to AEs. How-ever, IL-17 antagonists were associated with a higher proportion of patients with any AEs and infections than placebo.
Conclusion IL-17 antagonists were effective, with an acceptable safety profile, for patients with plaque psoriasis.Vigilance because of the potential for infection will be necessary for IL-17 antagonists.Received: 27 August 2016; Accepted: 3 January 2017Conflicts of interestThe authors declare that they have no competing interests.Funding sourcesThe authors received no payment in preparation of this manuscript.
IntroductionPlaque psoriasis is a chronic, inflammatory, immune-mediatedskin disease affecting about 1-3% of the world's population.1Currently almost incurable, psoriasis is frequently accompaniedby impairment of life quality,2 several complications and dissat-isfaction with therapy.3 Traditional oral treatment for plaquepsoriasis has been limited due to intolerable side effects andorgan toxicity.4 Through our understanding of psoriasis patho-genesis, a number of available biologics under development thattarget cytokines are increasingly receiving attention.5-10An abnormal T-cell-mediated immune response plays a majorrole in the process of plaque psoriasis.11 Type 17 helper T cells(Th17 cells) produce IL-17A, which is highly expressed in psori-atic lesions and is thought to be an important cytokine in thepathogenesis of psoriasis.12-16 Keratinocyte differentiation isextensively altered in plaque psoriasis.17 Besides IL-17A, variousother cytokines, including IL-23, IL-21, IL-22 and TNF-a, pro-duced by keratinocytes are present at highly increased concen-trations in psoriatic skin.18 The cytokines IL-23 and IL-1b areable to produce large amounts of IL-17A in the skin, and thisleads to disease progression by driving Th17 cell differentiationfrom naive T cells19 and stimulating gamma delta T cells.20 Pre-clinical studies indicate antagonizing the IL-17 pathway is aDan Wu and Si-Yuan Hou contributed equally to the work.worthwhile therapeutic target.21 The newest and most promisingbiologic therapies for psoriasis are antibodies targeting IL-17 orblocking its receptor.22,23 These primarily include anti-interleu-kin-17A monoclonal antibodies (anti-IL-17A mAb), secuk-inumab (AIN457) and ixekizumab (LY2439821), and an anti-interleukin-17 receptor A monoclonal antibody (anti-IL-17RAmAb), brodalumab (AMG827), which are collectively referred toas 'IL-17 antagonists' in the following discussion.24
In recent years, phase 3 randomized controlled trials (RCTs)of IL-17 antagonists for the treatment of psoriasis have beenreported. To provide an up-to-date picture of the clinical effi-cacy and safety of IL-17 antagonists in psoriasis, we conducted ameta-analysis.MethodsEligibility criteriaStudies included in this meta-analysis met the follow criteria: (i)they were phase 3 RCTs that enrolled patients with plaque psori-asis; (ii) their duration of treatment was >=12 weeks; (iii) theyused a parallel design or crossover design of IL-17 antagonistsvs. placebo; and (iv) their data reported information on PsoriasisArea and Severity Index (PASI) response, Dermatology LifeQuality Index (DLQI) score of 0 or 1 response, adverse events(AEs) and discontinuations.Study identificationWe searched all relevant studies published before May 2016 onthe electronic databases MEDLINE, Embase and CochraneLibrary, using the following search terms: 'psoriasis', 'secuk-inumab', 'brodalumab', 'ixekizumab', 'anti-IL-17', 'IL-17 inhibi-tor' or 'IL-17 antagonists'. Additionally, Center Watch ClinicalTrials Listing Service (), CurrentControlled Trials Service () andclinical trials registered at ClinicalTrials.gov () were searched for details of any relevant clinical trials inprogress.Study selection was performed by two independent investiga-tors. First, titles and abstracts of cited articles were evaluated bytwo investigators to determine their relevance. Then, full paperswere reviewed to identify all trials meeting the eligibility criteria.Disagreements were resolved through discussion or by consensuswith a third investigator. When there were multiple articles fromthe same trial, the most completely or recently reported datawere included.
Methodological quality and riskMethodological quality of included articles was assessed usingmodified Jadad criteria with an eight-item scale (randomization,method of randomization, blinding, method of blinding, with-drawals and dropouts, inclusion and exclusion criteria, adverseeffects and statistical analysis) by two independent reviewers.25Scores ranged from 0 to 8 (a high score indicating high quality).Scores of 4-8 denoted high quality and 0-3 poor or low quality.Meta-analysisThe analysis of efficacy was based on the proportion of patientswith a 75%, 90% and 100% improvement from baseline in thePASI score and the proportion of patients who achieved a DLQIscore of 0 or 1. We evaluated safety by reviewing AEs, includingany AEs, serious adverse events (SAEs), infection and cardiovas-cular disease (CVD). Additionally, discontinued treatment dueto an AE or unsatisfactory therapeutic effect was analysed toassess safety and efficacy, respectively.The statistical analysis was performed using Review Manager5.2 (The Nordic Cochrane Centre, Copenhagen, Denmark) fromthe Cochrane Collaboration. All analysis indicators, which werecategorical dichotomous variables, were assessed using relativerisks (RR). P < 0.05 was considered to be statistically significant,and 95% confidence intervals (CI) were reported. Homogeneitywas tested by the I2 statistic (significance level at I2 > 50%).26 Arandom-effects model was used if the I2 statistic was significant.Otherwise, a fixed-effects model was used. To assess the poten-tial confounding effect of heterogeneity, subgroup analyses foreach of the IL-17 antagonists were performed.Results
Literature searchA total of 405 relevant articles were identified from various data-bases, of which 359 articles were excluded after reviewing thetitles and abstracts. Then, an additional 37 articles were excludeddue to various reasons (not RCTs, not phase 3 trials or norequired data). EfficacyPASI 75 response Nine RCTs with 5941 patients reported theproportion of patients with a 75% improvement from baselinein the PASI score (PASI 75) at 12 weeks. IL-17 antagonists wereassociated with a higher PASI 75 response rate than the placebogroup (RR = 18.85, 95% CI: 15.04-23.63, P < 0.00001) with nosignificant heterogeneity between studies (P = 0.36, I2 = 9%;Fig. 2).PASI 90 response Nine RCTs with 5941 patients reportedPASI 90 response rates. IL-17 antagonists were associated with ahigher rate of PASI 90 responders than the placebo group(RR = 48.32, 95% CI: 31.48-74.15, P < 0.00001) with no signifi-cant heterogeneity between studies (P = 0.39, I2 = 6%; Fig. 3).PASI 100 response Nine RCTs with 5941 patients reported thePASI 100 response rates at 12 weeks. IL-17 antagonists wereassociated with a higher rate of PASI 100 responders than theplacebogroup(RR = 72.06,95%CI:34.13-152.16,P < 0.00001) without significant heterogeneity between studies(P = 0.64, I2 = 0%; Fig. 4).DLQI score of 0 or 1 response rate Five RCTs with 3625patients reported the DLQI score of 0 or 1 response rate at12 weeks. The IL-17 antagonists group was associated with ahigher rate of DLQI 0 or 1 response than the placebo group(RR = 7.11, 95% CI: 4.18-12.09, P < 0.00001) with significantheterogeneity between studies (P = 0.0005, I2 = 80%; Fig. 5).No studies reporting DLQI response for brodalumab werefound.SafetyThe meta-analysis results for safety showed that the IL-17 antag-onists were associated with a higher proportion of patients withany AEs (RR = 1.22, 95% CI: 1.15-1.29, P < 0.00001) with nosignificant heterogeneity between studies (P = 0.49, I2 = 0%;Fig. 6), but no increase in the proportion of patients with SAEs
(OR = 1.20, 95% CI: 0.75-1.92, P = 0.46), with no significantheterogeneity between studies (P = 0.97, I2 = 0%), comparedwith the placebo group (Fig. 7).The interest in AEs that accompany IL-17 treatment for psori-asis focuses on infection and CVD. The most commonlyreported infection events were nasopharyngitis, upper respira-tory tract infection (URTI), viral URTI, pharyngitis, sinusitis orrhinitis. IL-17 antagonists were associated with a higher infec-tion proportion than the placebo group (RR = 1.29, 95% CI:1.06-1.57, P = 0.01) with significant heterogeneity (P = 0.02,I2 = 56%; Fig. 8). Only four studies with 2143 patients receivingIL-17 antagonists and 700 patients receiving placebo reportedCVD. The results showed no significant difference between theIL-17 antagonists group and the placebo group (RR = 1.30, 95%CI: 0.35-4.91, P = 0.70). There was no significant heterogeneitybetween studies (P = 0.15, I2 = 47%; Fig. 9).In addition, we need to pay attention on the brodalumab.There are serious safety concerns involving suicide with thisdrug. In our included articles,31 four patients died from suicideafter receiving brodalumab. However, the investigator reportedthat there was no reasonable possibility that the suicide wasrelated to brodalumab.DiscontinuationsThe most common reasons for discontinuations were unsatisfac-tory therapeutic effect or unable to tolerate AEs. In five RCTs,significantly fewer patients receiving IL-17 antagonists werewithdrawn due to unsatisfactory efficacy than patients receivingplacebo (RR = 0.20, 95% CI: 0.08-0.49, P = 0.0004). In eightRCTs, the rates of discontinuations due to AEs did not differ sig-nificantly between IL-17 antagonists and placebo (RR = 1.16,95% CI: 0.65-2.07, P = 0.62; Fig. 10).
DiscussionThe meta-analysis results of phase 3 clinical trials indicate thatIL-17 antagonists are effective agents in the treatment of patientswith psoriasis. At week 12, a significantly greater percentage ofpatients receiving IL-17 antagonists compared with placeboachieved PASI 75, PASI 90, PASI 100 and DLQI 0/1. AlthoughIL-17 antagonists had a higher rate of any AEs and infectionthan placebo, there was no significant difference in SAEs anddiscontinuations due to AEs. This suggests that IL-17 antago-nists are well tolerated.Normally, achieving PASI 75 has been regarded as a successfultreatment objective,36 but now, achieving PASI 90 or PASI 100has become a new therapeutic goal.37 A study finding38 showedthat patients with moderate-to-severe plaque psoriasis whoachieve PASI 90 or PASI 100 experience greater improvement inquality of life than PASI 75 responders. Our research showed IL-17 antagonists not only improved psoriasis symptoms andresulted in superior PASI response but also improved DLQIresponse rates compared with placebo. In fact, four patients with psoriasis died from sui-cide after receiving brodalumab. Although concerning, the sui-cides have no a causative relationship with brodalumab,especially given that patients with psoriasis are already at a highrisk of depression and suicide attempt. The long-term pharma-covigilance will help resolve this issue.One limitation of our study was that we did not comparethe efficacies or AEs between different IL-17 antagonist dosesor different end points. However, from the included studyresults, we found that the secukinumab 300-mg dose wasmore effective than the 150-mg dose, suggesting that thereis a dose-response effect. We also found the PASI score wassignificantly improved as early as 1-4 weeks in IL-17 antago-nists28,48-52 compared to placebo. These rapid effects oftenoccurred in the higher-dose groups, which suggests that IL-17 antagonists may be more suitable for patients who wantto improve the clinical symptoms and quality of life as soonas possible. Another limitation was that IL-17 antagonistsare in ongoing clinical trials and not widely used in clinicaltreatment for patients with psoriasis. Hence, more high-qual-ity, large prospective RCTs with long-term follow-up areneeded.In summary, IL-17 antagonists were effective and well toler-ated. They could provide a credible alternative for patients withmoderate-to-severe psoriasis, or even those with heart disease.Vigilance with the potential for infection will be necessary forpatients administered IL-17 antagonists.
