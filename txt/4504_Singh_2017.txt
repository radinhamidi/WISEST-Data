Armstrong, MD, MPHd,eDavis, Los Angeles, and Sacramento, California, and Philadelphia, PennsylvaniaBackground: Psoriasis is associated with psychiatric comorbidities; however, the relationship betweenpsoriasis and suicidality is not well understood.Objective: To perform a systematic review and meta-analysis that elucidates the relationship betweenpsoriasis and suicidality.Methods: Applying the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses guidelines,we systematically searched the PubMed, EMBASE, PsycINFO, and Cochrane databases. We searchedliterature published between 1946 and 2017.Results: We identified 18 studies with a total of 1,767,583 participants, of whom 330,207 had psoriasis. Onthe basis of random effects modeling, the pooled odds ratio (OR) for suicidal ideation among patients withpsoriasis was 2.05 (95% confidence interval [CI], 1.54-2.74). Patients with psoriasis were more likely toexhibit suicidal behaviors (combined attempted and completed suicides) with a pooled OR of 1.26 (95% CI,1.13-1.40). Subgroup analysis showed that patients with psoriasis were more likely to attempt suicides (OR,1.32; 95% CI, 1.14-1.54) and complete suicide (OR, 1.20; 95% CI, 1.04-1.39) than those without psoriasis.More severe psoriasis and younger age were associated with greater likelihood of suicidality.
Limitations: There are few studies examining suicidality in conjunction with psoriasis severity.Conclusions: Patients with psoriasis have a significantly higher likelihood of suicidal ideation, suicideattempts, and completed suicides. Among patients with psoriasis, those who are younger and whosepsoriasis is more severe are at particular risk for suicidality. ( J Am Acad Dermatol 2017;77:425-40.)Key words: completed suicide; meta-analysis; prevalence; psoriasis; suicidal ideation; suicidality; suicideattempts; systematic review.Psoriasis is a chronic, inflammatory skin disor-der that affects 2% to 4% of the generalpopulation, with recent estimates suggestingmore than 125 million affected individuals world-wide.1,2 Psoriasis is characterized by pruritic andpainful lesions that can significantly affect patientquality of life, even when the disease is not exten-sive.3 Additionally, psoriasis has been associated withextracutaneous comorbidities such as inflammatoryarthritis and cardiometabolic disorders,4,5 which alsoadd to the overall burden of disease.

Studies have reported that up to half of patientswith psoriasis experienced feelings of profoundhelplessness and isolation on account of the func-tional limitations and social and psychologicalimpact of their disease.6,7 Because of its pervasivenature, psoriasis has been thought to contribute tothe development and progression of psychiatricdisorders. One study foundthat patients with psoriasisare 1.5 times more likely tohave depression and morethan 4 times as likely to useantidepressants as controlswithout psoriasis.8 Patientswith psoriasis are also athigherriskofreceivingadiagnosisofanxietydisorder than the generalpopulation.9,10There is a gap in the liter-ature examining the associa-tion between psoriasis andsuicidality. Population-basedstudies suggest that patientswith psoriasis may have an increased prevalence ofsuicidality. Suicidality may be defined as suicidalideation, suicide attempts, or completed suicides. Ofthese 3 aspects, suicide attempts and completedsuicidesarecollectivelyconsideredsuicidalbehavior. In this systematic review and meta-analysis, we aim to synthesize the available literatureon the epidemiologic association between psoriasisand the various aspects of suicidality.
METHODSToexaminetheepidemiologicassociationbetween psoriasis and suicidality, we followed thePreferred Reporting Items for Systematic ReviewsandMeta-AnalysesguidelinestoperformasystematicreviewsearchusingthePubMed,EMBASE, PsycINFO, and Cochrane databases. Thereview protocol was registered with PROSPERO(CRD42017054446). We used the following searchcriteria: (Psoriasis [MeSH]) AND (Suicide [MeSH] ORSuicide, Attempted [MeSH] OR suicid*). We used theterm suicid* to include other variations of the wordsuch as suicidal or suicidality. The search was limitedto English-language studies and included studiespublished from database inception to the present,1946e2017. The initial search yielded 261 references(Fig 1).Two authors (S.S. and C.T.) read referenceabstracts to determine eligibility for inclusion inthesystematicreview.Weappliedinclusion/exclusion criteria to 200 unique references. For this
meta-analysis, we focused on noninterventionalstudies (eg, case-control, cross-sectional, and cohortstudies) to examine the prevalence of suicidalityrather than change in suicidality in response tointerventions. The articles must have also fulfilledall of the following inclusion criteria: the article mustbe written in English; the study participants must beadults ($18 years); the diag-nosis of psoriasis must bedocumented;suicidalitymust be a primary or second-ary end point, and it must bedocumented through use ofaquestionnaire/surveyorpatient medical records; andsuicidality must be assessedin conjunction with psoriasisandnumericallyreported(numbersofpatientsaffected,percentvalues,odds ratio [OR] or hazardratio[HR]).Casereports,case series, review articles,letters,andcommentarieswere excluded. We identified 75 articles to bereviewed in full. In the process of screening articlesfor inclusion, we identified 2 additional articles forinclusion by manually searching article references.S.S. and C.T. independently reviewed 75 full-textarticles for eligibility and excluded articles with thefollowing characteristics because they did not fulfillthe inclusion criteria for the systematic review: noreport of suicidality in conjunction with psoriasis(n = 7), full-length article unavailable (n = 5), casereport/series/review article (n = 21), suicidality notevaluated or prevalence not reported (n = 12), pso-riatic arthritis only (n = 1), nonobservational studies(n = 9), and included pediatric patients (n = 2). Afterthese exclusions, 18 unique articles were eligible forinclusion in our systematic review.During data extraction for this systematic review,discrepancies were resolved by consensus amongS.S., C.T., H.K., and A.W.A. For each study, werecorded the study year; study setting; patient de-mographic characteristics (age and sex); prevalenceof suicidal ideation, attempts, or completion; anddiagnoses of depression and anxiety where avail-able. To measure study quality, we used a validatedassessment tool for observational studies.11,12
Of the 18 included studies, 13 examined at least 1aspectofsuicidality(ideation,attempts,orcompleted) in patients with psoriasis versus incontrols without psoriasis. Distinct from suicidal ideation, suicidal behaviorsor acts typically refer to suicide attempts andcompleted suicides. Eleven out of 18 studies exam-ined suicide attempts and completed suicides inpatients with psoriasis and cohorts without psoriasis.Pompili et al reported that 6.6% of the patients withpsoriasis had attempted suicide at some point duringtheir life versus 0% of the controls without psoriasisbut with other skin conditions.30 One study exam-ined occurrence of new suicide attempts andcompleted suicides per year among 68,511 patientswith psoriasis. The incidence rates of suicide at-tempts in patients with psoriasis and matchedcontrols were 1.43 and 1.00 per 10,000 person-years, respectively. The incidence rates of completedsuicides in patients with psoriasis and matchedcontrols were 2.03 and 1.64 per 10,000 person-years, respectively. Two additional studies examinedcompleted suicide and reported higher prevalenceor incidence of suicide completion in patients withpsoriasis than in cohorts without psoriasis.18,27After synthesizing data on suicidal behavior from7 studies, we found that compared with controlswithout psoriasis, patients with psoriasis are morelikely to attempt and complete suicides (pooled OR,1.26; 95% CI, 1.13-1.40) (Fig 3). A funnel plot of thesestudies suggested limited publication bias, which
was confirmed by the Egger regression test (P = .101)(Supplemental Fig 2; available at ). The I2 value of 0 suggests limited heterogene-ity. On the basis of our sensitivity analysis examiningsuicide attempts and complete suicides separately,patients with psoriasis have a higher likelihood ofsuicide attempts (pooled OR, 1.32; 95% CI,1.14-1.54)and completed suicide (pooled OR, 1.20; 95% CI,1.04-1.39) than patients without psoriasis.Three studies stratified patients with psoriasis byage when examining suicidality.16,18,24 The studiesreported that younger patients were more likely toexperience suicidality than older patients. This wasevidenced by a large study of 916,948 participants,which found that compared with controls withoutpsoriasis, patients with psoriasis who were in their20s had a higher risk of suicidality (HR, 1.83; 95% CI,1.64-2.05) than patients with psoriasis aged 60 yearsor older (HR, 1.04; 95% CI, 0.91-1.20).24In 5 studies, the authors stratified patients withpsoriasisbydiseaseseverity.16,24,27,29,32Threestudies found that patients with severe psoriasis aremore likely to experience suicidal ideation andsuicideattemptsthancontrolswithoutpsoria-sis.16,19,24 Specifically, Rapp et al reported thatpatients with psoriasis with suicidal ideation hadhigher disease severity scores than patients withpsoriasiswithoutsuicidalideation(Self-Administered Psoriasis Area and Severity Index score
of 11.47 vs. 6.23, respectively).16 One study exam-ining suicide attempts and suicide completion foundthat compared with controls, patients with mildpsoriasis disease had a lower rate of suicide attempts(1.17 per 10,000 person-years) than patients withsevere psoriasis (2.52 per 10,000 person-years). Adose-dependent relationship was not seen for sui-cide completion.29DISCUSSIONPopulation-based studies have posited a relation-ship between psoriasis and psychiatric comorbid-ities.10,33-35 This systematic review has examined theliterature to assess data from 18 studies with a total of1,767,583 study participants, of whom 330,207 werepatients with psoriasis.This study showed that patients with psoriasishave increased odds of all aspects of suicidality(ideation, attempts, and completion) comparedwith the general population. This finding was mostpronounced for suicidal ideation. Patients with pso-riasis are twice as likely to contemplate suicide apeople without psoriasis. Additionally, patients withpsoriasis had a higher likelihood of suicide attemptsand completed suicides. Patients with psoriasis had a32% higher likelihood of attempting suicide thanthose without psoriasis. They also had a 20% higherlikelihood of completed suicide than people withoutpsoriasis.
Patients with more severe psoriasis were morelikely to contemplate and attempt suicides. Greaterseverity of psoriasis has been associated with greatersocial stigmatization, substantially impaired qualityof life, and higher depression rates, all of which cancontribute to greater suicidal ideation and behavior.Thus, controlling psoriasis will not only have asignificant impact on morbidity but can also affectsuicide-related morality. This finding also highlightsthe importance of establishing effective treatmentgoals to monitor and reduce the severity of psoriasisdisease.36Another key finding from this investigation isthat compared with patients without psoriasis,younger patients with psoriasis are especially atrisk for suicidal ideation, suicidal attempts, andcompletedsuicides.Althoughlittleisknownregarding the reason for this, international studiesinvolving the general population have shown thatwhen the data are adjusted for socioeconomicstatus and degree of hardship, younger patientsare more likely to commit suicide than olderpatients.37,38 It is possible that many youngerpatients with psoriasis have not yet developedeffective disease-coping mechanisms or may bemore prone to impulsive behavior. Therefore,interventionsaimedathelpingyoungadultscope with psoriasis and its mental comorbiditiesare paramount.Several possible biologic mechanisms have beenproposed to explain the epidemiologic associationbetween psoriasis and psychiatric comorbidities.39
Multiple studies have reported an association be-tween the proinflammatory cytokines interleukin 6(IL-6), IL-1, IL-17, tumor necrosis factor-a, anddepression.40-46 Studies examining this link haveidentified higher blood and cerebrospinal fluidlevels of proinflammatory cytokines in patientswithdepressionwhoareotherwisemedicallyhealthy and without inflammatory skin disease. It isbelieved that elevated cytokine levels may affectserotonin metabolism.To increase understanding of the effect of proin-flammatory cytokines on suicidal behavior, studieshave examined levels of cytokines in the cerebro-spinal fluid and brain tissue of people who attemptorcompletesuicide.47,48Onestudyreportedhigher levels of IL-6 in the cerebrospinal fluid ofthose who attempted suicide than in healthy controlswhodidnotattemptsuicide.48Anelevatedinflammatory state, as seen in psoriasis, may affectthe development and progression of depressivesymptoms and suicidal behavior; however, the exactbiologicmediatorsinvolvedareunderactiveinvestigation.This systematic review and meta-analysis needsto be interpreted in the context of the availableprimary studies. As this was a meta-analysis ofstudy-level data, there were possible sources ofheterogeneityandbiasamongtheincludedstudies. There was variability in the methodologyof assessing and reporting suicidality. Additionally,a minority of the studies examined prevalence ofcompletedsuicidesinpatientswithpsoriasisversus in controls. There were multiple uncon-trolled studies in this systematic review that werealsolimitedintermsofstudyqualitywhenassessed by using the Newcastle-Ottawa scale. Toaccount for heterogeneity among the studies, weutilized random effects analysis to calculate pooledORs.In conclusion, this systematic review and meta-analysis showed that patients with psoriasis have asignificantly higher likelihood of experiencing sui-cidal ideation, suicide attempts, and completedsuicides. Patients with psoriasis with greater diseaseseverity and younger patients are especially at riskfor suicidality. These findings suggest a need forproviders to inquire about suicidality in patients withpsoriasis.
