REVIEW ARTICLEHearing loss in vitiligo: current concepts and reviewMarrigje Aagje de Jong1 * Cahtia Adelman2 * Menachem Gross1 Received: 12 October 2016 / Accepted: 3 January 2017 (c) Springer-Verlag Berlin Heidelberg 2017IntroductionVitiligo is a common progressive depigmentating skin disorder in which melanocytes of the skin are damaged. Destruction of melanocytes takes place within the epider-mis, the mucous membranes, eyes and occasionally in some hair bulbs. Melanocytes of leptomeninges, retinal pigment epithelium, uveal tract and inner ear may also be involved. Vitiligo can appear at any age, but more often affects chil-dren and young adults, with the incidence of the disease declining with age. The disease prevalence is 0.5-1% in the general population with all ethnic groups affected. Vitiligo is characterized by the appearance of pearl-white macules of different sizes and shapes on skin and hair in the hypo-pigmentary area [1, 2]. It may be classified as localized, segmental, generalized and universal, according to the area of pigmentation loss. Functional melanocytes in patients with vitiligo disappear from the involved skin by a mecha-nism that is yet to be identified. Various theories have been proposed such as:
1. auto-immunity due to frequent associations with other auto-immune disease such as thyroiditis and type I dia-betes, the presence of anti-melanocyte antibodies and a response to immunosuppressant therapy;2. cytotoxicity the possibility that metabolites formed dur-ing melanin synthesis may destroy melanocytes;3. neural chemical mediators released at nerve endings might destroy melanocytes or inhibit melanin produc-tion;4. free radicals excess free radicals might be toxic for melanocytes;5. convergent a combination of the above [2-4].Abstract The objective of this study was to describe the occurrence, clinical manifestations, audiometric findings, pathogenesis and approach to sensorineural hearing loss (SNHL) among patients diagnosed with vitiligo with a review of the literature. We present a systematic review of the literature on cases of SNHL in patients diagnosed with vitiligo and studies conducted to investigate audiometric changes in such patients. Data on presentation, diagnosis and medical approach were reviewed. A total of 21 stud-ies and case reports revealed at least 102 cases of SNHL in patients diagnosed with vitiligo. Arguments for a com-mon causative etiology related to melanocyte function were mentioned in most of the literature. Evaluation of hearing function among all patients diagnosed with vitiligo seems to be an accepted approach; it should include audiometry, otoacoustic emissions (OAE) and ABR measurements. Extra precaution to prevent ototoxic or noise-induced hearing loss is strongly recommended. Recent clinical and experimental studies support the theory that pathogenetic mechanisms of vitiligo could be a systemic event, as vitiligo is associated with ocular and auditory abnormalities including sensorineural hearing loss (SNHL). Alphonse Corti (1831) was the first to mention the presence of pigment cells in the inner ear. Within the cochlea, melanin is found with lower relative amount in the basal area compared to the middle turn and apex [5]. How-ever, the role of the melanocytes--and that of melanin--in the inner ear is not completely understood. Melanocytes are particularly present in the modiolus, osseous spiral lamina, Reissner's membrane and the vascular stria and in highly vascularized areas of apparently important secretory or metabolic function [6]. According to Savin, cells contain-ing pigments partially or fully adhere to blood vessel walls, which are sites of intense metabolite exchanges. Therefore, it is probable that they have a vasomotor function in the inner ear. It is also known that they are necessary for the normal development and function of the stria vascularis [7-10]. Furthermore, melanocytes are involved in creating the endolymphatic potential and might act as free radical scavengers [7]. A loss of hearing in the higher frequencies may represent a decreased protective affect to free radicals by the reduced amount of melanocytes in the basal turn [5, 11, 12]. See Fig. 1.Audiological abnormalities have been described in sev-eral hypopigmentary disorders such as albinism, Waarden-burg syndrome, Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada syndrome and Alezzandrini syndrome. Carvalho found that otoacoustic emissions (OAE) and the high-frequency sensitivity audi-tory function were affected negatively in patients with pig-ment disorders [13]. Evidence of sensorineural hearing loss (SNHL) in vitiligo patients has also been reported over the last decade [14-20]. However, most patients with vitiligo are clinically asymptomatic for audiometric abnormalities. Other studies have not found such a relationship [21, 22].
Since audiology test results are conflicting, we therefore considered it worthwhile reviewing the current literature on SNHL in patients diagnosed with vitiligo and discuss the approach with such patients.Materials and methodsSearch strategy and selectionA systematic review of the literature was conducted using the databases of PubMed and the Cochrane Library on August 2016. We searched for articles relating to patients with vitiligo (domain) with audiology testing (intervention) and subsequently searched for articles relating to SNHL among those patients. Case reports as well as clinical stud-ies if they contained a combination of domain and determi-nant were also searched. Articles were excluded if the main subject was not in relation to our domain in combination with determinant or in cases where the language was other than English. We screened references and related articles and conducted a general Internet search to verify if all sig-nificant articles were included until the day of admission of the manuscript. The results, acquired from the system-atic literature review, will predominantly be presented in the tables. Case reports are presented in Table 1, but not further mentioned in the results since the data were often incomplete. Most of those articles appear in the literature between 1972 and 1992, the same period in which autoim-mune SNHL was first mentioned [23].We assessed and compared the hearing performances based on different audiology tests in patients with vitiligo in the studies. In addition to the hearing performance, we evaluated the different hypotheses about the mechanism responsible for hearing loss among vitiligo patients and proposed a treatment strategy for SNHL possibly related to vitiligo.
ResultsPure tone audiometry thresholdsMore accurate descriptions of audiology testing among vitiligo patients appear in controlled clinical studies from 1991 until the present day. Eight studies were found evaluating hearing thresholds in vitiligo patients and healthy controls based on pure tone audiometry. Although there was no SNHL found in the study first described by Escelante et al., all other studies reported a higher preva-lence of SNHL among patients with vitiligo, ranging from 4.0 to 68.8%, compared to healthy controls [16, 18, 20, 22, 29-31]. Also in the study by Hong et al., significant lower thresholds were found in Asian vitiligo patients compared to their controls [19].Most of them had a threshold of >30  dB HL. The high prevalence of SNHL in the study of Fleissig et al. included mild and minimal SNHL as well. In more recent studies, attention was given to the thresholds at different frequencies. Significantly higher (worse) thresholds were found among vitiligo patients, at frequencies between 4 and 8 kHz with P values <0.05. To a lesser extent, there 
were higher thresholds among vitiligo patients for 0.125, 1, 10 and 12.5 kHz. The test results are summarized in Table 2 [16, 18-22, 29-33]. Fleissig et al. compared the shapes of the audiograms from 16 vitiligo patients and 16 healthy controls. They found a significantly more com-mon "notch shape" audiogram in the higher frequencies for vitiligo patients, with no history of noise-induced hearing loss (P = 0.021). Otoacoustic emissionsThree prospective studies included transient OAE in their evaluation to find possible cochlear dysfunction [20, 30, 32]. They all reported on frequencies of 1-4 kHz with 70 or 75% reproducibility and a response criteria of three fre-quencies at 3 dB or greater above the noise floor. Interest-ingly, they all found failure only at higher frequencies, 3 or 4 kHz. Two studies found significantly reduced otoacoustic emission amplitude at 4 kHz [20, 32]. All healthy controls in the studies passed the test.In addition, Angrisani et al. reported that in a third of the cases they examined, suppression of OAEs was absent although white noise was presented to the contralateral ear (to test the function of the efferent pathways). 66.7% of the vitiligo patients with normal audiometry results had par-tial or total loss, among them 64.7% with bilateral failure mostly at the high frequencies (63.3%) [32]. Fleissig et al. found only 2 out of 16 patients who failed [30]. Aslan et al. found significantly reduced otoacoustic emission amplitude at 4 kHz [20]. All healthy controls in the studies passed the test. Interestingly Anbar et  al. examined 53 patients with vitiligo and normal audiograms, 60% of patients had coch-lear dysfunction based on DPOAEs, often bilateral, while their control group had no abnormality [34].In addition, Angrisani et al. reported that in a third of the cases they examined, suppression of OAEs was absent although white noise was presented to the contralateral ear (to test the function of the efferent pathways) [32].Discussion
Several case reports and studies have been published men-tioning SNHL in patients with vitiligo. Some suggest a systemic mechanism responsible for both the symptoms of depigmentation in vitiligo and the hearing loss. Although several theories have been proposed, the exact pathogen-esis remains unclear. A study by Sun et  al. reported that Afro-American individuals had greater cochlear melanin levels compared to Caucasian individuals and overall bet-ter hearing thresholds [35]. To our knowledge, no research has been conducted to investigate the presence or absence of melanocytes in the ears of patients with vitiligo com-pared to healthy controls. The first studies evaluating hear-ing performance among vitiligo patients based on audiol-ogy tests did not confirm a significantly higher prevalence of SNHL in this specific group compared to healthy con-trols. However, they compared only pure tone audiometry at 0.5-2KHz or ABR, whereas later studies found that the hearing loss among patients diagnosed with vitiligo pre-sented in the higher frequencies.More recently, a larger number of clinically controlled studies did confirm the hypothesis that SNHL is signifi-cantly more common among vitiligo patients, between 4.0 and 68.8% of patients. The higher percentage includes also very mild SNHL and was described by Fleissig et al. [30]. They also found a negative relation to mucosal onset of dis-ease and for patients more than 20 years old. In other stud-ies, variables such as the duration of disease, gender, the age of the patient and the type of vitiligo did not correlate with the hearing loss. It should be noted that only one study mentioned a clear definition of sensorineural hearing loss as a difference >=25 dB HL in at least two consecutive fre-quencies [20].Audiometric testing of patients diagnosed with vitiligo demonstrated in most cases a hearing loss in the higher frequency range, 3-4  KHz during pure tone audiometry. Fleissig et al. described a typical notch-shaped audiogram that was more frequent among vitiligo patients and showed similarity to the audiogram common in noise-induced hear-ing loss [30]. The concept of noise-induced hearing loss in pigmentation disorders has previously been published in an experimental animal study in 1986 by Conlee et al. [11]. They found a reduced hearing restoration in albino guinea pigs after noise exposure compared to a control group. It seems that acoustic trauma appears to be of greater func-tional significance in strial dysfunction and thus makes vitiligo patients even more prone to hearing loss after noise exposure.ABR measurements in patients with vitiligo by Niki-froidis et al. showed a decrease in W I peak latency and an increased I-III interpeak latency, which was later con-firmed in a study carried out by Hong et al. [15, 19]. The

The interpeak latency of wave I-III represents the action potential, synaptic activ-ity of the otoacoustic nerve and conduction at the supe-rior olivary nucleus. Whereas melanocytes are known to be involved in generating the endolymphatic potential, the role of melanocytes in retrocochlear conduction remains unclear.A third hearing test performed among patients diag-nosed with vitiligo was transient OAE [20, 30, 32]. Whereas 100% of healthy controls in all studies showed responses, a variable percentage of vitiligo patients showed partial or complete failure mostly in the higher frequency range. Interestingly, some patients showed abnormal tran-sient OAE, but had a normal audiogram. Therefore, the prevalence of cochlear impairment may be higher among vitiligo patients than found only by audiogram evaluation. In addition, the lack of suppression in some patients indi-cates the possibility of damage to the medial olivocochlear bundle [32].
ConclusionsSince SNHL seems to be more common among vitiligo patients with hearing loss in the high-frequency range, audiometry should be performed including very high fre-quencies such as 10 and 12 kHz. As there are indications of impaired cochlear function with normal audiometry results, testing of hearing function with OAE is recom-mended. Suppression of OAE should be attempted to dis-cover abnormal auditory efferent function. ABR should be performed, as abnormalities have been reported in vitiligo patients. Furthermore, since there are no variables such as age, gender or type of vitiligo that correlate to hearing loss, all patients, including those who are clinically asympto-matic, should be tested and preferably at first presentation. Further studies are necessary to determine the recommen-dations for interval and frequency of follow-up. Vitiligo patients that present with sensorineural hearing loss of sud-den onset should be treated by steroidal therapy; either by oral intake or intratympanic injection of steroids.In summary, audiological evaluation of patients with vitiligo should include audiometry and very high-frequency audiometry, OAE and ABR measurements. Incomplete evaluation might result in underdetection of hearing loss. Additionally, patients should take extra precautions to pre-vent acoustic trauma and avoid ototoxic drugs, especially during active disease, as their auditory system may be less protected due to nonfunctional melanocytes.To fully understand the mechanism by which melano-cytes function in relation to hearing, we recommend further research among vitiligo patients to gain a better under-standing of the audiology functions and cell mechanisms in the absence of functional melanocytes in the ear.Compliance with ethical standards Conflict of interest The authors report no financial interests or po-tential conflicts of interest.References
