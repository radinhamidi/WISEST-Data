*Plain language summary available onlineSummaryWet wrap therapy (WWT) consists of topical steroids administered under a layerof wet cotton bandages or garments. Several trials with WWT have reportedpromising results in atopic dermatitis (AD). However, no systematic review andmeta-analysis on its efficacy and safety has been published. Our objective was toconduct a systematic review of the literature on WWT in AD to assess its efficacyand safety. We included randomized controlled trials among patients of all ageswith a diagnosis of AD based on predefined criteria or made by a dermatologist.Electronic searches were performed from 1970 to 30 March 2016 in theCochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials, MEDLINE, Embase and the WorldHealth Organization International Clinical Trials Registry. Selection of studies anddata extraction were performed independently by two researchers, and discrepan-cies were resolved by consensus. Six trials comparing WWT with topical steroidsin children or adults with AD were included. Sample sizes ranged from 19 to 51patients. Results on clinical severity and quality of life were reported incom-pletely and proved heterogeneous across studies. A nonsignificant tendency toincreased risk of mild skin infections was observed in those treated with WWT(pooled relative risk 635, 95% confidence interval 083-4855). The overallgrade of quality of evidence for the efficacy and safety outcomes was low. Inconclusion, the evidence that WWT is more effective than conventional treatmentwith topical steroids in AD is of low quality. Further clinical trials should estab-lish the efficacy of WWT in AD.What's already known about this topic?*Several clinical trials, as well as some qualitative reviews, have reported promisingresults for wet wrap therapy (WWT) in the short-term management of severeflares of atopic dermatitis (AD).
What does this study add?*This is the first systematic review on WWT for AD: it shows there is only low-quality evidence that this therapy is more effective than standard treatment withtopical steroids.Management of atopic dermatitis (AD) is based on the regularuse of emollients, together with topical steroids or calcineurininhibitors for acute flares. Phototherapy and immunosuppres-sive drugs are used in refractory cases.1,2In most patients, AD flares can be controlled with topicaltherapy; however, some cases are unresponsive. steroids is unclear, mainly due to their systemic adverse effectsand the rebound phenomenon following treatment cessation.2Wet wrap therapy (WWT) involves the application of atopical steroid, diluted or not, under two layers of cotton ban-dages or garments: a wet inner layer and a dry outer layer.Wraps are left for 8-24 h per day, and treatment durationshould not exceed 14 days.3,4 WWT is an interesting alterna-tive for short-term control in patients with severe or refractoryflares that avoids the use of systemic and potentially moretoxic treatments.3,4 Its efficacy has been claimed to derivefrom better penetration of the steroid, decrease in transepider-mal water loss, or from the wraps acting as a barrier againstscratching.3 Several studies and qualitative reviews on WWThave reported promising results in the management of AD,although the number of patients included has been small.5,6WWT has also been claimed to be safe, although its poten-tial adverse effects include systemic absorption of the steroid,bacterial skin infections and discomfort associated with theprocedure.3,7 However, no systematic review and meta-analy-sis on the efficacy and safety of WWT has been published.Therefore, the aim of this study was to systematically reviewthe literature to assess the efficacy and safety of WWT forpatients with AD.Materials and methods
Criteria for study selectionRandomized controlled clinical trials including patients of allages with a diagnosis of AD made according to predefined cri-teria or by a dermatologist were included. WWT, defined asthe application of a diluted or undiluted steroid under a layerof wet bandage or garment, should be compared against notreatment, placebo or other topical or systemic treatment forAD. The primary review outcome was clinical severity, andthe secondary outcomes were quality of life (QoL), mild localadverse events (folliculitis and infected eczema) and severeadverse events (eczema herpeticum, irreversible skin changessuch as atrophy or striae distensae and systemic steroideffects). For the timing of outcome assessment, we chose theavailable end point closest to 2 weeks. The reason for havingchosen this timing is that WWT is mainly recommended forthe short-term management of severe flares of AD.1,3Search strategyWe searched the following electronic databases from 1970 to30 March 2016: the Cochrane Central Register of ControlledTrials, MEDLINE through PubMed, Embase and the WorldHealth Organization International Clinical Trials Registry Plat-form ( No filters or lan-guage restrictions were used. Search strategies are reported inTable S1 (see Supporting Information).We also checked the reference lists of included studies forfurther references to relevant trials. Finally, we searched thewebsitewww.opengrey.eu/andestablishedcontactwith
researchers in the field to identify unpublished additionalstudies.Data collectionAfter removing duplicate records titles and abstracts werescreened to select the relevant reports. The full text of thesestudies was obtained and those meeting the elegibility criteriawere included in the review. For each study we extracted thefollowing information: study design, participants, interven-tions, outcome measures, results and risk of bias.Assessment of risk of bias in each study was performedwith the tool developed by the Cochrane Collaboration.8 Eachof the six domains in the tool was summarized as having low,unclear or high risk of bias. Quality of evidence on the effectof WWT on each clinical outcome was assessed using theGrading of Recommendations Assessment, Development andEvaluation (GRADE) system as high, moderate, low or verylow, considering within-study risk of bias, heterogeneity,directness of evidence, precision of effect estimates and risk ofpublication bias.8Selection of studies, data collection and grading of overallevidence were performed independently by two researchers(G.G.-L. and R.M.C.-R.), and discrepancies were resolved byconsensus.
Statistical analysisFor continuous outcomes the effect of WWT was expressed asstandardized mean difference (SMD) with its 95% confidenceinterval (CI). For dichotomous outcomes, the risk ratio (RR)and the corresponding 95% CI were used.Heterogeneity of results across studies was tested with theCochran Q test, and was quantified using the I2 statistic.9 Avalue of I2 > 50% was considered as substantial heterogeneity.Characteristics of the studies were assessed in an attempt tounderstand the sources of heterogeneity. A pooled estimate ofthe WWT effect for each outcome was calculated whenheterogeneity and clinical diversity across studies was deemedto be not too large. A random-effects model was chosen forI2 > 50%, and a fixed-effects model for I2 <= 50%.For missing data, we contacted the study authors for addi-tional information. In case of nonresponse, we imputed themissing data when possible: for continuous outcomes we usedthe last observation carried forward (LOCF) method, and fordichotomous outcomes, we assumed that participants withincomplete data did not suffer the event.Lastly, we performed sensitivity analyses, which consistedof excluding each study from the analysis, excluding studies athigh risk of bias, exploring the effect of the type of meta-ana-lysis used, assuming that missing data did suffer the outcomeevent and exploring the effect of the LOCF imputation. Thesmall number of studies included in the review precluded theuse of funnel plots to assess the risk of reporting bias.Statistical analyses were performed with Review Manager5.3 software.10Downloaded from  by Acquisitions Dept-Serials user on 17 July 2023Results

DiscussionOverall, three studies detected a significant improvement inclinical severity favouring WWT vs. conventional treatment,whereas the other three studies obtained nonsignificant results.Interestingly, one included study14 reported a slight, non-significant worsening in disease severity in the WWT group atweek 4, which could point to a decrease in efficacy after thefirst few weeks. As for QoL, Beattie and Lewis-Jones11 found asmall,nonsignificantdifferenceunfavourabletoWWT,whereas the other studies either did not address this outcomeor reported it insufficiently. Finally, the difference in mildlocal skin infections between the two groups was not statisti-cally significant.There were also some limitations in the type of informa-tion yielded by the reviewed studies. Firstly, duration oftreatment and steroid potency were highly variable in theincluded studies, though most used mild-potency steroids.Secondly, the results of this review apply only to the rela-tive efficacy of WWT vs. topical steroids as there were norandomized trials comparing WWT with other treatmentmodalities for AD. Thirdly, most of the studies includedchildren, and only one included adults:13 interestingly, thiswas one of the trials where the results were most favour-able to WWT. Fourthly, one of the trials was a left-rightstudy,13 which makes it impossible to evaluate outcomessuch as QoL and systemic side-effects. Fifthly, although thereviewed studies did not observe systemic or severe cuta-neous events, such as growth retardation or hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis suppression, such events were notactively investigated. Sixthly, the small sample sizes of thestudies allowed for detecting only frequent adverse out-comes. Finally, the short follow-up time, which was notlonger than 4 weeks in any of the trials, further limiteddetection of these events. Thus, the evidence from thesestudies cannot be used to draw definitive conclusions in thisregard.ScaleStudyDefinitionRangeValidated

According to the GRADE system, overall quality of the evi-dence was low for all the outcomes considered for this review(Table 3). The results of Beattie and Lewis-Jones11 and Hind-ley et al.14 might have a high risk of attrition bias; the remain-ing studies have unclear bias in several items of the Cochranetool. In addition, the small sample sizes in the trials led toimprecise results with wide CIs, which further limits the qual-ity of the evidence.We found high heterogeneity among studies in the resultsfor clinical severity, which precluded calculating a pooled esti-mate of the effect of WWT. Heterogeneity could be partly dueto the diversity of study participants, treatment regimens andscales used.With respect to potential biases in the review process, giventhat bibliographic searches were performed in several data-bases and that grey literature was also searched, it is unlikelythat we missed relevant studies.5,6,21,22 However, the smallnumber of included studies made the funnel plots meaning-less, and no conclusions on publication bias could be drawn.Finally, the imputation methods used can be another sourceof bias. Changing the imputation assumptions in the meta-analysis of local adverse events had a relevant influence on thepooled effect, but this issue has been dealt with by includingboth scenarios in this article.Comparing this with other studies, our results are consistentwith those of other reviews.5,6,21 However, previous reviewsdid not attempt a quantitative synthesis of data and alsoincluded nonrandomized studies and case series. Perhapsbecause of this, one review6 concluded that WWT is effectivefor severe cases of AD but acknowledged that the level of evi-dence was weak.Some studies describe infectious events such as herpes sim-plex infection or upper respiratory tract infection, which werenot reported in the studies included in this review.23 In con-trast, another study24 found no cases of local infection in 217children treated with wet wraps, although the mean durationof treatment was 36 days. It is likely that a short durationimplies a lower infection risk, which is consistent with thefindings by Foelster-Holst et al.13 Other studies have found asuppression of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis insome patients23,25 but they did not further investigate its clini-cal consequences.Looking at the implications for practice, our review foundthe evidence that WWT has a greater efficacy than topical ster-oids, for the treatment of AD is of low quality. The effect oftreatment on QoL was also inconclusive.WWT may lead to a higher risk of mild local infections(folliculitis and infected eczema) than topical steroids, butagain, this could not be proven. No cases of severe cutaneousor systemic events were found.With respect to implications for research, we believe thatmethodologically rigorous and adequately reported random-ized trials with a higher number of participants are needed toestablish the efficacy of WWT vs. topical steroid treatment inAD.
