SYSTEMATIC REVIEWPrimary Localized Cutaneous Amyloidosis: A SystematicTreatment ReviewTill Weidner1* Tanja Illing1 * Peter Elsner1 Springer International Publishing Switzerland 2017AbstractBackground Primarylocalizedcutaneousamyloidosis(PLCA) is characterized by extracellular deposition ofheterogenic amyloid proteins in the skin without systemicinvolvement. Lichen amyloidosis, macular amyloidosis,and (primary localized cutaneous) nodular amyloidosis aredifferent subtypes of PLCA.Objective The aim of this study was to review the currentreported treatment options for PLCA.Methods This systematic review was based on a search inthe PubMed database for English and German articles from1985 to 2016.Results Reports on the treatment of PLCA were limitedpredominantly to case reports or small case series. Therewere a few clinical trials but these lacked control groups. Avariety of treatment options for PLCA were reportedincluding retinoids, corticosteroids, cyclophosphamide,cyclosporine,amitriptyline,colchicine,cepharanthin,tacrolimus, dimethyl sulfoxide, vitamin D3 analogs, cap-saicin, menthol, hydrocolloid dressings, surgical modali-ties, laser treatment, and phototherapy.Conclusion No definitive recommendation of preferabletreatment procedures can be made based on the analyzedliterature. Randomized controlled trials are needed to offerpatients an evidence-based therapy with high-qualitystandardized treatment regimens for PLCA.Key PointsTherapy of primary localized cutaneous amyloidosisis a challenge for dermatologists.Many treatment options exist and no gold standardhas been established.
For lichen amyloidosis and macular amyloidosis, themost important issue is the resolution of anyunderlying disorder that may lead to chronicexcoriation of the skin.1 IntroductionPrimary localized cutaneous amyloidosis (PLCA) is char-acterized by extracellular deposition of heterogenic amy-loid proteins in the skin without systemic involvement.There are different subtypes of PLCA, namely: lichenamyloidosis (LA), macular amyloidosis (MA), and (pri-mary localized cutaneous) nodular amyloidosis (NA) [1].Additionally, the combined appearance of LA and MA isdescribed as mixed (biphasic) amyloidosis (BA) [2].Lichen amyloidosis, the most common form of PLCA,presents with multiple localized or rarely generalized,hyper-pigmented grouped papules with a predilection forthe shins, calves, ankles, and dorsa of the feet and thighs(Fig. 1). It is mostly associated with severe pruritus, and itis, together with MA, a keratin-derived amyloidosis, inwhich cytokeratins serve as amyloid precursors, affectingonly the dermis [3, 4]. Histological findings are groupedcolloid bodies, which correspond to apoptotic basal ker-atinocytes [5]. Macular amyloidosis is characterized by& Till 1Department of Dermatology, University Hospital Jena,Erfurter Strasse 35, Jena 07743, Germany
moderately pruritic macules of dark brown pigmentationwith a rippled surface [6]. Pruritus does not occur in everypatient and can be missing in up to 20% of LA cases [7].Nodular amyloidosis is typically indicated by solitary ormultiple tumors. In this condition, the amyloid originatesfrom immunoglobulin light chains and is associated withdermal plasma cell infiltration, which has the potential toinfiltrate the subcutaneous tissue and blood vessels andmay progress to systemic disease, estimated to occur in atleast 7% of cases [8, 9]. It has been observed that PLCA isrelatively common among Southern Chinese and SouthAmerican populations [10-12]. The etiopathology has yetto be clarified. It has been proposed that signaling abnor-malities via the oncostatin M-type II receptor and/or theinterleukin (IL)-31 receptor leads to keratinocyte apoptosiswith subsequent amyloid deposition, a reduced number ofcutaneous nerves, and pruritus [13, 14]. Moreover, a pointmutation in the IL-31 receptor A gene in a family withhereditary PLCA was found by Lin et al. [15].A wide array of medical conditions have been reportedcoincidentally with PLCA. Repeated skin friction may beassociated with LA and MA [16-19]; to date, the causalfactors are still unknown [20]. Multiple endocrine neo-plasia type 2A is frequently associated with PLCA (espe-cially LA), typically presenting in the interscapular region,and thus leading to the supposition that the amyloiddeposition is a secondary phenomenon when a geneticdisposition could not be observed [21].A systemic involvement of amyloidosis should beexcluded by a laboratory work-up. While many topical,systemic, and surgical treatment modalities for PLCA havebeen described, only a few minor clinical trials have beenperformed to date. The lack of treatment standards forPLCA and the unsuccessful therapies are underlined by theabsence of European and national guidelines. The central
aim of this review is to outline and summarize the reportedtreatment options for PLCA.2 Materials and MethodsAccording to the Preferred Reporting Items for SystematicReviews guidelines, we performed a systematic reviewusing the MEDLINE (PubMed) database with the follow-ing research criteria: a: ['cutaneous (localized OR loca-lised) AND amyloidosis AND (treatment OR therapy)'].Our search was limited to human-subject reports publishedbetween 1 January, 1985 and 24 July, 2016. A total of 62articles was identified from this search, and a further 34articles from a manual review of reference lists wereincluded. Six of these were excluded because they werereviews and 20 publications were ruled out because ofabsent or imprecisely described treatment modalities orclinical outcomes. Seventy articles were deemed appro-priate for inclusion in this review (Fig. 2). For each pub-lication included, we recorded the author, study year, studydesign, localization, number of treated patients, therapies,and outcomes (Table 1).3 Results
Throughout the medical specialist literature, reports onPLCA treatment are limited to case reports, retrospectivecase series, and minor studies of patients treated without acontrol group. A wide range of treatment options arereported, encompassing topical and systemic agents, pho-totherapy, laser therapy, and surgical interventions.3.1 Topical and Systemic RetinoidsRetinoids have been used for disorders of keratinizationbecause of their advantageous effects on epidermal pro-liferation, desquamation, and inflammation of the skin.They are known to induce apoptosis and to stimulatemacrophages, resulting in the removal of redundantextracellular material [22]. Retinoids can be applied astopical and systemic agents. Systemic retinoid treatmentwas reported for LA and BA only, and it has been used inten case series and case reports. In LA, positive clinicalresults have been demonstrated, referring to decreasedpruritus and flattening of the papules [23-30], except in asingle case report on the use of etretinate [31]. The authorsdescribed typical side effects of retinoids, namely dry lipsand eyes, generalized xerosis, burning sensations, andelevated serum cholesterol. Additionally, etretinate ele-vated liver enzymes. Treatment with oral acitretin led tosatisfactory results, but to residual hyperpigmentation inBA [32]. Topical application of tretinoin tocoferil, a syn-thetic esterified compound of tocopherol and retinoic acid,was reported to result in mixed outcomes varying between'very good and no response' in a prospective clinical trialincluding ten patients with LA and MA [33]. Tazarotene ina single case report of MA was without positive clinicaleffects [34]; in a case report on the use of tretinoin, littlerelief of pruritus occurred [35].
3.2 Topical Vitamin D3 AnalogsVitamin D3 analogs are known to modulate keratinocytedifferentiation and proliferation as well as inflammation[36]. A single double-blind, right-left comparison study on16 patients with LA showed beneficial effects of topicalcalcipotriol, but there was no significant difference tobetamethasone 17-valerate. Three patients reported a milddegree of redness and scaling while treated with cal-cipotriol [37].3.3 Immunosuppressive Agents3.3.1 CyclophosphamideAn excellent outcome has been found with oral application ofthe immunosuppressive alkylating drug cyclophosphamide inpulse treatment combined with systemic corticosteroids in LA[38] and NA [39]. A clinical trial with 36 patients usingcyclophosphamideshowedagoodresponsetoitching,thesizeof lesions, and hyperpigmentation in PLCA [40]. Observedside effects were diffuse alopecia in 11% of patients.3.3.2 CorticosteroidsTopical corticosteroids are the gold standard treatment formany inflammatory skin diseases owing to their anti-in-flammatory effect via interaction with the intracellularglucocorticoid receptor and the induction of specific genesencoding signaling proteins with an immunosuppressiveand anti-proliferative efficacy spectrum. Corticosteroidswere used as a regimen with no benefit in MA [17] and incomparison studies with contrary results, as mentioned inSects. 3.2 [37] and 3.12 [41].
3.3.3 Cyclosporine and TacrolimusAs a result of the inhibition of calcineurin phosphatase andthe subsequently reduced IL-2 production, cyclosporineand tacrolimus might work in PLCA. 3.6 Dimethyl SulfoxideDimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) has penetration-enhancingproperties and acts as an anti-inflammatory pharmaceu-tical agent [50]. Beneficial topical application has beendescribed in two case reports of LA. A histologicaldisappearance of amyloid deposition could not be shown[51, 52]. No response to topical DMSO has beenobserved in a further case report of extensive LA [53].One trial reported on topical DMSO in patients with LA,MA, and BA (n = 13) with positive outcomes, referringto the disappearance of pruritus, flattening of papules,and remission of hyperpigmentation in 50% of thepatients [54]. A major study compared 50 and 100%solution of DMSO (topical) with unsatisfactory clinicalresults for each concentration with a relapse and nochanges in amyloid deposits in all patients [55]. The sideeffects were contact urticaria, desquamation of the skin,and a burning sensation.3.7 CapsaicinCapsaicin binds to the transient receptor, potential ionchannel vanilloid-1 on cutaneous histamine-sensitive andmechanosensitive C and Ad fibers. Antiprurigenic effectsare achieved by overstimulation of these fibers and arefractory mode setting [56]. In a case series of twopatients with LA, impressive results (reduction of thepruritus and improved clinically and histologically skinlesions) were demonstrated by applying a high dose ofcapsaicin (8% patch) with the suspicion of destroying thosespecial fibers [57]. The authors describe an initial burningsensation and a self-limited erythema as side effects. Onepatient with MA is mentioned, who received capsaicincream (0.025%) without achieving a beneficial outcome[35].
3.8 MentholMenthol triggers the cold-sensitive, transient receptor,potential cation channel subfamily M member 8 in the skin,leading to cooling sensations. Moreover, there are reason-able grounds for suspecting that menthol reduces pruritusby the selective activation of j-opioid receptors [58]. Anexcellent outcome (flattening of papules and almost com-plete absence of pruritus) could be seen in one case reportusing topical menthol in LA [59].3.9 Hydrocolloid DressingsA case report using hydrocolloid dressings (HDs) in apatient with LA described an improvement in pruritus and'cosmetic appearance'. The mechanism may be explainedas followed: first, HDs may act through a mechanicalprevention of scratching [60]. Second, the amyloid depositsin LA originate from the epidermis layer [61]. Moreover,HD application in patients with psoriasis could positivelyaffect the normalization of the epidermis [62]. The authorsof this case report recommend HDs as first-line therapy inLA.3.10 AmitriptylineA case series of two patients with familial LA using oralamitriptyline showed an impressive reduction in prurituswithoutanychangesoftheskinlesions[63].Amitriptyline is a tricyclic antidepressant, indicated forneuropathic itching [64]. A small-fiber neuropathy playsa major role in the pathogenesis of itching in PLCA,thus amitriptyline may be an effective treatment optionfor familial LA [13].Primary Localized Cutaneous Amyloidosis3.11 Laser Treatments
Laser therapy has been an efficient treatment modality inmany medical publications of PLCA. Side effects includedburning sensations, transient erythema, and edema. There wasone review by Al Yahya [65] of laser treatments for PLCA.3.11.1 Carbon Dioxide LaserThe carbon dioxide laser was the most frequently usedlaser treatment for PLCA. There was one case reportshowing good clinical outcomes on the flattening of thepapules and an improvement in itching in LA [66]. Arandomized single-blind clinical trial, which included 16patients with MA and nine patients with LA, revealed asignificant reduction in pigmentation, thickness of thelesions, itching, and amyloid deposits in repeated biopsiesafter treatment with the carbon dioxide laser [67].The carbon dioxide laser induced clinical improvementof all treated areas in NA as reported in one case report;and cosmetically acceptable results with the recurrence ofskin lesions after 9 months in another reported case of NA.[68] The authors report problems with hemostasis thatcorrelated with amyloid infiltration of the dermis and bloodvessels. Atrophic scars occurred post-treatment [69].The supportive use of surgical excision followed bydermabrasion and carbon dioxide laser [70], and curettagefollowed by carbon dioxide laser [71] has been described intwo case reports of NA, resulting in effective results and norecurrence of lesions.3.11.2 Ytterbium/Erbium Laser
Ten patients with LA underwent ytterbium/erbium lasertreatment in one prospective clinical trial. They revealed animprovement in itching and demonstrated a shrinkage ofamyloid deposition in repeated biopsies [72]. The authorsalso mentioned a burning sensation.3.11.3 Pulsed Dye LaserThe applicationof a pulsed dye laser has been recognized as abeneficial treatment regimen in all variants of PLCA. In MA,improvement and lightening of the brown macules could beobserved in one single case report [73]. Reduced itching anda flattening of the LA papules was shown in another casereport 15 months post-treatment [74]. Lesion color, size, andpliability was improved in one patient with NA [75].3.11.4 Erbium:Yttrium Aluminum Garnet LaserA single case report of LA revealed 95% clearance of thelesions after a 6-month follow-up [76].3.11.5 Neodymium-doped:Yttrium Aluminum Garnet LaserAn effective reduction in the degree of pigmentation wasobserved using the Q-switch neodymium-doped:yttriumaluminum garnet laser in 20 patients with MA. The authorsdetermined that treatment with 532 nm was more efficientthan 1064 nm [77]. Very good results without clearance ofLA skin lesions have been described in another case report.A cellulitis occurred in one patient after laser therapy [78].3.12 Phototherapy
Different phototherapeutic modalities, especially for chronicinflammatory skin diseases, are available and offer a safe andcontrolled treatment option but require a continuous therapyover a long period. Narrow-band UVB irradiation led to com-plete resolution of LA lesions in one case report [79]; completeresolution of a generalized LA lesion in another patient treatedwith topical corticosteroids and oral olopatadine [80]; a markedimprovement of itching and clearing of the amyloid deposits ina repeated biopsy in a patient with generalized LA [81];remission of pruritus, decreased hyperpigmentation, and flat-tening of the papules in patient with BA who received topicaltacrolimus additively [82]; and successful management ofpruritus in a patient with MA receiving UVB therapy [83].In patients with PLCA, topical psoralen with ultraviolet A(PUVA) was used in all documented cases. The additionalintake of acitretin in one case series of two patients with LAtreated with bath PUVA, revealed an efficient reduction inpruritus and an impressive resolution of the papules [84]. Adisappointing clinical outcome was described in a case seriesof three patients with LA who applied bath PUVA. Theauthors report modest effects and a persistence of the skinlesions in more than 50% [85]. There is a small, prospectiveleft-right comparative study of LA, which included ninepatients treated with UVB, five treated with PUVA, and 11treated with topical corticosteroids. Ultraviolet B treatmentwas more advantageous with regard to decreased skinroughness compared with UVB irradiation. However,PUVA therapy showed a better improvement in itchingcompared with PUVA treatment. Topical corticosteroidswere inferior to the phototherapeutic treatments, which wereapplied inversely. Reported side effects include hyperpig-mentation after PUVA therapy and increased itching in onepatient after UVB irradiation [41]. Clearance of lichenifi-cation, resolution of itching, and reduced hyperpigmentationwere described using PUVA therapy and discontinuingseveral dietary supplements in generalized LA [86].
3.13 Transcutaneous Nerve StimulationThe exact mode of antipruritic action of transcutaneousnerve stimulation is not fully understood. One putativeT. Weidner et al.mechanism is a peripheral nociceptive influence of anelectrical current on itching and pain fibers [87]. Relief ofpruritus in eight patients with MA receiving transcutaneousnerve stimulation therapy was described in one singlepublished trial [88].
3.14 Surgical InterventionsSurgical excision was performed in several case reports inNA [89-94], leading to complete clearance withoutrecurrence of skin lesions. An atrophic scar remained inone report [91]. Surgical interventions in LA were reportedas electrodesiccation [95], a special technique 'scrapingwith the scalpel' [96], and dermabrasion [97]. Both casereports lead to complete clearance of LA lesions withoutrecurrence [95, 97]. The patient treated with dermabrasionexperienced delayed wound healing, superficial scarring,hypopigmentation, and a transient edema. A case series ofnine patients with LA developed a new technique: 'scrap-ing with the scalpel', resulting in a disappearance of pru-ritus and heterogeneous effects on clinical presentation(44.5% with 'good results', 55.5% with 'acceptable re-sults'). Persistent pigment changes were side effects of thisapproach.One case report of PLCA of the eyelid treated by sur-gery exists. The tumor could not be further classified andrecurred 6 months post-treatment [98]. A case of primarylocalized cutaneous amyloid elastosis healed by surgicalexcision and local radiotherapy subsequently showed norecurrence after a 1-year follow-up [99]. In a singlereported case of LA associated with thyroid papillary car-cinoma, skin lesions improved markedly after the under-lying tumor was removed [100]. Curettage led to completehealing in two cases of NA [101, 102].As mentioned above (Sect. 3.11.1), the supportive use ofsurgical excision followed by dermabrasion and carbondioxide laser [70] and curettage followed by carbon diox-ide laser [71] has been described in two case reports of NA,resulting in effective results and preventing the recurrenceof lesions.3.15 CryotherapyOne approach using cryotherapy in NA was described as'unhelpful' and was complicated by a minor hemorrhage[94].
4 DiscussionTreatment of PLCA represents a complex challenge forphysicians, as no guidelines exist regarding an evidence-based therapy regimen. Based on this current review, weconclude there are a large variety of therapies available forPLCA subtypes.4.1 Lichen Amyloidosis, Macular Amyloidosis,and Mixed Amyloidosis
The treatment of LA, MA, and BA should refer to the strikingsymptoms and the patient's needs. For LA and MA, the mostimportant issue is the resolution of any underlying disorderthat may lead to chronic excoriation of the skin. We deter-mined there were different modalities acting on pruritus andother treatment regimens affecting skin roughness, hyper-pigmentation, and itching. Minor trials revealed beneficialoutcomes for all symptoms using topical calcipotriol, pho-totherapy, oral cyclophosphamide, and laser treatments.Topical DMSO was used in many patients but led to severalside effects. Neodymium-doped:yttrium aluminum garnetlaser treatment resulted in hyperpigmentation responses in aclinical trial of MA [76]. All other treatment regimens arebased on case reports and small case series. Physicians mustcompare side effects, opportunities, and patient's psycho-logical strain to determine an appropriate therapy from thelisted options. Furthermore, the size and localization of theskin lesions should influence the choice of treatment.4.2 Primary Localized Cutaneous NodularAmyloidosisNodular amyloidosis, the rarest form of PCLA, was pre-dominantly excised by surgical interventions followingcarbon dioxide laser application in most cases. There is onesingle published case by Tong et al. with underlyingCREST and Sjo"gren syndrome treated successfully withsystemically cyclophosphamide, which is used in thetreatment of systemic amyloidosis [103]. Surgical removalshould be the goal because of the potential infiltration insubcutaneous tissue and blood vessels and progress tosystemic disease [8, 9]. This is underlined by Vestey et al.,who reported a hemorrhage after cryotherapy of a NAlesion. Therefore, physicians have to consider bleedingcomplications and use preventative techniques such ascauterization or a carbon dioxide laser, and also considerthe putative persistence of amyloids in the dermis that canlead to recurrences.5 Conclusions
No definitive recommendation on preferable treatmentprocedures for PLCA can be based on the present literature.Patients should be well informed that offered treatmentmodalities are not based on clinical studies, but on casereports and expert judgment. Randomized controlled trialsPrimary Localized Cutaneous Amyloidosisare needed to offer patients an evidence-based therapy withhigh-quality standardized treatment regimens for PLCA.Because of the rarity of the condition, this will probablyrequire multicenter and possibly even internationallycoordinated studies.
