The effect size of each study was calculated by the standardized mean difference (SMD) with 95%confidence interval (CI) or quartiles.Results: A total of eight studies including 475 cases and 265 controls were finally included in thismeta-analysis. We found significant between-study heterogeneity and conducted analyses using ran-dom-effects models. No significant association was found between the serum levels of Hcy and SSc(pooled SMD  1/41.382 lmol/L, 95%CI  1/4 0.442 to 3.206, p  1/4 .137), but there are two outlier studies thatdeviate significantly from most other studies, which made it difficult to generalize these results. Afterexcluding these two studies, six studies were included in the meta-analysis. The results showed thatthe serum levels of Hcy in SSc were significantly higher than that in healthy controls (pooledSMD  1/4 1.182lmol/L, 95%CI  1/4 0.230-2.134, p  1/4 .015).Conclusion: Serum/plasma levels of Hcy in SSc diseases were higher than that in healthy controls.

IntroductionSclerosis (SSc) is an autoimmune disorder of the connectivetissue, characterized by microvascular damage with associ-ated cutaneous and internal organ fibrosis [1]. Although theclinical expression, severity and progression are rather het-erogeneous and it is not clear which of these abnormalitiesis first expressed, recent studies have known that vasculardysfunction and injury may play a primary role in thepathogenesis of SSc [2]. Moreover, there are several factorsthat have also been considered to be the possible triggeringagents of vascular injury, such as anti-endothelial antibodies,mechanisms of molecular mimicry, microchimerism phe-nomenon and oxidative stress [3].Homocysteine (Hcy) is a nonessential sulfur-containingamino acid mainly derived from the diet of methionine,which occurs naturally in the blood plasma. Its chemicalproperties showed a similarity to cysteine, hence the namehomocysteine. In the human body, about 1/2 of Hcy andmethyltetrahydrofolate turn into methionine and tetrahydro-folic acid under the action of methylenetetrahydrofolatereductase (MTHFR), and this process needs the help of folicacid and vitamin B12 [4]. The rest of the Hcy turn into cyst-eine under the catalysis of cystathionine b-synthase (CBS),which require the participation of vitamin B6 (Figure 1).
When any factors cause the impair of Hcy metabolism ordefects in crucial co-factors that participate in its recycling,it will develop into hyperhomocysteinemia (HHey). Therehave been a lot of findings shown that HHey was recog-nized as a modifiable independent risk factor for coronary,cerebral and peripheral vascular diseases [5-8], and experi-mental studies have also suggested that Hcy induces vascu-lar endothelium injury and leads to vascular damage [3].These findings suggest that Hcy might be involved in thepathogeneses of both central and peripheral vascular disor-ders. Recently, many studies have also revealed that mul-tiple rheumatic diseases may be associated with HHey[9-11], and the possible role of HHey in the complexdamage mechanisms of systemic rheumatic diseases hasalso the reason for concern.In recent years, various similar studies about the associ-ation between SSc and Hcy have also been performed(Table 1), but there is controversy between these reports. Asshown earlier, several studies have reported that plasma Hcylevels are elevated in SSc patients compared to healthy con-trols [12-15], but some studies do not support this finding[16-18]. Among them, one study has reported there is nosignificant difference in Hcy levels between SSc patients andcontrols, and in another two study performed by NazariniaCONTACT Jing of Epidemiology and Biostatistics, School of Public Health, Anhui Medical University, MeishanRoad 81, Hefei, Anhui 230032, PR ChinaThese authors contributed equally to this work and should be considered co-first authors.

These might be explained by small sam-ple sizes or the heterogeneity of the study design, and themethods used to detect plasma Hcy level as well as someinherent biases. Therefore, the aim of the present study wasto summarize the available evidence to investigate whetherserum/plasma levels of Hcy were associated with SSc usingmeta-analysis, and summarize recent literature data regard-ing the reasons for the increased Hcy and the supposedmechanisms involved in SSc.2. Studies meeting the followingcriteria were considered to be included in this meta-analysis:(a) case-control study, clinical cohort or cross-sectionalstudy design; (b) human subjects with SSc; (c) detailed dataabout plasma/serum level of Hcy in both patients andhealthy controls were accessible. Data extractionAll the data were extracted by two independent reviewersaccording to the inclusion criteria. The extracted informa-tion for each eligible papers included the first author'sname, publication year, study subjects (cases and controls),assay methods of serum Hcy levels measured, mean Hcy lev-els (+-standard deviation) or median (95%CI/quartiles).2.3. Statistical analysis
Stata version 11.0 (StataCorp, College Station, TX) was usedfor statistical analysis. The effect size of each study was cal-culated by the standardized mean difference (SMD). SMDand 95%CIs were described by a forest plot. In most studies,the mean and deviation were obtained, but in several stud-ies, only the median and 95%CI or quartiles were reported.Therefore, when the original data were median and quar-tiles, we transformed and calculated the data to gain theappropriate values [19]. I2 statistics and Q-test were used toassess the degree of heterogeneity between studies. If p < .05for the Q2 test or I2 > 50%, a random-effects model wasselected to pool the data. Otherwise, a fixed-effect modelwas used. Begg's test and Egger's test were conducted toevaluate potential publication bias.3. Results3.1. Hcy concentration in SSCWe firstly retrieved 31 relevant references from PubMed,ElsevierScienceDirectandCochraneLibrarydatabases.However, after carefully reading the title and abstract, ninearticles [12-18,20,21] with abstracts in total were selected inthis systematic review (Table 1). However, in one study [16],only the median was reported. Finally, only eight eligiblestudies including 475 cases and 265 controls were mergedby us. We found significant between-study heterogeneityand conducted analyses using random-effects models. Nosignificant association was found between the serum levelsofHcyandSSc(pooledSMD  1/41.382lmol/L,95%CI  1/4 0.442 to 3.206, p  1/4.137; I2  1/4 98.7%, p  1/4 .000), butthere are two outlier studies [12,18] that deviate significantlyfrom most other studies, which made it difficult to general-ize these results. Therefore, we excluded two studies in sen-sitivity analysis. After excluding these two studies, sixstudies were included in the meta-analysis. The resultsshowed that the serum levels of Hcy in SSc were higherthan that in healthy controls (pooled SMD  1/4 1.182lmol/L,
95%CI  1/4 0.230-2.134,p  1/4 .015;I2  1/4 94.4%,p  1/4 .000](Figure 1). There was no publication bias performingEgger's regression test (t  1/4 1.20, p  1/4 .298) and Begg's test(p  1/4 .707) in the six studies (with two outlier studiesexcluded).In conclusion, this is the first preliminary meta-analysisto assess the relationship of Hcy serum levels with SSc.However, several limitations should be acknowledged. First,significant heterogeneity was observed in this meta-analysis.The heterogeneity of the population probably originates inthe differences in the characteristics of patients, such as age,gender, region, study type and other residual confoundingeffect. Indeed, the different measurement used to define Hcyplasma level may also play a role in high heterogeneity.Moreover, many factors have an effect on Hcy levels, suchas blood folate and vitamin B12 levels and polymorphismsof genes involved in the metabolism of Hcy. Unfortunately,due to the insufficient information of these factors, a com-prehensive meta-regression or subgroup analysis was notsuccessfully conducted. Thus, the results of meta-analysisshould be interpreted with caution. Therefore, it needs to bedemonstrated by well-designed prospective studies whetherHcy was higher in SSc.3.2. Hcy and clinical features of SSC
As we have shown, many studies have revealed that Hcyplasma level in SSc patients was significantly higher thanthat in normal controls. And previous studies have alreadyshown that high level of Hcy might be a risk factor for cor-onary artery disease, stroke and arterial thrombosis in sys-temiclupuserythematosus(SLE)[22].Therefore,thecorrelation between Hcy level and clinical features of SSchas also the reason for concern in recent years (Table 1).Microvasculopathy is an important pathological changesin SSc. Nailfold videocapillaroscopy (NVC) is a useful toolfor the assessment of SSc microvasculopathy. Based onNVC, the microvascular alterations were classified into'early', 'active' or 'late' pattern [23]. In the study performedby Caramaschi et al., they not only found the plasma Hcylevel in SSc was higher than that in controls, but alsoshowed that there was a significant correlation betweenplasma Hcy concentration and the pattern of NVC with aprogressive increase from early to active and above all tolate pattern [12]. And in their later study, they further eval-uated the association of NVC patterns with both demo-graphic and clinical features in a large group of sclerodermapatients, and they still found the Hcy plasma levels wereincreased across NVC patterns, from early to late pattern inSSc patients [13]. On the other hand, as the patients withSSc typically reveal RP and persistent digital ischemia andoccasionally develop digital ulcers (DU), the correlationbetween Hcy and RP and DU has also already been investi-gated. For example, both Caramaschi et al. [12] andMarasini et al. [21] have shown a higher Hcy level in SScpatients with secondary RP than healthy controls. Inconclusion, these above findings suggest that Hcy plasmalevel may also be related to microvascular involvement andendothelial damage in SSc patients.In addition to microvascular involvement in SSc, HHeymay also play some roles in the pathogenesis of macrovascu-lar damage in SSc. Reasons for the increased Hcy in SScHcy's metabolic process is affected by many nutrients (folateand vitamin B12) and various metabolic enzymes (MTHFRand CBS), so HHey is caused either by genetic defects ofenzymes involved in Hcy metabolism or by reduced vitaminintake. For example, a previous study suggested that both ofserum levels of folate and vitamin B12 were lower in SScpatients than that in controls [24]. This showed that their
deficiencies may lead to elevated plasma Hcy level in SSc.However, in addition to dietary vitamin deficiency, geneticdefects of enzymes involved in Hcy metabolism may alsolead to the rise of plasma Hcy level [16]. Szamosi et al. [16]have found that the frequent mutation (C677T) in the geneencoding for MTHFR may lead to moderate HHey andinfluence the incidence of macrovascular abnormalities inSSc patients. Beyond that, CBS deficiency may also lead tosevere HHey. We all know that homocystinuria is a raregenetic disorder, and it is mainly characterized by HHeythat is caused by mutations in CBS, and leads to early-onsetatherosclerosis and arterial and venous thromboses [25], andthe main treatment strategy to lower cellular and plasmaHcy levels includes vitamin B6 intake, dietary methioninerestriction, betaine supplementation, folate and vitamin B12administration. At the same time, some new strategies totreat homocystinuria have also been made in recent years,and these mainly include functional restoration to mutantCBS, enhanced clearance of Hcy from the body and so on.These suggest that again HHey is mainly provoked by nutri-tional deficiency and genetic defects of enzymes involved inHcy metabolism (Figure 2).However, in addition to the above reasons, other factorsmay also affect Hcy concentrations, including habitualsmoking, coffee intake, some medications, alcohol consump-tion and physical activity. For instance, a previous study hasrevealed that the use of diuretics in the SLE group withHHey was significantly higher than that in normohomocys-teinemia group (39% vs. 5%; OR  1/4 12.7 [95%CI  1/4 3.3-46.0],p < .001) [23]. Moreover, most of the authors have alsofound that antihypertensive drugs can significantly increaseHcy levels [26,27]. On the other hand, in exploring the rela-tionship between HHcy and lifestyle factors, Chen et al. [28]Downloaded by [University of Florida] at 00:09 20 October 2017

And in both genders, currentsmoking status and dietary habits were significantly associ-ated with HHey.In conclusion, a variety of factors have been proved tocause elevated plasma Hcy level. Thus, clinicians should takesome corresponding measures to lower patients' plasma Hcylevels, and patients with HHey should also actively adjusttheir unreasonable diet and lifestyle especially for SScpatients.5. Possible mechanisms of Hcy in vasculardysfunction of SScAs shown earlier, high Hcy concentrations have beenobserved in SSc patients in numerous studies, and high-Hcylevels have also been showed to associate with clinical fea-tures of SSc. Currently, many studies have focused on themechanism of Hcy in vascular dysfunction. As we know,HHey may cause vascular damage by several mechanisms.Firstly, Hcy induces oxidative stress condition. Hcy mayfavor an oxidant status by generating superoxide radicals.Moreover, Hcy also reported to disrupt uncoupling of nitricoxide (NO) synthase activity, as well as quenching of NOand inhibiting enzyme [3]. Secondly, Hcy induces inflamma-tory or prothrombotic conditions. Moreover, Hcy can dir-ectly induce toxic into endothelium and may influence its
physiologic thromboresistance by enhancing platelet activa-tion and enhancing coagulation [29,30]. Finally, it was dem-onstrated that Hcy alters NO release by cultured endothelialcells and increases collagen production by cultured rabbitsmooth muscle cells [31,32] (Figure 3).Based on the role of Hcy in endothelium dysfunction, thepossible Hcy -scleroderma interaction can be explored.Firstly, vascular tone control is abnormal in scleroderma,and vasodilatation and vasoconstriction are imbalance. Aswe noted above, HHey induces vascular dysfunction throughthe generation of reactive oxygen species. Moreover, it alsoleads to the reduction of NO and inactivate NO, which is apowerful regulator of endothelium-dependent vasodilatation[33]. Therefore, it may partially explain the diminishedendothelial-dependentvasodilatationinsclerodermabyreduction of NO production in endothelial cells. Secondly,many studies have shown that the oxidative stress mightplay a role in inflammation and fibrosis in heart or liver byusing animal models of homocysteinemia [34,35]. At thesame time, Raaf et al. [36] have shown that TGF-b levelswere elevated in hyperhomocysteinemic rats. These resultssuggested higher Hcy level is possibly associated with skinsclerosis in patients with SSc. Thirdly, oxygen-free radicalsmay generate tissue ischemia and subsequently mediate theendothelial injury. Previous studies have suggested thatreactive oxygen species may fragment autoantigens, support-ing the generation of the typical autoantibodies found inpatients with SSc [37]. Finally, it has beenreported that oxidized lipoproteins and the susceptibility tooxidation of low-density lipoproteins were increased inscleroderma [38]. Hcy may have an effect on oxidized lipo-proteins and the oxidation of low-density lipoproteins.Therefore, Hcy may play an important role in both macro-vasculature and microvasculature involvement in SSc.6. Possible mechanisms of Hcy in fibrosis of SSc
Fibrosis is the formation of excessive extracellular matrix(ECM) deposition in an organ or tissue during reparative orreactive process. Previous studies have also revealed that ele-vated plasma levels of Hcy are closely associated with mul-tiplefibroticdiseasesincludingcardiacremodeling,glomerulosclerosis, liver fibrosis and pulmonary fibrosis[39,40]. Skin and organ fibrosis is an important pathologicalfeatures in SSc, and the epidemiology study has shown thatplasma Hcy levels are positively correlated with modifiedRodnan total skin score [15]. These suggest that Hcy may beassociated with skin fibrosis of SSc. Next, we will focuson the possible mechanisms of Hcy in fibrosis of SSc(Figure 4).During the process of ECM degradation, matrix metallo-proteinases (MMPs) and tissue inhibitors of metalloprotei-nases (TIMPs) play a pivotal role in this process. MMPs andTIMPs are central to ECM turnover and actively regulateinflammation and remodeling [41]. Hcy seems to regulatethe expression and synthesis of tissue inhibitor of metallo-proteinases-1 (TIMP-1), and influence MMP/TIMP balance.In liver fibrosis, Torres et al. [42] have found that Hcy isable to induce the expression and synthesis of the TIMP-1in many different cell types. At the same time, they alsofound that Hcy could induce the expression of a1(I) procol-lagen mRNA and promote collagen synthesis in vascularsmooth muscle cells [42]. These studies suggest that Hcymay alter extracellular matrix homeostasis. Moreover, inanother rat model of intestinal fibrosis, it has been foundthat the protein expression of TGF-b1, MMP-2, MMP-9 and
Downloaded by [University of Florida] at 00:09 20 October 2017 connective tissue growth factor as well as mRNA expressionof TGF-b1, MMP-2, MMP-9 and TIMP-1 was significantlyincreased after injecting Hcy compared with normal group.At the same time, it also found that the expression level ofcollagen I and collagen III was increased in normal withHcy injection group when compared with the normal con-trol group [43]. These suggested Hcy promotes intestinalfibrosis which may be attributed to its effects of increasinginflammatory damage, promoting the expression of profi-brogenic cytokines and influencing MMP/TIMP balance.In addition to the regulation of MMPs and TIMPs infibrosis, Hcy also participates in fibrosis through othermechanism. In liver fibrosis, Hcy is an intermediate inmethionine metabolism, which takes place mainly in theliver. Impaired liver function leads to altered methionineand Hcy metabolism. Garcia-Tevijano et al. [44] have shownthat the TIMP-1 and a1(I) procollagen were upregulated invascular smooth muscle cells and liver stellate cells afterexposing to pathological concentrations of Hcy. The similarresults have also been reported by Sen et al. [45], and theyrevealed that Hcy induces alpha (a)-smooth muscle actin(a-SMA) and collagen I expression in mouse aortic endothe-lial cells as evidenced by immunoblot and confocal imaging.Moreover, Lei et al. [46] have demonstrated that elevatedconcentration of Hcy induced the expression of collagen I inboth cultured human liver cells and HHey mice cells. Takentogether, HHey may promote ECM production.
7. ConclusionIn this review, we have summarized the latest studiesregarding Hcy concentration in SSc and the associationbetween Hcy concentration and clinical features of SSc.Although our preliminary meta-analysis showed that theserum levels of Hcy in SSc were higher than that in healthycontrols (with two outlier studies excluded), several limita-tions need to be acknowledged and these results still need tobedemonstratedbywell-designedprospectivestudieswhether Hcy was higher in SSc. However, most studies havealso shown a higher plasma Hcy level in other systemicrheumatic diseases, and some symptoms of SSc could alsobe improved significantly by using strategies to lower plasmaHcy level. Therefore, the plasma Hcy level still could beused as a therapeutic target and potential biomarker and canhelp clinicians in developing new therapeutic strategies anddesigning novel drugs against HHey. In addition to theinvestigation of Hcy concentration in SSc, we also summar-ized the reason why Hcy was higher in SSc. And we foundthat high Hcy concentration in SSc patients may be associ-ated with folate and vitamin B12 deficiency, MTHFR andCBS gene defects, and some unhealthy lifestyle such assmoking and drinking.Unfortunately, in this secondary data analysis, someresearchers explored the difference in plasma Hcy levelsbetween limited cutaneous SSc and diffuse cutaneous SScsubtypes, while others analyzed the correlation between Hcylevels and clinical features of SSc, and the results werecontradictoryorinconclusiveamongthesefindings.
Therefore, we were not able to further illustrate whether theplasma Hcy level is related to the disease activity of SSc dueto some detailed information being unavailable. But, therewas one study that showed a significant correlation betweenNVC patterns ('early', 'active' or 'late' pattern) and plasmaHcy levels that progressively increased from the early to thelate pattern (p  1/4 .020) [13], and this suggest that the SScpatients with high disease activity would have high level ofHcy. However, we cannot ignore this important aspect andfurtherstudieswillbeneededtobetterexplorethisassociation.On the other hand, we also analyzed the possible mecha-nisms of Hcy in endothelial dysfunction of SSc. Hcy mayinduce endothelial dysfunction and vascular damage throughinducing oxidant stress and inflammatory or prothromboticconditions as well as altering NO release and increasing col-lagen production. For the possible mechanism of Hcy infibrosis of SSc, there are three possible mechanisms includ-ing promoting ECM production, influencing MMP/TIMPbalance and inducing myofibroblast proliferation and differ-entiation. However, these mechanisms are still not com-pletely clear.
