Keywords:Diabetic foot ulcerAcellular matrixStandard of careMeta-analysisRandomized controlled triala b s t r a c tBackground: Diabetic foot ulcer(DFU) is a chronic, refractory disease in need of multidisciplinaryendeavour, miscellaneous strategies have been adopted to address this annoying issue, including acel-lular dermal matrix(ADM)/negative pressure wound therapy/standard of care (SOC) etc. However,consensus has not been reached. As a promising procedure, the efficacy and safety of ADM remainscontroversial. We therefore performed a meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials (RCTs) to comparethe efficacy and safety of acellular dermal matrix to standard of care in DFU.Methods: Databases, including Pubmed, Medline, Embase and Cochrane library were searched to identifyRCTs comparing ADM to SOC in DFU patients. The outcomes mainly included complete wound healing,mean time to heal and adverse events.Results: A total of 632 DFU patients from 6 RCTs were subjected to meta-analysis. The results showedthat compared with the merely SOC, the complete healing rate in ADM group was higher both at 12weeks [risk ratio (RR) 2.31, 95% confidence interval (CI) 1.42 to 3.76] and 16 weeks [RR 1.57, 95% CI 1.28 to1.93]. The mean time to complete wound healing was shorter in ADM group [MD  1/4 2.98, 95% CI: 5.15to 0.82]. The occurrence of adverse event in both groups showed no significant difference [RR 0.98, 95%CI 0.58 to 1.67].Conclusion: Compared with standard of care, acellular dermal matrix may accelerate the healing velocityof uninfected, non-ischemic, full-thickness diabetic foot ulcer. Acellular dermal matrix showed superi-ority compared with standard of care alone, while generating no more complications.(c) 2017 IJS Publishing Group Ltd. Published by Elsevier Ltd. According to a global estimation, In 2013, 382 million peoplehad diabetes; this number is expected to rise to 592 million by 2035[1]. The lifetime incidence of DFU, which may cost as much as$28000 at the first 2 years after diagnosis, in patients with diabetescould be as high as 25% [2,3]. DFU is a chronic, refractory and easy-to-recur disease in need of multidisciplinary endeavour. Also, DFUis the principal cause of hospitalization and non-traumatic lowerextremity amputation [4]. The reported healing rate varied from 20.0% to 46.2%[7,8]. In one meta-analysis, the pooled healing rate of SOC is 24% at12 weeks [9], which is far from reaching satisfactory expectation. Inrecent years, several new modalities such as negative pressuretherapy, topical therapies, hyperbaric oxygen therapy, growth fac-tors, bioengineered skin substitutes, electrophysical therapy bringabout new opportunities for DFU patients, however consensus hasnot been reached [6]. The reported healing rate using ADM could beup to 80% at 12 weeks [10]. It suggests that ADM may be a prom-ising method for DFU. In recent 2 years, 3 new RCTs publishedwhich help us regain a new understanding of this relatively newbut not prevalent method. Our cliche on the utility of ADM in DFUmay change.ADM has long been used as soft tissue replacement andcommonly be used in the field of wound healing and tissue repairand reconstruction [11]. Extracellular matrix (ECM) is critical inwound healing, as it could provide structural support and signals tomodulate cellular responses [12]. Commonly, donor skin is decel-lularized using a proprietary process, preserving structure andbioactive agents within the dermal matrix that may support hostcell repopularization [13]. Possible mechanism may be providing abarrieragainstcontamination,promotingrevascularization,increasing cell regeneration, containing moist wound environment[14e17]. Several published articles have evaluated in clinical trialsand demonstrated its success in accelerating the healing rate[7,8,17]. Some published articles have already proved its efficacyand safety in third degree burns, scar reconstruction, acute andchronic wounds [17,18]. However, whether ADM is more effectivethan SOC alone in DFU is still lack of adequate high-level evidence.Thus, we conducted this meta-analysis of RCTs to determinewhether ADM is superior to SOC for the DFU. This article does notaim to analyze the difference between various ADM products.
2. Materials and methodsThis meta-analysis was performed in accordance with thePreferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) [19] reporting guidelines for the conduct ofmeta-analysis of intervention trials.2.1. Search strategyPubmed, Medline, Embase and Cochrane library were searchedup to August 2016 for comparative studies involving ADM in themanagement of DFU. The search terms were as follows: "acellularmatrix", "acellular dermal graft tissue", "decellularized dermalscaffold", "diabetic foot", "foot ulcer", "dysvascular foot". We onlyincluded English-language articles. Reference lists of all eligiblestudies and relevant reviews were manually searched for anyadditional trials.
2.2. Inclusion criteria and study selectionWe identified RCTs comparing ADM to SOC in DFU. Any type ofRCTs consisted of more than 10 participants per group wereincluded. Outcomes included the healing rate, time to heal, woundarea reduction and adverse events. Articles that reported at leastone outcome were included and those without the outcome mea-sures of interest were excluded. Non-RCT, retrospective studies,letters, comments, editorials and guidelines were excluded.2.3. Data abstraction and quality assessmentTwo authors independently reviewed all titles and abstracts ofstudies identified by the above searches. Full texts of any poten-tially useful studies were reviewed, and disagreements wereresolved by discussion. Data on patient characteristics (age, woundsize, and other baseline characteristics), intervention and outcomeswere extracted in duplicate by the two authors, using a standard-ized form. Data in other forms (i.e. median, interquartile range,mean +- 95% CI, diagram) were converted to mean +- SD according toCochrane Handbook [20]. If the data were not reported numerically,we extracted them by manual measurements from the publishedfigures.Two authors independently assessed the risk of bias of theincluded studies, based on the following items: random sequencegeneration, allocation concealment, blinding, incomplete outcomedata, selective outcome reporting and other sources of bias [20].Disagreement was resolved by the third author. The quality of ev-idence of outcomes was judged according to the modified Jadadscale [21]. Two authors independently evaluated five factors (gen-eration of allocation sequence, allocation concealment, investigatorblindness, description of withdrawals and drop-outs, efficacy ofrandomization).
2.4. Statistical analysisAll calculations were performed using Revman 5.3 software.Mean difference (MD) with 95% confidence interval (95% CI) wascalculated for the continuous data and risk ratios (RR) with 95% CIfor the dichotomous data. Heterogeneity among studies was esti-mated using I [2] statistic and substantial heterogeneity was rep-resented by an I2 value > 50%. A fixed effects model was used if theheterogeneity test did not reveal statistical significance (I2 < 50%, P> 0.1). Otherwise, we adopted the random effects model. P < 0.05was considered to be statistically significant. Sensitivity analysiswas performed to explore the impact of an individual study bydeleting one study each time.3.1. Search resultsThe initial search yielded 266 citations, of which 7 wereexcluded due to duplication. After screening the titles and ab-stracts, 247 studies were excluded based on the inclusion criteria,while 6 studies were excluded after reading the full texts for thefollowing reasons: lack of controlled study [22e24]; retrospectivestudy [25]; SOC is not the control group [26]; skin substitute con-sisted of cellular components [27]. Finally, 6 RCTs [7,8,17,28e30]involving 632 patients were eligible for data extraction and meta-analysis (Fig. 1). Of these 632 patients, 330 patients belonged tothe ADM group and 302 to the SOC group.
3.2. Study characteristics and quality assessmentThe characteristics of the included studies are presented inwound healing in 12 weeks according to the healing rate in the first4 weeks, three studies [17,28,29] reported the number of completewound healing in 16 weeks. Time to heal was reported in allstudies, but only 4 studies [7,8,29,30]were eligible for meta-analysis. Adverse events such as infection, amputation, seromawere reported in all studies. Outcomes of wound area reduction,healing rate, quality of life were reported, yet these outcomes werenot analyzed owing to insufficient data.Risk of bias in the included studies is shown in Fig. 2. Among theincluded studies, all were described as being randomized but onlyone study described the use of paper block system. One study re-ported allocation concealment. The numbers and reasons forwithdrawals and clinically relevant outcomes were reported in allstudies. Two studies reported blindness merely to assessors, as theADM was easily to be recognized during the application by thestudy stuff. Given the intrinsic characteristic of such RCTs, doubleblindness was hard to obtain, thus in modified Jadad scoring,blindness does not take into account by rating each study a "2"score.4.1. Complete wound healing
Five studies [7,8,17,28,29] with 592 DFUs reported the number ofcomplete wound healing in 12 weeks, one study [30] containing 40DFUs estimated the possible wound healing in 12 weeks accordingto the healing rate in the first 4 weeks. The complete healing rate inADM group was higher than SOC group [risk ratio (RR) 2.31, 95%confidence interval (CI) 1.42 to 3.76; Fig. 3]. Deleting the 4-weekstudy would not change the result of this meta-analysis [RR 1.94,95% CI 1.26 to 2.98]. There was, however, moderate heterogeneitybetween studies (I2  1/4 68%, P  1/4 0.01), thus, random effects modelwas used.As to the 16 weeks investigation after treatment consisting of217 DFUs, there still displayed a conspicuous superiority comparedto the SOC group [RR 1.57, 95% CI 1.28 to 1.93; Fig. 4]. As the het-erogeneity was limited (I2  1/4 37%, P  1/4 0.21)), fixed effect model wasused.4.2. Adverse eventsAll studies reported the adverse events encountered during thetrial. Most of the complications were infection, amputation,seroma, immune response, graft resorption, graft nonintegration,etc. We only pooled the adverse events related to ADM usage andDFU, such as infection, amputation and seroma. There were nosignificant differences in adverse events between ADM and SOCgroup during the follow-up ranged from 4 to 16 weeks [RR 0.98,95% CI 0.58 to 1.67; Fig. 6]. As the heterogeneity was limited(I2  1/4 0%, P  1/4 0.91)), fixed effect model was used.4.4. Healing rate/wound area reduction/quality of lifeTwo studies reported healing rate [17,30], wound healing rate inADM group ranged from 3.4 to 7.2% compared with 1.0e4.8% in SOCgroup. Two studies [28,30] reported wound area reduction, itrangedfrom73.1to91.4%inADMgroupcomparedwith34.2e80.3% in SOC group. Only one study [17] reported the elevatedscore of quality of life metrics in ADM group assessed by the ShortForm Health Survey.5. Sensitivity analysis
Sensitivity analysis investigating the influence of a single studyon the overall outcome was estimated by omitting 1 study in eachturn. Adverse events in the ADM group were not significantlydifferent from that in the SOC group when omitting anyone of thestudies. However when removing one study conducted by Zelen [7]and recalculating the remaining study, heterogeneity changed fromsevere to moderate (before, I2  1/4 77%, P  1/4 0.005; after, I2  1/4 50%,P  1/4 0.14). The results of sensitivity analysis of complete woundhealing,adverseeventswerenotmateriallydifferentiatedcompared with those of the original analysis.6. DiscussionTo our knowledge, this is the first meta-analysis of RCTscomparing the efficacy and safety of ADM in the management ofDFU. Six studies satisfied our inclusion criteria. Four studies weremulticenter RCTs, two studies were single-center RCTs. Inclusionand exclusion criteria were similar within the included studies.Participants in every study were diabetic foot ulcer with adequatevascular perfusion. All recruited patients had full-thickness ulcerwhich corresponded to Wagner Grade 2 or University of TexasGrade 1 to 2 and showed no significant healing after conventionaltreatment for at least 6 weeks before the trial, thus they could bedefined as refractory DFU. Infection or ulcer containing the tendon,bone was excluded. Patients in experimental group underwent a
specific ADM plus the SOC, while patients in control group receivedmerely SOC. SOC refers to the combination of several routinemethods, they were sharp debridement, glucose control, infectioncontrol, off-loading, and daily dressing change.The pooled results indicated that ADM may help acceleratingthe healing velocity of foot ulcer in patients who have diabetes. Thecomplete wound healing in ADM group is 1.57 times more than SOCgroup at 16 weeks, and 2.31 times at 12 weeks. Sensitivity assess-ment implied that elimination of any study would not reverse theoriginal outcome. ADM showed superior advantages to conven-tional treatment, standard of care, meanwhile, generated no morecomplications [RR 0.98, 95% CI 0.58 to 1.67]. Many surgeons is notwilling to use ADM in case of infection transmission and immu-nogenicity [31]. However, in this first pooled analysis of adverseevent, the ADM group showed no statistic difference comparedwith SOC group. Additionally, the "time to heal" is reduced by usingADM [MD  1/4 2.56, 95% CI: 3.55 to 1.57, P < 0.001]. Althoughsensitivity analysis confirmed the stability of the pooled result of"time to heal", when omitting Zelen's [7] study, homogeneityincreased considerably. This may be explained by the differentduration of the follow-up because the "time to heal" estimation waslargely based on the trend of healing rate. The diagram delineatedthe healing process was in opposite "S" curve, thus the wound
healed faster at first and then slowed down. Zelen [7] conducted a16 weeks trial while the other three studies [8,32,33] included inthis analysis only lasted for up to 12 weeks. If we remove the 16-week study, the homogeneity wound increased. However, the 16-week study wouldn't be eliminated, for a prolonged study wasmore likely to close the reality.Different commercial products of ADM were included in thismeta-analysis, including DermACELL, Graftjacket, Integra DermalRegeneration Template(IDRT), human reticular acellular dermismatrix(HR-ADM), ADM is human-derived in five studies andanimal-derived in one study [17]. If omitting the animal-derivedone, human-derived ADM still show superiority to SOC group incomplete healing rate at 12 weeks[RR 2.55, 95% CI 1.30 to 4.99] andno difference in adverse event[RR 1.04, 95% CI 0.56 to 1.94]. As allproducts were manufactured under parallel process and playedsimilar roles in wound healing, they were assessed as a whole.According to risk of bias and modified Jadad score, the strengthof evidence is moderate, as most of the trials related to commercialcorporations which may especially reduce the publication ofnegative outcomes, what's more, blindness of investigator onlydescribed in two studies owing to the intrinsic character of suchtrialddADM is easily to be found on the wound bed. Except for theblindness, the quality of these clinical trials was well controlled.
Given that the above mentioned two shortcomings could not beeasily overcame in recent years, although ranked as moderate, thisevidence is still recommendable and could provide meaningfulsuggestions for clinical treatment of DFU.Reyzelman A.M. et al. [34] conducted a literature review andanalysis of one specific human acellular dermal wound matrix(-Graftjacket) in 2015, three studies containing 154 DFUs wereincluded. On account of insufficient data, merely two assessmentindicatordtime to heal, proportion of wounds that healeddwereanalyzed with two studies involved in each one. The pooled averagetime to heal for ADM group is around 2e7 weeks shorter than thatreported by previous retrospective studies [22,25]. Santema T.B.et al. [35] reported a systematic review about skin substitute. Bio-engineered or artificial skin, autografts, allografts or xenograftswere defined as skin substitute in this review. 17 studies wereincluded and pooled analysis showed the healing rate wasincreased and amputation rate was reduced by using skin substi-tute. As far as I know, although several published articles haveproved the advantages of acellular/cellular skin substitute in thehealing of DFU [10,27,36,37], they actually acts in different ways notonly physically but also biomechanically. ADM is produced bydecelluarizing an autologous or xenologous skin and retaininginsoluable molecules such as elastin, collagen, fibronectin [13]. Itserves as a scaffold and may provide signals that can promoteproliferation, cell attachment, migration etc [11,12]. Cellular dermalmatrix is usually made up of extracellular matrix, viable cells,growth factors, etc [10,37,38]. Being damaged by consistentinflammation, chronic wound is known to be lack of growth factors,stem cell, proteases etc [39]. Cellular dermal matrix is believed toaddress this defect. As to clinical application, mostly ADM is used 1or 2 times, while cellular skin substitute is reported to be 4 to 8times due to short shelf-life, death of live cell [31,36,40]. In Sante-ma's review, no further information about the difference betweencellular and acellular skin substitute was mentioned. Accountingfor their difference in physical mechanism and clinical application,it's advisable for us to conduct respective summary. What's more,studies which are pertinent to ADM were not fully included andtwo new studies published this year.The present study has some limitations. First, publication biasmay be the most important limitation, as majority of the studieswere related to corresponding corporations. Second, there was alack of uniform ADM product, the variation of different kind ofdressing products might also be a problem. Third, on behalf of thepatients, principal investigators had the right to adopt a secondADM in Walter's [28] study, thus a better outcome may generate.Fourth, the sample size of each included study was relatively small.Fifth, data was insufficient for total analysis. Observation indicatorswere limited to 16 weeks, with no long-term follow-up. Furtherstudies are needed to clarify the long-term outcomes and recur-rence rate. Finally, the cost-efficiency should also take into account,further RCTs about the total expense are needed to illustrate theclinical value of such method.
7. ConclusionCompared with standard of care, ADM may help accelerating thehealing velocity of uninfected, non-ischemic, full-thickness footulcer in patients who have diabetes. ADM plus SOC showed supe-rior advantages compared with merely conventional treatment, atthe same time, generated no more complications. However, due tothe variations and limited statistics in the included studies, morelarge-sample and high-quality clinical trials and systemic reviewson the cost-efficiency, long-term follow-up are needed in the futureto demonstrate the efficacy and safety of ADM in DFU treatment.Ethical approvalSources of fundingNone.Author contributionXiaoshuang Guo: Main author of this article.
