Conflicts of interestNone declared.*Plain language summary available onlineSummaryBackground The antimalarials (AMs) hydroxychloroquine (HCQ) and chloroquine(CQ) have demonstrated variable cutaneous response rates in cutaneous lupuserythematosus (CLE).Objectives We sought to assess the global cutaneous response rates to HCQ andCQ, with respect to CLE subtypes, based on previously published studies.Methods We performed a systematic review and meta-analysis of studies publishedin MEDLINE, Embase and the Cochrane Library between 1965 and December2015. The proportions of responders to AMs according to CLE subtypes wereextracted from individual studies and pooled using random-effects or fixed mod-els. The odds ratio (OR) was used as the measure of association to compare theresponse rates between CLE subtypes and AMs.Results Among 1990 courses of treatment with AMs from 31 included studies, theoverall response rate to AMs was 63% [95% confidence interval (CI) 55-70], withimportant statistical heterogeneity across the included studies. HCQ had a higheroverall efficacy than CQ, but this was not significant (OR 148, 95% CI 098-223). The response rate to AMs was different between CLE subtypes, rangingfrom 31% (95% CI 20-44) for chilblain lupus to 91% (95% CI 87-93) for acuteCLE. The response was significantly higher for acute CLE than for subacute CLEand intermittent CLE. In case of failure of monotherapy with AM, the combinationof quinacrine with HCQ or CQ seemed effective, whereas too little data wereavailable to assess the efficacy of the switch to another AM agent.Conclusions Wide discrepancies in cutaneous response to AMs are observedbetween CLE subtypes. A specific therapeutic approach considering CLE subtypesmay improve CLE management.

At the present time,it is generally accepted3 that CLE may be divided into acuteCLE (ACLE), subacute CLE (SCLE), intermittent CLE (ICLE)/lupus tumidus and chronic CLE (CCLE). The latter is furthersubdivided into discoid lupus erythematosus (DLE), lupuserythematosus profundus (LEP) and chilblain lupus erythe-matosus.3First-line treatment of CLE includes general measures suchas photoprotection and smoking cessation,4 as well as localtreatments with topical steroids or calcineurin inhibitors,5
especially in cases of mild CLE. The antimalarials (AMs)hydroxychloroquine (HCQ) and chloroquine (CQ) are usuallyrecommended as the first-line systemic treatment of CLE,5 andhave demonstrated cutaneous response rates of 50-90%.5 Theuse of second-line systemic treatments such as the antimalarialquinacrine, or of methotrexate, thalidomide or lenalidomide,is limited by the availability of these drugs in some countries,or by their potentially severe toxicity.6-9 Therefore, the opti-mization of AM treatment is an important challenge inpatients with CLE. Furthermore, it is important to understandthe factors associated with a favourable cutaneous response toAMs. To the best of our knowledge, global cutaneous responserates to HCQ and CQ have never been assessed, particularlybetween CLE subtypes.In order to characterize the efficacy profile of AMs better,we performed a systematic review of the literature and ameta-analysisofobservationalstudiesorrandomizedcontrolled studies to obtain pooled cutaneous response ratesto HCQ and CQ in patients with CLE. The primary aim of thestudy was to assess overall response rates of AM agents and tocompare these response rates between CLE subtypes. Thesecondary aim was to assess the efficacy of the switch ofone AM agent to another, as well as that of different AMcombinations.Materials and methods
Literature search and information sourcesWe searched Embase (1974 to December 2015), MEDLINE(1965 to December 2015) and the Cochrane Database of Sys-tematic Reviews until December 2015 for original articles,without language restrictions. In addition, the grey literaturewas explored by searching the conference abstracts of theAmerican College of Rheumatology, the American Academy ofDermatology and the European Academy of Dermatology andVenereology until December 2015. The search strategy com-bined free-text search, exploded MeSH and Emtree terms andall synonyms of the following medical subject headings terms:'cutaneous lupus erythematosus' (and subtypes), 'antimalari-als', 'hydroxychloroquine' and 'chloroquine' (a detailed searchstrategy is provided in Appendix S1; see Supporting Informa-tion). We also searched for additional articles from the refer-ence lists of relevant papers obtained from the electronicsearch. When the data of interest could not be found in themanuscripts, we contacted the main authors, and excluded thestudies for which these data were not available.Study selection and eligibility criteriaObservational studies or randomized controlled studies wereconsidered if: (i) they included patients with CLE with orwithout associated SLE; (ii) the number of patients was atleast five; (iii) patients received HCQ or CQ as first-line sys-temic treatment, or a combination therapy (HCQ and quina-crine, or CQ and quinacrine) after failure of a monotherapywith HCQ or CQ; (iv) the number of patients treated withAMs and the number of responders were available to calcu-late the AM response rate; and (v) data regarding specificassessment of cutaneous involvement were available in themanuscripts. Reviews, editorials, guidelines and case reportswere excluded (Fig. 1). The quality of studies was assessedusing the Newcastle-Ottawa Scale10 for observational studies,the Cochrane Collaboration's tool for assessing risk of bias,11
and the Jadad12 scale for randomized controlled studies. Cutaneous response to AMs was defined accordingto the definition used in each included study, thus by theCutaneous Lupus Erythematosus Disease Area and SeverityIndex (CLASI)14 or according to study-specific criteria consid-ering the size and number of lesions.Statistical analysesThe proportion of responders to AMs (HCQ or CQ), as wellas according to each CLE subtype, were extracted from indi-vidual studies and subsequently pooled following a double-arcsine transformation after continuity correction.15 In orderto compare the efficacy between AMs and CLE subtypes, weconstructed 2 9 2 tables and used the odds ratio (OR) asthe measure of association. Publication bias was examinedusing a funnel plot. Heterogeneity (Cochran's v2 and I2
tests) was considered statistically significant at P < 010 andI2 > 50%. When significant heterogeneity was absent, pooledresponse rates and 95% confidence intervals (CIs) were cal-culated using a fixed-effects model, whereas random-effectsmodels according to the method of DerSimonian and Laird16were used when significant heterogeneity was present. Sub-group analyses according to study design (prospective vs. ret-rospectivedatacollection)andthemethodstoassesscutaneous response (CLASI vs. study-specific response crite-ria) were performed to analyse potential heterogeneity. Optimization of AM treatment thereforeremains an important challenge in CLE. Our study furtherrevealed major differences in cutaneous response rates to AMsbetween CLE subtypes, ranging from 31% (95% CI 20-44)for chilblain lupus to 91% (95% CI 87-93) for ACLE.Which AM is most effective as a first-line treatment in CLEremains controversial. By pooling studies reporting on the effi-cacy of either HCQ or CQ, we found that the overallcutaneous response rate was higher for CQ. However, pooledestimates from papers that enabled direct comparison betweenHCQ and CQ suggested that HCQ was more efficacious thanCQ, but this was not significant, possibly due to lack ofpower. These discrepancies may also be due to differences insample sizes, to the larger reported number of patients treatedwith HCQ compared with CQ, possibly to differences in theproportion of each CLE subtype, and to the various methodsused to assess cutaneous response across the studies.We found that the response rate of ACLE to AMs was 25-fold higher than that of SCLE, and 39-fold higher than that ofICLE. There are various strategies that might be used followingfailure of AM monotherapy. These include smoking cessa-tion,51 increasing HCQ doses to reach blood concentrations >750 ng mL1,52 switching to another AM agent (whichseemed poorly effective in the long term, although too fewdata were available to draw final conclusion)39 or combiningAMs. Regarding the latter strategy, we found a pooledresponse rate of 66% (95% CI 51-80) for the combination ofquinacrine with HCQ or CQ. However, quinacrine is notavailable in many countries, and may have side-effects such asjaundice-like discoloration of the skin.48 Solid data regardingthe risk of retinal toxicity using combinations of AMs are stillmissing.Among the limitations of this meta-analysis is the importantstatistical heterogeneity measured across the included studies.This might reflect differences between study design, CLE sub-types, doses and types of AM agents, and modalities of cuta-neousresponseassessment.However,subgroupanalysesshowed similar response rates according to study design andmethods used to assess cutaneous response. Consequently,pooled estimates were obtained by combining individual studyestimates using random-effects models, as recommended inthe context of heterogeneity.15 In some studies, some patientswith more than one CLE subtype were considered to haveonly the subtype with the highest risk of developing systemicorgan manifestations (ACLE > SCLE > CCLE > ICLE).44 Thisconstraint may have contributed to limiting the sample size insome of the less common CLE subtypes. However, this studyallows us to obtain data on rare CLE subtypes. Although directcomparisons with other CLE subtypes were not possible, bothchilblain lupus and LEP seemed to be more refractory to AMsthan other CLE subtypes, with overall response rates of 31%(95% CI 20-44) and 48% (95% CI 31-66), respectively.Studies focusing on the treatment of these rare CLE subtypesare currently lacking to improve CLE management.Overall, in this systematic review and meta-analysis of 31studies including 1990 courses of AM use, we found an over-all response rate to AMs of 63% (95% CI 55-70), withimportant differences between CLE subtypes. While the cur-rently available data did not allow us to draw any formal con-clusion regarding the superiority of one AM agent overanother, we believe that HCQ should be preferred as the first-linesystemicagent,givenitsbettertoleranceprofile
(particularly for retinal toxicity)53 and the possibility of mea-suring blood concentration, which is associated with cuta-neous response and can be used to identify nonadherentpatients.38,52 In case of refractory CLE, smoking cessation,51adaptation of the dose of HCQ to its blood concentration52 oraddition of quinacrine, when available, to HCQ or CQ shouldbe considered. In order to improve CLE management, futureprospective studies should focus on various CLE subtypes withstandardized outcome reporting measures, in order to definetherapeutic strategies based on CLE subtypes.References
