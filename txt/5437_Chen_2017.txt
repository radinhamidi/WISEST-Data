Methods: All relevant literature was retrieved from the PubMed, EMBASE, Web ofScience and China National Knowledge Infrastructure (CNKI) databases. The qualities ofthese studies were evaluated using a modified version of the Newcastle-Ottawa scale. Thecharacteristics and clinical manifestations of involved individuals were extracted from eachstudy. Pooled odds ratio (OR) was calculated using the random effects-method, and the het-erogeneity between studies was quantified using the I2 statistic. Results: Of 330 studies iden-tified by the search strategy, six were included in this review. In total, 733 cases were familialSLE and 1405 were sporadic SLE. Analysis revealed that photosensitivity, nephritis andthrombocytopenia were negatively associated with familial SLE, with OR (95% CI) valuesof0.73(0.60-0.89),0.72(0.59-0.88)and0.75(0.57-0.98),respectively. Conclusions:Photosensitivity, thrombocytopenia and renal involvement could be more common in non-familial SLE, which should be further confirmed by well-designed studies with largepopulations.Lupus (2017) 0, 1-6.Key words: Systemic lupus erythematosus; family history; meta-analysis
IntroductionSystemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) is a complexautoimmune disease characterized by diverse clin-ical phenotypes and various autoantibodies againstnuclear components. The disease is believed toresult from an interaction of genetic, epigenetic,hormonal and environmental factors.1 The contri-bution of genetic factors to SLE development wasevidenced by the high heritability (> 66%) and sib-ling risk ratio (8< s <29), and the higher concord-ance rate in monozygotic twins (24-69%) than indizygotic twins or siblings (2-5%) among SLE aswell.2 Familial lupus accounts for about 10% ofSLE cases and has long been appreciated asan appropriate model to study the interaction ofgenetic and environmental factors.3 More than40 susceptibility loci have been identified withinthe past several decades.4 However, further studiesare required to determine how the causal variantsat each locus lead to an SLE-specific phenotype.5On the other hand, as a clinician, one of the mostfrequent questions asked by patients is whether the''healthy'' family members will develop SLE andhow their clinical manifestations, disease durationand prognosis are different from SLE patients with-out a family history. Thus, to better understand theassociation of genetic factors with the manifest-ations and the pathogenesis of SLE, we performeda meta-analysis to evaluate the correlation of theclinical manifestations and laboratory disorders inpatients with familial and sporadic-onset SLE.MethodsLiterature searchA literature search to identify all relevant articleswas conducted according to the search strategiespublished by Furlan et al.6 The literature databasesof PubMed, EMBASE, Web of Science and ChinaNational Knowledge Infrastructure (CNKI) weresearched without language restrictions. We also compared theauthors' names, affiliations and geographic loca-tions and the periods of studies to identify sequen-tial or multiple publications. If several articleswere published by the same authors using thesame patients' data, only studies with the largestsample size or the most recent study were included.Two reviewers separately included papers usingthe inclusion and exclusion criteria. Relevant datafrom each eligible article was extracted independ-ently by two authors using a standardized dataextraction form: the first author's name, publica-tion date, location, total number of SLE patients,sex ratio, the mean age at inclusion, the mean ageonset, clinical manifestations (expressed as eitherthe number or percentage of familial and sporadicpatients). Discrepancies were resolved by groupdiscussion.
Quality evaluationThe quality assessment of included studies wasbased on the Newcastle-Ottawa assessment (NOS)scale.7 The criteria for selection assessment weremodified according to the Critical Appraisal SkillsProgram (CASP)8 and Quality Assessment Tool forSystematicReviewsofObservationalStudies(QATSO)9 to make the subcategories more applic-able to grouped cohort studies. Total score variesfrom 0 to nine points, with nine representing thehighest quality.Statistical analysisMantel-Haenszel ORs with 95% CIs were used toassess the strength of the association of familialhistory with various clinical and immunologicalfeatures.10 The ORs for each study were calculatedand then pooled in a meta-analysis using ReviewManager (Rev Man) (computer program) version5.3 (Copenhagen: The Nordic Cochrane Centre,The Cochrane Collaboration, 2011).Both the chi-square-based Q-statistic test and I2were used to assess the heterogeneity among stu-dies. The I2 value represented the proportion ofvariation due to heterogeneities in studies. The het-erogeneity level was classified into four categories:unimportant(0-40%),moderate(30-60%),substantial (50-90%) and considerable (75-100%)heterogeneity.11 If the p value of the heterogeneityQ-statistic  0.10 or I2 50%, the random-effectsmodel was selected.12 Otherwise, a fixed-effectsmodel was adopted.13 Publication bias was investi-gated by funnel plot if there were more than10 studies involved.ResultsEligible studies and characteristics

DiscussionThis study aimed to investigate the impact of famil-ial history on the clinical manifestations andlaboratory disorders in SLE patients. We foundthat photosensitivity, thrombocytopenia and renalinvolvement, but not the other clinical and labora-tory parameters, were negatively associated withfamilial SLE.ThepathogenesisofSLEismultifactorial,including genetic and environmental factors aswellasinnateandadaptiveimmunologicalabnormalities.Somerisklociidentifiedbygenome-wide association studies (GWAS), such asITGAM, FcgR, PRDM1-ATG5, and TNFAIP3,are strongly associated with SLE.21 One geneticvariant may be involved in several disease-relatedmanifestations, suggesting a common pathogenicmechanism shared by a group of disorders. Forinstance, the genetic variants of ITGAM werefound to be simultaneously associated with theinvolvementofskin,kidneyandjoints,andimmunologic disorders as well.22 Accordingly, wefound that photosensitivity, thrombocytopenia andrenal involvement were negatively associated withfamilial SLE, which could be related to some spe-cific genetic variants in a common locus. In thecourse of SLE disease, more than 75% of patientshave skin damage as one of the earliest symptomsand signs of SLE.1,21,23 As for lupus, malar rashand discoid rash are two directly visible signs.The rashes lead to an early diagnosis and thus anearly treatment of LE (including SLE, discoid LEand subacute cutaneous LE). Early treatment may
reduce the risk of the development of renal andhematologic diseases, and decrease the risk of thetransformation from cutaneous LE to SLE as well.In addition, patients have better compliance whenpatients and their families pay more attention tothe disease. Thus, SLE may be well controlled atearly onset without developing into severe symp-toms in these patients. All together, these factorscould contribute to the negative association offamily SLE with photosensitivity, thrombocyto-penia and renal involvement. In accordance withthis finding, Wang et al. reported that class Vlupus nephritis was less common in familial SLE,whereas Systemic Lupus Erythematosus DiseaseIndex score and other clinic pathological featureswere not significantly different between 34 familialSLE and 102 sporadic SLE patients.24There are several limitations to be consideredwhen interpreting the results of this meta-analysis.First, with respect to the genetic component, theconcordanceratewashigherinmonozygotictwins (>20%) than in dizygotic twins and otherfull siblings (2-5%), indicating a linkage betweenthe degree of relatives and genetic factors.25-27 Inthis meta-analysis, there were not enough data toanalysiswhetherthelupus-relatedparametersvaried with the degree of relatives.
