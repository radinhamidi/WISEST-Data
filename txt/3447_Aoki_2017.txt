Description of Malignancy Rates in Childhood- andAdult-Onset Systemic Lupus Erythematousby Proportional Meta-analysisPriscila Rodrigues da Silva Aoki, MD,* Regina El Dib, PhD,*Clovis Artur Almeida Silva, MD, PhD,+ and Claudia Saad Magalhaes, MD, PhD*Objective: The aim of this study was to describe the malignancy ratesin childhood and adult-onset systemic lupus erythematous (SLE) byproportional meta-analysis.Methods: Two reviewers screened data from PubMed (1966-2015),EMBASE (1980-2015), and LILACS (1982-2015) for SLE-associatedmalignancy. Proportional meta-analysis with a random-effects model and95% confidence intervals (CIs) were calculated according to SLE onset ageand mean follow-up time. Statistical differencewas defined by 95% CI overlap.Results: Malignancy rate reported in 30 case series with 96,578 subjectswas 3.4% (95% CI, 0.0260-0.0442; I2 = 97.6%; P < 0.0001). It was 4.2%(95% CI, 0.0318-0.0531; I2 = 98%; P < 0.0001) in 25 adult-onset SLE se-ries, compared with 0.5% (95% CI, 0.0003-0.0154; I2 = 62.6%; P = 0.03)in 5 childhood-onset SLE series. There was a significant difference in themalignancy rates, with lower rates in childhood-onset compared withadult-onset SLE. Overall, in those with less than 5 years' follow-up, itwas 2.8% (95% CI, 0.013-0.047; I2 = 91%; P < 0.0001) compared with3.6% (95% CI, 0.0226-0.0531; I2 = 98.3%; P < 0.0001) in those with morethan 5 years' follow-up, which was not significant, with 95% CI overlap.Conclusions: The meta-analysis indicated lower malignancy rates inpediatric-onset SLE compared with adult-onset SLE, but accrued data fromchildhood-onset SLE are still needed.Key Words: cohort studies, malignancy, meta-analysis,systemic lupus erythematosus(J Clin Rheumatol 2017;23: 00-00)T
here is increasing concern of malignancy susceptibility in sys-temic lupus erythematosus (SLE). Risk estimates have moreoften been calculated in case series and population cohorts,1,2 in-cluding subjects with definite diagnosis of SLE according to theAmerican College of Rheumatology 1997 criteria3 or previousAmerican Rheumatism Association 1982 criteria.4 More recently,collaborative work reported by SLICC (Systemic Lupus Interna-tional Collaborating Clinics) group was published.5 Estimatedstandardized incidence ratio, which is the ratio of observed andexpected malignancy rates in SLE patients compared with refer-ence population, was also reported.Other question of interest is the comparative incidence of he-matologic and nonhematologic malignancy rates.6-9 Associatedlifestyle risks factors, such as smoking, obesity, viral exposure,and immunosuppressive treatment; or genetic factors; or race orethnicity could account for increased relative risks. Recent dataconfirmed that lymphoma incidence is higher in SLE patients,but the risk factors of such association are still unknown.10
Studies on adult-onset SLE confirmed increased risk of non-Hodgkin lymphoma, lung, vulvar/vaginal, and thyroid and decreasedrisk of hormone-sensitive breast and prostate cancer. The higher riskof vulvar/vaginal site is related to abnormal virus clearance such ashuman papillomavirus. The increased risk of non Hodgkin lym-phomas is related to multiple mechanisms, including viral reacti-vation and cytokine up-regulation.8,11,12 Bernatsky et al.13 addressedthe issue of SLE disease activity versus treatment, suggesting cyclo-phosphamide exposure and high cumulative steroid doses wereassociated to higher risk, whereas disease activity itself was notclearly associated with malignancy. Overall, reports of malignancyin SLE subjects vary from 1.1% to 2.6%.5 There were reportsfrom multisite international cohorts from more than 30 centers.There is also increased malignancy risk associated withchildhood-onset SLE. The incidence rates are higher comparedwith the reference population of those not affected by SLE.14,15Still, it is not known if time of disease exposure or similar concur-rent risk factors could be implicated. Major concern for both pedi-atric and adult individuals could be related to cytotoxic or biologictreatment. However, there is no previous study comparing cancerincidence in childhood and adult-onset SLE populations.Therefore, we revised the reports of malignancy associatedwith SLE, comparing childhood and adult-onset SLE, by propor-tional meta-analysis of cohort studies.METHODS
This report complies with the MOOSE (Meta-analysis ofObservational Studies in Epidemiology) statements and quality-control guidelines of prognosis studies.16,17 Studies were includedif participants had an established diagnosis of SLE by clinicalevaluation and classification criteria,3,4 regardless of age, sex, orexposure to steroids or immunosuppressive or biologic treatment.This review included all longitudinal studies with malig-nancy reports or a proper score for the SLICC/American Collegeof Rheumatology Damage Index (SDI). The SDI scores 1 pointfor any biopsy-proven malignancy from each site, excluding dys-plasia, after 6 months of SLE onset.18 Those longitudinal studieswere mainly cohort studies with a minimum 6 months' follow-up and cancer registry reports. We excluded reviews and cross-sectional and case-control studies. We selected the articles by a search strategychoosing the terms "lupus" and "cancer" (Appendix 1). Therewas no language restriction, and reference lists of the identifiedrelevant studies were scrutinized for additional citations.Two of the authors reviewed the articles independently, to ex-tract the data. A standard form was used for data collection: au-thors, year of publication, number of participants, subjects meanage, follow-up duration, and description of the outcomes of inter-est. Studies with incomplete data about the outcome were ex-cluded from the review. Ethical approval was stated in each ofthe selected studies. We evaluated the risk of bias of the selectedarticles using a modified version of the Ottawa-Newcastle instru-ment.19 It includes confidence in assessment of exposure and out-come and adjusted analysis for differences between groups inprognostic characteristics or missing data.Statistical AnalysisMalignancy rates were treated as dichotomous variables with
95% confidence interval (CI). The meta-analysis has 3 main steps:test whether the studies results are homogeneous, obtain a globalindex about effect magnitude of homogeneity or heterogeneity,and are joined to a CI and its statistical significance. For assessingheterogeneity, there can be 2 sources of variability. First, there isthe variability due to sampling error, also named within-study var-iability. The sampling error variability is always present in a meta-analysis because every single study uses different samples. Theother source of heterogeneity is the between-studies variability,which can appear in a meta-analysis when there is true heteroge-neity among the population effect sizes estimated by individualstudies. We calculated statistical heterogeneity by I2 statistics. Sig-nificance was assumed when I2 was greater than 50%. This reflectsthe percentage of the variability in effect estimates resulting fromheterogeneity rather than sampling error.20 Assessing heterogene-ity in meta-analysis is essential because the presence versus theabsence of true heterogeneity (between-studies variability) can af-fect the statistical model the meta-analyst decides to apply. Whenstudies' results differ by the sampling error (homogeneous case), afixed-effects model can be applied to obtain an average-effectsize. By contrast, if the study results differ by more than samplingerror (heterogeneous case), then the meta-analyst can assume arandom-effects model, in order to take into account both within-and between-studies variability.Because of the clear differences of results among the includedstudies and several uncontrolled variables, we used a random-effects model21 to perform the proportional meta-analysis. The soft-ware used was StatsDirect (StatsDirect Ltd, Altrincham, Cheshire,United Kingdom).Statistical difference between studied groups was considered
significant if their combined 95% CIs did not overlap. We consideredP < 0.1 as significant for the calculation of I2 heterogeneity statistic.22,23We performed a subgroup analysis according to differentSLE onset ages, defining childhood-onset subjects at 18 years oryounger compared with those older than 18 years at SLE onset. Meanfollow-up time (<=5 vs. >5 years) subgroups were also compared.Data summary is presented in forest plots. Each horizontalline of the forest plot represents 1 cohort study included in themeta-analysis. The length of the line corresponds to 95% CI effectestimation in that case series. The effect estimates are marked by asolid black square. The size of the square represents the weightthat the corresponding study exerts in the meta-analysis. Pooledestimated rates are marked with an unfilled diamond at the bottomof the forest plot. Confidence intervals are displayed as horizontallines through the diamond; this line might be contained within thediamond if the CI is narrow.RESULTSThis literature search identified approximately 2726 titles(Fig. 1). After exclusion of duplicate records and studies that didnot meet our inclusion criteria, 56 studies were examined in fulltext for qualitative analysis. Results of 30 articles with a total of96,578 subjects were included in the meta- analysis.

Malignancy rate in 30 case series with 96,578 subjects withSLE was 3.4% (95% CI, 0.0260-0.0442). There was a significantheterogeneity (I2 = 97.6%) showing the consistency of clinical andmethodological aspects between the studies included in the meta-analysis (P < 0.0001) (Fig. 2).Malignancy rates in 25 adult and 5 childhood-onset SLE se-ries were 4.2% (95% CI, 0.0318-0.0531) and 0.5% (95% CI,0.0003-0.0154), respectively. There was a significant differencecomparing the groups according to SLE onset age, as the 95%CIs did not overlap (Fig. 2). Also, statistical heterogeneity was sig-nificant: adult-onset SLE I2 = 98%, P < 0.0001, and childhood-onset SLE, I2 = 62.6%, P = 0.03.Reported follow-up was grouped as mean follow-up time ofless than or equal to 5 years and more than 5 years. Malignancyrate from 11 studies with a total of 34,462 SLE subjects with meanfollow-up of less than or equal to 5 years was 2.8% (95% CI,0.013-0.047), whereas the rate in 16 studies with a total of30,724 SLE subjects, with mean follow-up time more than 5 years,was 3.6% (95% CI, 0.0226-0.0531). Follow-up time was not re-ported in 2 of the reported studies, comprising 19,475 SLE sub-jects (Table 1). There was no significant difference betweenthese groups as the CIs overlap. Also, I2 heterogeneity was sig-nificant in those with less than 5 years' and more than 5 years'mean follow-up time, I2 = 91%, P < 0.0001, and I2 = 98.3%,P < 0.0001, respectively.This meta-analysis pointed to a significantly higher malig-
nancy rates in adult-onset compared with childhood-onset SLE.DISCUSSIONThe main findings of malignancy reports in adult- andchildhood-onset SLE cohorts, from different geographic regionsand population sources, were combined in the meta-analysis. This systematic review adds to prior work about malignancyrisk in SLE patients by comparing different groups according toSLE onset age, including childhood-onset and adult-onset sub-jects with the same disease, although with a much lower propor-tion of pediatric subjects, because of lower frequency ofchildhood-onset SLE. This review may be limited by asymmetryin the comparison; therefore, we could not rule out bias possibilitybecause of unbalanced pooled proportions.There are prior studies, including recent meta-analyses,11,51-53addressing specific malignancy site or organ involvement in adults,such as breast, ovarian, endometrial,53-55 or urologic cancer.6,7 Inaddition, there are others reporting leukemia and lymphoma,8,12,13
but we did not perform the site-specific malignancy analysis be-cause of scarce data in childhood-onset SLE subjects. In thisgroup, only overall malignancy rates were reported. Previous re-views and meta-analysis pointed out to a higher risk of non-Hodgkin lymphoma, estimated from overall malignancy rates,but so far no pooled rates were estimated in childhood-onset SLE.In the only population study of childhood-onset SLE com-paring the malignancy standardized incidence ratio, it was 4.7%(95% CI, 2.6%-7.8%), higher than that for the reference popula-tion. In that study, most events occurred 10 to 19 years afterSLE diagnosis. This study reports age group stratification, sex,and disease duration, varying from less than 1 year to more than20 years. Malignancy presentation was represented by acute leuke-mia and lymphoma. The younger patients presented pancytopeniarelated to leukemia, whereas older patients presented either Hodg-kin or non-Hodgkin lymphoma.15 Very little is known about malig-nancy risks in other pediatric rheumatic diseases, such as juvenileidiopathic arthritis, dermatomyositis, or Sjogren syndrome.14 Inadult-onset SLE, the higher malignancy risk in comparison tothe general population occurs in those younger than 45 years.5Despite all the limitations, malignancy surveillance in childhood-onset SLE is needed. The background risk must be evaluated forsafe treatment with glucocorticoids and immunosuppressive andbiologic treatment. It is indeed a concern from patients, parents,physicians, and other health professionals caring for children withpediatric rheumatic diseases.
