KEYWORDSPsoriasis; herb; random;control; trial; reviewBackgroundPsoriasis is a non-communicable chronic dermatological conditionwithout current cure (1). Believed to be related to immune dys-function, there is strong evidence of genetic involvement in itsdevelopment; however, other potential triggers have also beenproposed (2,3). The most common psoriasis phenotype is vulgaris(plaque type), presenting in around 80% of cases (4,5). Clinically,affected skin features raised and thickened areas, rough in appear-ance, erythematous with scaling on the surface (lesions) (6).Severity is measured most commonly using the psoriasis areaseverity index (PASI) (7). Recently, emphasis has also been placedon improving the sufferer's quality of life (QoL) (8). Historicallythought to be benign, emerging evidence associates psoriasis withthe development of comorbidities, such as cardiovascular diseaseand diabetes (9). Treatments aim to reduce inflammation anddecrease irregular skin cell proliferation (7). Recent discovery ofbiologicaldrugshasseenimprovedantipsoriatictherapeuticeffects; however, they pose risk of adverse effect, which are highin cost and potential for drug tolerance, and/or resistance can limittheir use (10).Chinese medicine has long history of treating psoriasis-relatedsymptoms, with previous reviews identifying 174 different herbshave been used for psoriasis (11-13).
A review by Huang et al. 2012 was conducted on Chineseherbal medicine (CHM) for psoriasis; however, it consisted predom-inantly of literature review of classical CHM usage. While it didinclude a small section on clinical trial evidence, it was not com-prehensive, searching only Chinese databases. The review did notappear to be conducted using a systematic approach and no riskof bias or meta-analyses were presented (14,15). Further outcomeswere not those recommended in international treatment guide-lines and adverse events were unreported (11).A review by Zhang et al. (2014) evaluated oral CHM combinedwithconventionalpharmaceuticaltreatmentusingsystematicapproach. Analysis of 14 randomised controlled trials (RCTs)(n  1/4 1279) concluded CHM combined with conventional pharma-ceutical (acitretin) was more effective than acitretin without add-ition of CHM (n  1/4 626 RR 1.50[1.33, 1.70] I2 1/456%) for improvingpsoriasis (PASI and lesion score) (12).Another comprehensive review of CHM RCT's of CHM bath ther-apy combined with phototherapy for psoriasis vulgaris included 13RCTs (n  1/4 2168). The results indicated combined therapy wassuperior to phototherapy without use of CHM (RR1.25 [1.18, 1.32]I2 1/423%) (13).
Evaluation of clinical evidence from RCTs is recognised as themost rigorous method to evaluate an intervention. Although previ-ous reviews have been conducted for CHM efficacy, there is cur-rently no published comprehensive systematic review comparingoral CHM to placebo for psoriasis vulgaris. In order to evaluate theeffects of oral CHM for psoriasis vulgaris, this systematic review fol-lowed the Cochrane method and reported the results according tothe PRISMA statement (14-16). Study selection and data extractionRandomised controlled trials data, where oral CHM was the inter-vention, were included. Condition was limited to psoriasis vulgaris(plaque) type, of any stage, severity or duration. Only trials pub-lished in English or Chinese were included with no limitations onage or gender. Available journal articles, conference proceedingsand thesis were eligible for inclusion. Multiple arm studies wereconsidered if one of the control arms was placebo. Studies investi-gating psoriasis type other than vulgaris or utilising CHM in a formother than orally were excluded. Any RCTs involving use of aconventional therapy along with CHM were also considered, pro-viding the control group (placebo) received same conventionaltherapy and the therapy was recommended by internationalguidelines. Reviewers (Claire Shuiqing Zhang, Jason Jingjie Yu andShefton Parker) extracted data from RCTs to Excel spread sheet.Data included author, year, country, trial design, number of partici-pants, treatment duration, intervention, methodological quality,outcome measures, study results and adverse events. In situationsof missing data, attempts were made to contact authors of publi-cations to attain the information.Risk of bias was assessed according to the Cochrane collabora-tion's tool by Claire Shuiqing Zhang, Jason Jingjie Yu and SheftonParker for items of sequence generation, allocation concealment,blinding of participants and practitioner, blinding of outcomeassessors, incomplete data, selective outcome reporting and otherbias (if any).
Meta-analysisData were entered into RevMan 5.2 for analysis; clinically, hetero-geneous studies were pooled together and within these meta-analyses, subgroups were formed of studies with similar character-istics. For dichotomous data, relative risk (RR) and 95% confidenceinterval (CI) were reported, while mean difference (MD) and 95%CI were reported for continuous data. Statistical heterogeneity wasexplored through sensitivity and subgroup analyses where the I2statistic was >50%. Where I2 remained >50% a random effectsmodel was employed and a sensitivity analysis conducted.ResultsInitial database search identified 16 020 records containing rele-vant search terms. Outcome measuresTotal effective rate (TER) was the primary outcome measure usedfor all included studies except Dai 2014 study, which only utilisedPASI score. Rates were all based on PASI score change (%) exceptTan 2008, which based primary outcome on percentage of lesionreduction (95% and 50%). The endpoint for PASI score change var-ied for each study. Four studies indicated PASI90 endpoint asgreatest degree of improvement (21,24,25,27), with mid-levelimprovement considered PASI70 (21,24) or PASI60 (27). Zhou 2012required greatest PASI reduction outcome with 95% and Ho 2009study had lowest PASI reduction outcome with 75% (Table 4).Variation also existed for lowest level improvement, withtwo studies evaluating PASI50 (21,24), one PASI25 (27) and onePASI20 (25).Of the studies four specifically reported PASI assessment data(21-23,25), with remaining only reporting effective rates. Two stud-ies presented QoL data, only one utilising the most recognisedQoL outcome for psoriasis, the dermatology life quality index(DLQI) (31) as well as a nondermatologically specific QoL instru-ment, the SF-36 (25). Ho 2009 utilised the psoriasis disability index(PDI), a measure of QoL specific to psoriasis but not commonly uti-lised (32).Potential biological marker changes were reported in two stud-ies (22,23), with no data on relapse rates provided by any studies(Table 4).DropoutsFour of the seven studies reported dropouts, with Ho 2009 studyreporting highest (n  1/4 11), seven of these from the interventiongroup. Risk of bias assessmentTwo studies were assessed as ''low risk'' for sequence generation(25,26) and the remaining assessed unclear. Allocation conceal-ment was assessed as ''low risk'' for Wang 2003 and Zhou 2012studies; all others were unclear. Risk of bias assessments for blind-ing of participants and blinding of personnel for Ho 2009 and Su2010 studies were assessed as ''high risk'', Dai 2014 was unclearfor personnel and remaining four low risk of both categories. Forblinding of outcome assessors all were rated unclear. For incom-plete outcome, of the seven studies, only three (23,26,27) wereassessed as ''low risk'', considering none reported dropouts, finaldata were assumed complete. The other four studies were rated as''high risk'', as they did not include dropout data. There was noevidence to suggest bias so selective outcome reporting was con-sidered ''low risk'' for all studies. Without available published pro-tocols for each study manuscript, data could not be completelyevaluated for selective outcome reporting, as a result it could beargued this criterion should be assessed as unclear (Table 3).
For outcome measure evaluation, Chinese guidelines recommendminimum PASI60 for treatment to be considered successful.Internationally recognised clinical guidelines commonly considertreatment success as PASI75 and that below PASI50 as treatmentfailure (33). As reported in a similar designed systematic review, aPASI reduction of at least 60% was selected as the outcome criter-ion for meta-analysis and pooling of data (12).Of those studies evaluating CHM compared to placebo fourstudies(n  1/4 215)usedanoutcomeofPASI60orabove(22,24,25,27). The four studies were subgrouped in either decoc-tion form (25,27) or capsule/tablet form (22,24). Pooled together,the studies showed no significant effect for CHM intervention (RR:2.74 [0.92, 8.21]), with considerable heterogeneity (I2 1/465) (Figure 2).As separate subgroups, the studies using capsule/pill form still didnot favour CHM (RR: 2.86 [0.01, 805.72]) and heterogeneity increased(I2 1/488). However, the studies using decoction forms did favourCHM (RR: 2.65 [1.57, 4.49]) and the results showed no heterogeneity(I2 1/40).For PASI score change five studies (21-25) (n  1/4 141) were sepa-rated into two subgroups, two using decoction form of CHM(23,25) and three using capsule/tablet form (21,22,24). When thesubgroups were pooled together, they showed effect in favour ofCHM, compared to placebo (MD: 7.00 [10.74, 3.27]); however,heterogeneity of the studies was considerably high (I2 1/498) (Figure 3).Pooled separately the capsule form studies were still in favour ofCHM (MD: 7.41 [8.34, 6.48]) and the heterogeneity significantlydecreased (I2 1/435). However, the two decoction studies failed toshow significance as the confidence interval (CI) was very wide (MD:8.10 [17.89, 1.69]) and the heterogeneity of the studies was veryhigh (I2 1/499). The large difference in the baseline PASI severity scorecharacteristics of participants between the Dai 2014 (24.21+-2.49) andZhou 2012 10.30+-2.65 studies may explain the large CI and resultingheterogeneity.Although not utilising PASI as an outcome, Tan 2008 study data(n  1/4 89) also showed superiority of CHM compared to placebo,where CHM reduced lesions by at least 50% (RR:4.49 [2.40, 8.40]).For QoL via the DLQI, only one study reported sufficient data(25), in which CHM showed improvement in health related QoL ofsubjects (n  1/4 58) (MD: 4.08 [7.56, 0.60]).Known as a key inflammatory factor, tumour necrosis factoralpha (TNF-a) is often targeted by conventional drugs for psoriasis(34). Two studies (22,23), reported data on TNF-a change, showingCHM had effect on its reduction (MD: 4.92 [5.31, 4.53]).
Adverse events data were presented in all but three studies(23,26,27). Gastrointestinal upset was most commonly reported bythree studies (21,22,24). Diarrhoea or loose stools were next com-monlyreported,bythreestudies(21,24,25).Otherreportedadverse events included infection and abnormal liver function(number of events not reported) (22), and one event of menstrualdisorder (24). The placebo control arm of the Ho 2009 study alsoreported adverse events of infection and increased liver enzymes,yet again did not report number of events. SafetyAll studies reported relatively mild or at most moderate sideeffects for oral CHM. Interestingly, from Zhang (1999), althoughconsidered toxic, tripterygium wilfordii reported only mild gastro-intestinal disturbance, relieved by reduction in its dosage. It ishighlighted that the preparation and processing of tripterygium wil-fordii is important for reducing its toxicity (39). In addition toreduce toxic effects on the gastrointestinal tract, researchers andclinicians should consider combining glycyrrhizae radix, which isoften added to reduce toxic effects of other ingredients (40).Ho 2009 utilised two CHM well known for their toxicity, ephedrasinica and radix aconiti lateralis praeparata, the latter's toxicity canbe reduced by special processing (41,42). No included trials included allCONSORT items (44). Such reporting would provide more detail onaccuracy and specificity of each intervention for its target popula-tion. LimitationsDue to variation in the primary outcome for efficacy in the studies,only the outcomes PASI60 or above and general PASI change hadsufficient number of studies to be pooled and evaluated usingmeta-analyses. Remaining studies were evaluated separately. Anyconclusion needs to consider relatively low sample sizes of studiesand identified risk of bias. Three studies (21,23,27) did not detailthe full ingredients of their interventions so some CHM are missingfrom this review.Clinical implication of review findingsThe variety of formulations and their diverse ingredients makesrecommendation on which CHM are of most benefit for psoriasisdifficult. Review does indicate that various forms of administration(decoction, capsule or pill) of CHM may still benefit psoriasis how-ever the degree of the effect is unclear.Formulations of CHM in this review appear safe for use in peo-ple with psoriasis.Future research implications of review findingsThe sample size for included studies was relatively low and likelyinsufficient in power to detect statistical significance for all out-comes. Future studies should utilise larger samples sizes, minimisedesign bias and publish study details that follow the CONSORTand its extension for herbal interventions (44,51). Formulations ofthis review should be explored further to evaluate comparativeefficacy and their constituents' investigated for potential antipsori-atic activity. In vitro and in vivo investigation may provide furtherdata on the effects of oral CHM and provide recommendation onsafe dosage optimisation. While studies reported psoriasis symp-toms according to international treatment guideline recommenda-tions, they did not consistently report on QoL effects. Futureclinical trials should include reliable QoL instruments, validated forpsoriatic populations.Conclusion
When compared with placebo control, CHM's overall effect wasnot significant for PASI60 or above, however for overall PASI scorechange, CHM was superior to placebo. The CHM form of adminis-tration appears to influence the degree of the effect; however, it isunclear which form (decoction or capsule/pill) is superior from thereview. Risk of adverse events from CHM in this review is mild,however long term safety is unknown. Review was limited by lackof consistent intervention and outcome measures, as well as lowparticipant numbers and low quality of some included studies.More high-quality studies are needed which utilise targetedinterventions, compare CHM administration forms, have psoriasis-specific outcome measures, utilise validated QoL instruments andreport long-term follow-up periods.Disclosure statementThe authors report no conflicts of interest.
