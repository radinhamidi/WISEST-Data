Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) and serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs) are first-line pharmaco-logical treatments for depressive disorders, obsessive-compulsivedisorder (OCD), and anxiety disorders (Bose, Korotzer, Gommoll, & Li,2008; Coric et al., 2010; Lepola, Loft, & Reines, 2003; Mendels, Kiev,& Fabre, 1999; Montgomery, Kasper, Stein, Hedegaard, & Lemming,2001; Stein, Wreford Andersen, Tonnoir, & Fineberg, 2007; Sulimanet al., 2015). Atypical antidepressant agents, such as bupropion,mirtazapine, vortioxetine, and vilazodone are also utilized as potentialfirst-line treatments for depressive disorders (Baldwin, Ajel, & Garner,2010; Dell'Osso, Nestadt, Allen, & Hollander, 2006; National Guide-line, 2010; Reinhold, Mandos, Rickels, & Lohoff, 2011; Thase, 2006;Weilburg, 2004; Whooley & Simon, 2000). Although these medicationsare generally well tolerated, adverse effects are common and are oftenthe primary cause of medication discontinuation or dose reduction(National Guideline, 2010). Roughly one-third to one-half of patients
discontinue pharmacological treatment (Lader, Stender, Burger, &Nil, 2004; S. Kasper, 2005) with a quarter of those patients reportedDepress Anxiety. 2017;34:1134-1146.c 2017 Wiley Periodicals, Inc.1134wileyonlinelibrary.com/journal/daside effects to be the reason for their discontinuation (Lader et al.,2004). Meta-analysis has demonstrated that higher doses of SSRIsare modestly more effective than lower doses of SSRIs for depressiveand anxiety disorders as well as OCD (Issari, Jakubovski, Bartley,Pittenger, & Bloch, 2016; Jakubovski, Varigonda, Freemantle, Taylor,& Bloch, 2016). On the other hand, higher doses of antidepressantsare associated with higher rate of dropout due to side effects (Issariet al., 2016; Jakubovski et al., 2016). Although these higher dosesare associated with higher rates of dropout, many side effects ofantidepressants are likely not dose dependent. Therefore, reducingdosing of antidepressant medications often does not eliminate oreven reduce side effects. Better understanding of likelihood of sideeffects of common antidepressant medications and their relationship
to individual agents and antidepressant dosing will help clinicians tomake more evidence-based treatment decisions when side effectsoccur.Antidepressant-induced excessive sweating (ADIES), or hyper-hidrosis, occurs in 5-14% of patients taking antidepressants overall(Mago, Thase, & Rovner, 2013), approximately 10% of patients treatedwith SSRIs, and 5-20% of patients treated with SNRIs (Baldwin et al.,2010; Dell'Osso et al., 2006). Hyperhidrosis can be embarrassing andresult in changes in daily activity. Furthermore, excessive sweatingcan often worsen underlying symptoms of an existing anxiety disor-der, and this combination can contribute to patients discontinuing theirantidepressant medications. Although limited data exist, roughly 2% ofpatients prescribed antidepressants may discontinue the medicationdue to hyperhidrosis (Marcy & Britton, 2005). Furthermore, hyper-hidrosis often appears to be a long-term side effect of antidepres-sant medication that does not remit with continued treatment (Mavis-
sakalian, Perel, & Guo, 2002).Sweating or perspiration is primarily thermoregulatory, and whilethe exact mechanism by which antidepressant medications inducehyperhidrosis is unclear, it is clear that it involves a degree of cen-tral dysregulation of this thermoregulatory mechanism. Several neuro-transmitters affected by antidepressants are important in thermoreg-ulation. Serotonin may exert a direct effect on hypothalamic serotoninreceptors involved in thermoregulation (Marcy & Britton, 2005). Therelationship between serotonin and thermoregulation is supportedby the observation that serotonin syndrome is associated with anincreased core temperature and increased sweating. Impulses fromthe hypothalamus travel through an autonomic pathway to the spinalcord and then to the skin via the sympathetic nervous system. Sweatglands are primarily innervated by cholinergic nerve fibers and there-fore antidepressant effects on adrenergic neurotransmitters (e.g.,norepinephrine, acetylcholine, and dopamine) may also have direct
effects on sweating in the peripheral nervous system (Marcy & Britton,Understanding differences in the risk of hyperhidrosis betweendifferent antidepressant agents may (1) aid in understanding the pri-mary underlying mechanism for this side effect; (2) aid in the selectionof effective treatments for ADIES or hyperhidrosis, and (3) help guideoptimal management of this side effect and underlying psychiatricillness when both occur. Previous meta-analyses have compared therisk of hyperhidrosis among commonly prescribed antidepressantmedications but have relied exclusively on the incidence of hyper-hidrosis within the active medication group (and not normalizing datato the reported incidence of hyperhidrosis in the placebo group).This methodology is extremely vulnerable to confusing the differentmethods of reporting side effects between trials across agents withactual differences between agents. Nonetheless, these previousmeta-analyses have suggested that tricyclic antidepressants are mostlikely to cause hyperhidrosis, while the differences in risk between SRIagents are unclear (Baldwin et al., 2010; Trindade, Menon, Topfer, &Coloma, 1998).The goals of the current meta-analysis are to determine (1) themagnitude of risk of hyperhidrosis associated with individual phar-macological agents; (2) if there is a difference in the risk of hyper-hidrosis between antidepressant classes; (3) if there is a differencein the risk of hyperhidrosis within antidepressant classes; (4) if thereis a relationship between antidepressant dose and the risk of hyper-hidrosis; (5) if there is a relationship between diagnostic indication forthe use of second-generation antidepressants and the risk of hyper-hidrosis; (6) whether pharmacological properties of an antidepressantmedications (e.g., receptor affinities) are associated with the risk ofhyperhidrosis.2METHODS2.1Search strategy for identification of studiesTwo reviewers searched the electronic database of PubMed on June
10, 2016 for relevant studies using the following search: (fluoxetineOR fluvoxamine OR citalopram OR escitalopram OR sertraline ORparoxetine OR bupropion OR trazodone OR desvenlafaxine OR dulox-etine OR mirtazapine OR venlafaxine OR vilazadone OR vortioxetine)OR (serotonin uptake inhibitors) AND (Depressive disorder OR anxi-ety disorders) AND (Humans). The search was limited to randomizedcontrolled trials. The references of relevant systematic reviews andmeta-analyses were searched for relevant published and unpublishedresearch.2.2Selection of studiesThe titles and abstracts of studies obtained by this search strat-egy were examined by two reviewers (C.B. and K.C.) to determineinclusion in this meta-analysis. Any discrepancies were resolved by afinal reviewer (M.H.B.). Inclusion criteria for this study were: (1) ran-domized, double-blind, (2) placebo-controlled clinical trials of (3) asecond-generation antidepressant of interest (fluoxetine, fluvoxamine,citalopram, escitalopram, sertraline, paroxetine, bupropion, trazodone,desvenlafaxine, duloxetine, mirtazapine, venlafaxine, vilazadone, andvortioxetine); targeting (4) clinical symptoms of major depressive dis-order, anxiety disorder, or obsessive compulsive disorder. Exclusioncriteria for this study were: (1) discontinuation studies; (2) head-to-head studies between antidepressants without a placebo control; (3)crossover trials; (4) studies requiring concomitant medications; (5) nontreatment trials.2.3Meta-analytic proceduresData were extracted by independent reviewers (C.B. and K.C.) onspecifically developed spreadsheets on Microsoft Excel. Our primaryoutcome measure was the proportion of patients or subjects in a trial(n) reporting hyperhidrosis as a side effect of medication, comparedto the total number of patients or subjects in a trial (N). Data werealso gathered on trial medication, trial design, maximum daily medica-tion dose, diagnostic indication for second-generation antidepressant,
and other relevant attributes and results of the trials. Any disagree-ment among reviewers was mitigated through discussion and the pro-curement of more information from the study investigators if possible.When agreement could not be attained between the initial reviewers,the senior investigator (M.H.B) resolved all disputes.All statistical analyses were completed in Comprehensive Meta-analysis Version 3 (Biostat, 2015). For our outcome measures of inter-est, proportion of participants experiencing hyperhidrosis was ana-lyzed using pooled RR. For all outcome measures, 95% CIs wereconveyed. A random-effects model for meta-analysis was used. Pub-lication bias was assessed by plotting the effect size against standarderror for each included trial (i.e., funnel plot). In addition, publicationbias was statistically tested by the Egger test. When publication biaswas present, we also present sensitivity analysis where we adjusted forpublication bias using the Duval and Tweedie trim-ad-fill method.For the primary analysis, antidepressant medications were grouped
into the categories of SSRI (fluvoxamine, sertraline, citalopram, fluoxe-tine, paroxetine, escitalopram), SNRI (venlafaxine, duloxetine, desven-lafaxine), and atypical (vilazadone, bupropion, vortioxetine, mirtaza-pine, trazodone). The following questions were examined in meta-analysis: (1) what is the risk for hyperhidrosis for different for SSRI,SNRI, and the atypical medications (analyzed individually) as comparedto placebo (P <0.05 for SSRI, SNRI, and other medications in random-effects meta-analysis using RR as primary outcome); (2) do SSRIs andSNRIs differ in the risk of hyperhidrosis when compared to placebo(test for subgroup differences in random-effects meta-analysis withtrials restricted to SSRIs and SNRIs); (3) do different pharmacologi-cal agents within a medication class (e.g., SSRI and SNRI) differ in riskof hyperhidrosis (test for subgroup difference with trials stratified bypharmacological agent and trials restricted to individual medicationclasses (e.g., SSRIs and SNRIs); (4) is dosing of medications (SSRIs orSNRIs) associated with the risk of hyperhidrosis (metaregression by
dose with trials restricted to each medication class (e.g. SSRIs andSNRIs)); (5) does diagnostic indication for treatment affect the risk ofand (6) are pharmacological properties of antidepressant medications(e.g., receptor affinities) associated with the risk of hyperhidrosis. Allsubgroup analyses were performed using a random-effects model toconductatestforsubgroupdifferences(between-groupheterogeneity2). Metaregression analysis was used to examine the effects of max-imum daily dose of medication used in trials, all daily doses of medi-cation were converted into venlafaxine (imipramine) equivalents usingpreviously described methodology (Bollini et al., 1999). The thresholdfor statistical significance was P <0.05 for the primary analysis, as wellas for all stratified subgroup analyses and metaregression.Our analysis examining the association between pharmacologi-cal properties of antidepressant medication and sleep problems wasbased on a recent meta-analysis performed examining the associa-tion between antidepressant receptor affinities and weight gain. We
obtained data on receptor affinities of different antidepressants frompublished data and from searching the Psychoactive Drug Screen-ing Program Ki database ( Theinhibitory constant (Ki--the concentration of a ligand in which thereceptor is 50% occupied) was used as our measure of binding affin-ity, and consistent with previous analysis in this area, (1) only humandata was used to estimate Ki, (2) all values of Ki were natural log trans-formed and (3) a maximum value of 10000 nM was used for low-affinityinteractions. We utilized random-effects metaregression to examinethe association between log Ki at receptors of interest and the rela-tive risk of hyperhidrosis. We conducted metaregression for all recep-tors where data on affinity is typically available for antidepressants--specifically the serotonin transporter, norepinephrine transporter,dopamine transporter (DAT), H1-histamine receptor (H1), alpha-1 andalpha-2 adrenergic receptors, M3 muscarinic receptor, and 5-HT1A, 5-HT2A, and 5-HT2C serotonin receptors.

Doses of SSRI and SNRIs and diagnos-tic indication for treatment were not associated with differences inrisk of hyperhidrosis. There were also significant differences betweenindividual SSRI medications and risk of hyperhidrosis with fluvoxamineappearing to carry a lower risk, and sertraline and paroxetine carryinga higher risk than other SSRIs. Metaregression suggested that risk ofhyperhidrosis for SRI medications was associated with increased affin-ity for the DAT.Hyperhidrosis is a side effect of SSRI and SNRI medications as wellas several other antidepressant medications (e.g., tricyclic antidepres-sants) not included in this review (Cheshire & Fealey, 2008; Marcy& Britton, 2005; Preskorn, 1995). Although the exact mechanismof ADIES is unclear, it is hypothesized to be related to serotonin'seffects on thermoregulation in the hypothalamus. Increases in extra-
cellular serotonin in the hypothalamus of rats has been demonstratedto induce hyperthermia (Lin, Tsay, Su, & Chueh, 1998). The clearestdemonstration of a similar effect in humans are the symptoms ofserotonin syndrome (Donoghue, Tylee, & Wildgust, 1996). Serotoninsyndrome is a group of symptoms that may occur following use ofcertain serotonergic medications (including antidepressants) or drugs(Donoghue et al., 1996). Serotonin syndrome and its spectrum of symp-toms are a product of hyperstimulation of both central and periph-eralserotoninreceptors,particularly5-HT2A(Boyer&Shannon2005).Diaphoresis and hyperthermia are principle symptoms of serotoninsyndrome along with hyperreflexia, diarrhea, agitation, seizures, andpupillary dilation (Boyer & Shannon 2005).Our metaregression suggests that hyperhidrosis risk among antide-pressant medications was associated with decreased DAT affinityand inhibition. Agents with low-DAT affinity, such as fluvoxamine,(es)citalopram, and venlafaxine were among the least likely to induce
hyperhidrosis. Dopamine, like serotonin, is critical for thermoregula-tion in the hypothalamus (Lee, Mora, & Myers, 1985). Similar to sero-tonin syndrome, diaphoresis and hyperthermia are association withconditions caused by excessive dopamine, such as neuroleptic malig-nant syndrome (Gurrera, 1999). Hyperhidrosis is also a common symp-tom of another disorder associated with dopamine dysregulation-Parkinson's disease (Swinn et al., 2003). In Parkinson's disease (PD),dopamine nerve terminals and consequently DAT are reduced by 30-50% in early PD and to a greater extent in more severe PD (Marek et al.,1996; Rinne et al., 1999). Excessive sweating may be experienced by asmany as two-thirds of individuals with PD and is more common withprogression of the illness (Swinn et al., 2003). Aripiprazole, a partialagonist of the D2 receptor, has also been suggested as a possible treat-ment for ADIES in case reports (Lu, Cullen, Eide, Williams, & Apfeldorf,Although the mechanism of ADIES is unclear and likely multifacto-rial, several treatments exist for patients to reduce excessive sweat-
ing. Terazosin is an alpha-1 agonist that has been demonstrated tosignificantly reduce ADIES in randomized and placebo-controlled tri-als (Ghaleiha, Shahidi, Afzali, & Matinnia, 2013). Terazosin is hypothe-sized to reduce ADIES by decreasing sympathetic tone at the level ofthe hypothalamus (Mago et al., 2013). Other medications, such as ben-zotropine, glycopyrrolate, cyproheptadine, and aripiprazole have beenreportedincasereportsorcaseseriestoimproveADIES.Benzotropineand glycopyrrolate are anticholinergic agents that have been usedeffectively to control ADIES (Kolli & Ramaswamy, 2013; Mago, 2013).Specifically, these agents are believed to reduce excessive sweatingby the direct inhibition of sweat glands that are controlled peripher-ally by cholinergic nerve fibers (Marcy & Britton, 2005; Marek et al.1996; Swinn et al., 2003). Cyproheptadine, which is an antagonist ofacetylcholine, serotonin and histamine has also been utilized to con-trol ADIES (Ashton & Weinstein, 2002). As such we demonstrated that there was areporting bias on hyperhidrosis as a side effect in antidepressant trialsacross medication classes. Additionally, patients and subjects often donot report hyperhidrosis as a side effect. This may be due to embar-rassment, or not making the connection between increased sweat-ing and the medication. Thus either clinical trials employing ratingscales that measure hyperhidrosis (such as the Hyperhidrosis DiseaseSeverity Scale) or use of physiologic measures of sweating in responseto challenge or treatment with antidepressant medications would bemore sensitive to detecting differences between medications (Kowal-ski, Eadie, Dagget, & Lai, ). Furthermore, methods of rating side effects
across trials may differ and thus differences in the risk of hyperhidro-sis between agents may not only represent actual differences in therisk between agents but also possibly systematic differences in assess-ment method. While the gold standard method for determining dif-ferences in side effects between medications would be head-to-headtrials rather than indirect comparison using a placebo, such trials arenotwidelyavailable inthe literature. Likewise, the associationbetweenantidepressant dosing and the risk of hyperhidrosis would also ideallybe examined using fixed-dosage trials.
