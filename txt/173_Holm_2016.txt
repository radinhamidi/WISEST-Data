Omalizumab for atopic dermatitis: case series and asystematic review of the literatureJesper Gronlund Holma, BSc, Tove Agnera, MD, DMSc, Carsten Sanda, MD, DMSc, andSimon Francis Thomsena,b, MD, PhDaDepartment of Dermatology, BispebjergHospital, and bDepartment of BiomedicalSciences, University of Copenhagen,Copenhagen, DenmarkCorrespondenceJesper Gronlund Holm, BSCDepartment of DermatologyBispebjerg HospitalDK-2400 Copenhagen NVDenmarkE-mail: Conflicts of interest: None.AbstractOmalizumab is a recombinant humanized monoclonal antibody targeting the high-affinity Fcreceptor of IgE, registered for the treatment of chronic spontaneous urticaria and severeallergic asthma. We present a case series of nine patients with atopic dermatitis (AD)treated off-label with omalizumab and a systematic review of the existing literature.Patients were selected consecutively from a tertiary dermatological referral center during a5-year period. All patients were treated with omalizumab at a starting dose of 300 mgsubcutaneously every 4 weeks. Systematic literature searches were performed in PubMed,Web of Science, EMBASE, and ClinicalTrials.gov to identify any study (case reports, caseseries, and controlled trials) evaluating the effect of treatment with omalizumab in AD.Based on physicians' assessment, 50% of our patients had a good or excellent responseto treatment with omalizumab; a further 12.5% had a moderate response, while 37.5%experienced no response or deterioration of symptoms during treatment. Treatment was
generally well tolerated. Twenty-six studies with a median of four patients each (range 1-21), comprising 174 patients, were included in the systematic review. Summed over allstudies, a total of 129 patients (74.1%) experienced a beneficial effect of treatment rangingfrom little to complete response. Omalizumab appears to be a safe and well tolerated,however expensive, treatment with some clinical benefit in patients with severe recalcitrantAD. Recommendation for use in clinical practice awaits evidence from larger randomizedcontrolled trials.IntroductionAtopic dermatitis (AD) is a chronic inflammatory pruriticskin disease,1 which is part of the atopic triad involvingalso asthma and allergic rhinitis.2,3 AD has a lifetimeprevalence of 10-20% and primarily affects young chil-dren,4 although it can present at any age.5 AD impactsnegatively on quality of life,6 and optimal disease controlis therefore paramount. Current management of severerecalcitrantADincludessystemictreatmentwithimmunosuppressant drugs such as azathioprine, pred-nisolone, cyclosporine, or mycophenolate mofetil, as wellas methotrexate and ultraviolet radiation. Treatment withbiologic agents is in the pipeline but not yet registered fortreatment of AD.Omalizumab is a recombinant humanized monoclonalantibodytargetingthehigh-affinityFcreceptorofimmunoglobulin E (IgE) approved for the treatment ofmoderate to severe asthma and chronic spontaneous urti-caria.7 Additionally, omalizumab has been used off-labelfor various other diseases involving IgE such as AD with
varying results.8-10 We present a case series of patientswith AD treated with omalizumab in a dermatologicaluniversity department. Furthermore, we systematicallyreview the existing literature identifying studies of ADtreated with omalizumab.MethodsThe patients reported herein were selected consecutivelyfrom the Department of Dermatology, Bispebjerg Univer-sity Hospital (Copenhagen, Denmark), in the periodbetween June 2010 and July 2015. All patients were trea-ted with omalizumab for recalcitrant AD, defined as per-sisting AD despite treatment with >=4 different drugsincluded in the Danish guidelines for AD treatment.Patients were treated with an initial dose of 300 mg every4 weeks, based on the dosage recommended for the treat-mentofchronicspontaneousurticaria.Theclinicalresponse to treatment with omalizumab was evaluatedbased on the physician's assessment as well as the changein the patient's symptomatic complaints reported in eachpatient file representing three grades:11 grade 1, failure oftreatment; grade 2, moderate improvement; and grade 3,successful treatment. In addition, the duration of treat-ment, as well as any adverse effects of omalizumab, wasrecorded.Furthermore,thefollowingvariableswereretrieved from patient records: presence of atopy, sys-temic comorbidities, duration of and previous treatmentof AD, serum total IgE, and filaggrin gene (FLG) muta-tion status.The response to treatment in our patients was com-pared to reports from the existing literature retrievedfrom PubMed, Web of Science, EMBASE, and Clini-calTrials.gov using the search terms: eczema, dermatitis,atopic, omalizumab, and anti-IgE. Cross-references wereretrieved, but this did not identify additional studies.Studies published by August 2015 were included.
ResultsPresent studyNine patients (five males) with a mean age of 49.1 yearswere identified (Table 1). Seven patients had asthma, andall patients had atopy verified by skin prick test or RAST.The mean baseline IgE was 2750 IU/mL (range 1540-12,500).Allpatientsreceivedtopicalcorticosteroids,whereas six patients (77.8%) had received systemic corti-costeroids, and seven (77.8%) had received tacrolimusointment and/or pimecrolimus cream. All patients hadpreviously been treated with cyclosporine, methotrexate,and/or azathioprine. The average number of earlier topi-cal and systemic treatments was 7.4 (range 3-11).Follow-up data on treatment effectiveness was onlyavailable for eight of the nine patients, because treatmentstartup for the ninth patient was too recent to involveevaluation. Four patients (50%) had a good or excellentresponse (successful) to treatment with omalizumab; one(12.5%) had some effect (moderate), while three (37.5%)experienced no response or deterioration of symptoms(failure) during treatment. In general, treatment was toler-ated very well, and no patients reported any adverseeffects except episodic local flare-ups of AD, which isbelieved to be a natural part of active AD. One patientreported shivering and subfebrilia (flu-like symptoms),which is a known side effect of omalizumab.
Literature searchOur literature search identified 26 studies (five case stud-ies, 18 case series, one prospective single-center investiga-tion,andtworandomized,double-blinded,placebo-controlled pilot studies) comprising 185 (results reportedon 174) patients, with a median of four, ranging from 1to 21 patients exploring omalizumab treatment for recal-citrant AD. A total of 129 (74.1%) patients experienceda beneficial effect of treatment ranging from little to com-pleteresponse(Table 2).8,10,12-28Forty-fivepatients(25.9%) from seven different studies reported no or nega-tive effect off omalizumab treatment.9,29-32 Ongoing tri-als (ClinicalTrials.gov) exploring the use of omalizumabin the treatment of AD are being conducted.33 We identi-fied seven trials with two currently posting results (notincluded).DiscussionThis case series of nine adult patients with recalcitrantAD treated with omalizumab showed that 62.5% experi-enced some benefit of the treatment, whereas 37.5% hadno effect or experienced deterioration of symptoms dur-ing treatment. This adds to the growing body of literaturesupporting the use of omalizumab in the treatment ofAD, refractory to more conventional treatments.Two randomized placebo-controlled trials (RCTs) wereidentified17,18 with a combined total of 17 patients treatedwith omalizumab. Notably, Iyengar et al.18 showed anoverall positive effect of the treatment corresponding to a20-50% reduction in the scoring of atopic dermatitis(SCORAD) that, however, did not exceed the effectobserved in the placebo group (approximately 45-80%reduction). Likewise, Heil et al.17 did not show any statisti-cally significant difference in clinical improvement betweenthe treatment and placebo groups, evaluated on investiga-tor's global assessment. Both studies reported a decrease inIgE levels during treatment. Furthermore, one prospectivesingle-center investigation of 11 patients by del Pozo16
reportedastatisticallysignificantdecreaseinmeanSCORAD of 31.5 points from a baseline average of 71.2(range 43-101) and a reduction in mean dermatology lifequality index (DLQI) from 9.36 to 3.72 after treatment. Inaddition, of the 18 retrospective case series, with a range of3-21 patients, 110 of 141 patients (78%) showed a clini-cally positive effect from treatment with omalizumab. Ele-ven patients experienced a deterioration of disease, while20 had no response to treatment. Finally, five case studiesof single patients were reviewed, with two studies reportingexcellent19,20 and two good15,22 response to treatment. Inparticular, Forman and Garrett22 observed a deteriorationof disease during a 4-week interruption of treatment but apositive clinical effect during treatment periods. The studyshowing no response29 reported improvement when dosagewas increased to 375 mg/2 weeks (from an initial dose of150 mg/4 weeks).A report on 73 patients by Hijnen et al.11 foundimprovement ranging from moderate (16%) to successful(77%) in 93% of their study population treated withcyclosporine. Limet al.34 reported a comparable effect (SCORAD decreasedby 25 points) between three patients treated with omal-izumab and one treated with cyclosporine (3 mg/kg body-weight).The observational studies of omalizumab for AD, ingeneral, report more positive results compared to theRCTs, which found no difference in effect from placebo.This could indicate publication bias. Both RCTs and fourof the case series23,24,26,27 use overall effect as a simplifiedmeasure for clinical outcome. In these studies, diseaseseverity was usually stratified to outline different patientgroups assessed at baseline, involving numerous validatedscoring systems including IGA,10,17 visual analogue scale(VAS)24,DLQI,23orSCORAD,14,16,18-21,24-26whileothers report main anatomical skin areas of involve-ment.12,13,22,29 SCORAD was the most widely used, in 13studies. This could argue SCORAD as the recommendedstandardized measure for disease severity. However, with-out a stringent definition of clinical outcome, based on avalidated disease severity measure, results are difficult tocompare and validate. As an example, Caruso et al.19
presented a case study with clinical benefit, based on an80% SCORAD reduction over 5 months, validated as anexcellent response. Contrarily, Zink et al.24 report theresults of their case series of 10 patients as an overallgood effect, referred to SCORAD and VAS improvementduring treatment, without any exact measures. No trendhas been noted between baseline severity measured bySCORAD and clinical outcome. Furthermore, it is notexplicit to what extent patient interviews have been thebasis of treatment evaluation in the reviewed studies. Thesource of evaluation is therefore inconsistent betweenstudies. We did not use a validated scoring system toassess objectively the treatment effect of omalizumab,which is a limitation of our study. However, physicianglobal assessment has previously been used as an informa-tivemarkeroftreatmentresponsetosystemicimmunomodulatory drugs in AD.11Our results agree with Amrol,21 reporting variation inthe clinical improvement between patients. This differencein effect may reflect the clinical heterogeneity of AD.Notably the best effect was seen among the youngestpatients, suggesting a contribution of the treatment to thenatural outgrowing of AD.21 As seen in the case serieswith the lowest mean age8,19,21,26 (<20 years), whichreports good or excellent clinical effect on all but onepatient, who still showed mild improvement. The inter-pretation of improvement as an effect of treatment shouldaccommodate the fact that most patients with AD (75%)have a spontaneous remission of disease before adoles-cence.4 It is therefore impossible to determine whether
the effects reported are a combination of the two, orsolely due to the natural history of AD.The role of FLG mutation status was explored byHotze et al.30, where none of the seven patients carryingan FLG mutation responded to treatment. All eightresponders were non-mutation carriers, indicating thatpatients with primary skin barrier deficiency are lesslikely to benefit from immunomodulatory treatment withomalizumab. This did not agree with our results, wherenone of the two non-mutation carriers had a response totreatment, whereas one mutation carrier had a positiveresponse and one experienced failure of treatment.11
In the reports reviewed, eight different baseline treat-ment regimens were identified, with the initial dosagerangingfrom150 mg/2 weeks(Quistet al.29every4 weeks)8,13,14,18,23,29-32 to 600 mg/2 weeks,15 and withdifferent strategies for increasing dosage throughout treat-ment8,14,29,30 (see comments in Table 2). The averageduration of treatment was 8.1 months, ranging from 3 to29.2 months, with two studies far exceeding averagetreatment duration.25,26 The regimen 150 mg/2 weeks (or300 mg/4 weeks) was used in eight studies and herebythe most widely used. One study increased dosage untilan improvement in dermatological status was seen, with amaximum of 450 mg/2 weeks.23 Yet another followedthe guidelines for treatment of asthma and dosed afterbodyweight and IgE level (0.016 mg/kg/IU).17 Some stud-ies treated with lower than recommended dose andreported clinical benefit from treatment.8,31 Of the fivestudies reporting negative or uncertain positive effects,four were of minimal starting dose,29-32 while Krathenand Hsu9 were at 450 mg/2 weeks.Several studies address the effect of omalizumab inbinding free IgE. Heil et al. reported no significant clini-cal improvement despite great changes in IgE levels.17 Incontrast Zink et al.24 found a relationship between reduc-tion in free IgE levels and clinical improvement. Treat-ment discontinuation led to an increase in IgE withinweeks. The same aggravating pattern was seen for bothSCORAD and VAS.24 No observational pattern was seenbetween baseline IgE and clinical outcome in any otherstudies or among our patients.The studies including the longest follow-up periods alsotended to report more positive results. Amrol21 observedimprovements in DLQI, SCORAD, and VAS, 3 monthsafter initiation of treatment, whereas Velling et al.23 trea-ted patients for 12 months despite showing an improve-ment in AD already after 2 months with a far lowerdosage. This accounts for the possible placebo-inducedclinical improvement in AD suggesting follow-up periodssufficiently long to allow an effect on clinical symptomsfrom treatment with omalizumab to be seen. Only eight studiesreported side effects,8,12,14,17,23-25,28 and none of thesewere severe. The most common side effects were injectionsite reactions14,17,25,28 and headache, dizziness, and nau-sea.8,17,23,24 The most severe reaction was a vasovagalsyncope28 and a hypotensive episode after the first dose.14No studies reported dose-dependent side effects. Com-pared to, for example, cyclosporine11 or azathioprine,35where a sizable proportion experience short- and long-term side effects, omalizumab is a safe and well-tolerated,however expensive, alternative.In conclusion, omalizumab appears to be a safe andwell-tolerated treatment, with some clinical benefit inpatients with severe recalcitrant AD. However, recom-mendation for use in clinical practice awaits evidencefrom larger randomized controlled trials, using a vali-dated disease severity measure such as SCORAD, thoughastartingdoseof300 mg/monthfornolessthan6 months appears sufficient for the evaluation of treat-ment efficacy.
