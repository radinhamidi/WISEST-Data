Chi. E-mails: AbstractThe effects of tofacitinib in treating moderate-to-severe plaque psoriasis were unclear. We aimed to assess the effectsof tofacitinib in treating moderate-to-severe plaque psoriasis. We searched PubMed, Cochrane Central Register of Con-trolled Trials and EMBASE for relevant randomized controlled trials (RCTs) and conducted a systematic review and
meta-analysis. Four RCTs with 2724 participants were included. Compared to placebo, tofacitinib significantly improvedpsoriasis {>=75% reduction in the Psoriasis Area and Severity Index score: 5 mg BID: risk difference (RD) 0.32 [95% confi-dence interval (CI) 0.28-0.35], 10 mg BID: RD 0.51 (95% CI 0.43-0.58); >=90% reduction in the Psoriasis Area and Sever-ity Index score: 5 mg BID: RD 0.19 (95% CI 0.17-0.22), 10 mg BID: RD 0.36 (95% CI 0.31-0.42); Physician's GlobalAssessment 0/1: 5 mg BID: RD 0.31 (95% CI 0.27-0.35), 10 mg BID: RD 0.48 (95% CI 0.44-0.53)} and participants' lifequality [Dermatology Life Quality Index 0/1: 5 mg BID: RD 0.24 (95% CI 0.20-0.2), 10 mg BID: RD 0.36 (95% CI 0.33-0.40)]. Tofacitinib was associated with an increase in minor adverse events [upper respiratory tract infection: 5 mg BID:RD 0.02 (95% CI 0.00-0.03), 10 mg BID: RD 0.02 (95% CI 0.00-0.04); hypercholesterolaemia: 5 mg BID: RD 0.02 (95%CI 0.01-0.04), 10 mg BID: RD 0.02 (95% CI 0.01-0.04)]. In conclusion, tofacitinib may be a treatment option for moder-

Moderate-to-severe plaque psoriasis, defined as Psoriasis Areaand Severity Index (PASI) >10 and Dermatology Life QualityIndex (DLQI) >10,14 was estimated to affect 20% of all patientswith psoriasis.15 Patients with moderate-to-severe plaque psoria-sis may need treatments with phototherapy, systemic non-biolo-gic medications, or biologics.15-18 However, concerns aboutlong-term safety and efficacy, for example the associationC.-M. Materials and methodsWe conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis of random-ized controlled trials (RCTs) on tofacitinib in treating moderate-to-severe plaque psoriasis. We searched the PubMed, CochraneCentral Register of Controlled Trials and EMBASE for relevantRCTs from inception to 6 December 2016. The search strategywas composed of 'psoriasis' and 'tofacitinib/CP690550' with thedetails listed in Table S1 (Supporting Information). The inclu-sion criteria of studies were RCTs which evaluated the efficacyand safety of tofacitinib in treating moderate-to-severe plaquepsoriasis and provided usable data for assessing the followingoutcomes. The primary outcomes included the proportion ofparticipants achieving at least 75% reduction in the PsoriasisArea and Severity Index (PASI) score (PASI 75), at least 90%reduction in the PASI score (PASI 90), and Physician's GlobalAssessment of 'clear' or 'almost clear' (PGA 0/1) at week 12-16.The secondary outcomes were the proportion of participantsachieving a Dermatology Life Quality Index score of 0 or 1(DLQI 0/1) at week 12-16, short-term adverse events (AEs) upto week 12-16, and long-term AEs up to week 52. We specifiedserious adverse events (SAEs) as AEs that resulted in death, werelife-threatening, required hospitalization, resulted in significantdisability or resulted in birth defect. Also, we examined specificAEs that the included RCTs commonly reported.Two authors (CK and CC) independently screened the searchresults and selected relevant RCTs according to the aforemen-tioned inclusion criteria. One author (CK) extracted the datafrom the included trials and assessed the risk of bias of includedRCTs using the Cochrane Collaboration tool.28 Another author(CC) verified the extracted data and the evaluation of the risk ofbias. Discrepancies were solved by consulting a third author(TT).
We used the Review Manager 5.2 (The Nordic Cochrane Cen-tre, The Cochrane Collaboration, Copenhagen, Denmark, 2012)for executing meta-analysis. We expressed continuous outcomesas risk difference (RD) with 95% confidence interval (CI). Werecalculated the extracted data following the intention-to-treatprinciple, that is we considered those with missing outcome dataas treatment failure. The random-effects model was employed inconducting meta-analysis. For significant results, we calculated thecorresponding number needed to treat (NNT) and the numberneeded to treat to harm (NNH) with 95% CI to achieve beneficialand adverse outcomes, respectively. The I2 statistic was calculatedto assess the statistical heterogeneity across the included trials.When the I2 was over 50%, significant heterogeneity was present.28ResultsCharacteristics of included studiesThe PRISMA study flow chart is shown in Fig. 1. Also,the 5 mg BID and 10 mg BID groups did not differ in long-termSAEs (46/886 vs. 48/884, P = 0.82).DiscussionThis study shows both tofacitinib 5 mg BID and 10 mg BID reg-imens are effective in treating psoriasis, with the latter moreeffective than the former. Around one-third of the 5 mg BIDgroup and half of the 10 mg BID group achieved PASI 75 andPGA 0/1 at week 12-16. Nearly one-fifth of the 5 mg BID groupand over one-third of the 10 mg BID group achieved PASI 90 atweek 12-16. About one-fourth of the 5 mg BID group and one-third of the 10 mg BID group achieved DLQI 0/1, representingno or minimal impairment of life quality. In addition to theimprovement of plaque lesions identified in the present study,another post hoc analysis of two included RCTs found tofaci-tinib also effective in treating nail psoriasis.34 Furthermore,tofacitinib has been found effective in treating psoriatic arthritisas well.35
Our study found tofacitinib well tolerated in treating psoria-sis. Only the 10 mg BID group was associated with 9% increasein short-term AEs. Neither the 5 mg BID nor 10 BID groupswere related to short-term SAEs. Specifically, both the tofacitinib5 mg BID and 10 BID groups were associated with a 2% increasein minor adverse events (upper respiratory tract infection andhypercholesterolaemia). A recent study revealed that despite theassociation with a very small increase in the cholesterol levels,the effects of tofacitinib on lipid-related indicators and circulat-ing cardiovascular risk biomarkers do not suggest an increasedcardiovascular risk.36 We also found no dose-response relationbetween tofacitinib and long-term AEs or SAEs.A limitation of this study was the limited number of Asianparticipants in the included RCTs. Asians occupied from 5% to13% of the participants.29-31 However, a Japanese RCT con-firmed the efficacy and safety of tofacitinib in treating moderate-to-severe plaque psoriasis.35 The efficacy of tofacitinib in thisJapanese RCT was better than the included RCTs, with 62.8%and 72.7% of participants on 5 and 10 mg BID achieving PASI75, 37.2% and 54.5% achieving PASI 90 and 67.4% and 68.2%achieving PGA 0/1 at week 16, respectively.35 The higher efficacyin Japanese patients might be attributed their lower mean body-weight (67 kg) than those of included RCTs (83-93 kg).29-31,35An analysis has confirmed bodyweight affects the efficacy oftofacitinib in treating psoriasis.37Except for Russia, tofacitinib is not approved for use in treat-ing psoriasis elsewhere.16 However, the evidence up to date sup-ports the efficacy and safety of tofacitinib in treating moderate-to-severe plaque psoriasis.
