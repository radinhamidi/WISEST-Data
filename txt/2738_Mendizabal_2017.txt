A B S T R A C TObjetives: Melanoma is one of the most frequent malignancies during gestation. However, oncologicaland perinatal management is still challenging. Our first objective is to describe the cases of pregnancy-associated melanoma (PAM) diagnosed in our centre between January-2004 and May-2015. Secondly, toperform a systematic review of the published articles analysing the maternal-perinatal outcomes ofpatients diagnosed with PAM.Design, population and methods: Obstetrical, oncological and perinatal variables were recorded in the caseseries. For the systematic review we include all published articles assessing the obstetric and neonataloutcomes in PAM cases in Pubmed, Web of Knowledge and Cochrane Library. The search was restricted toarticles published in English, between January-2004 and May-2015. Study characteristics, oncologicaland maternal-perinatal variables were recorded in the systematic review.Results: Two patients were found: the first case presents a newly diagnosed metastatic melanoma at 26-weeks of gestation with fatal maternal and neonatal outcome. The second case presents a patient withmetastatic melanoma who got pregnant during her treatment. For the systematic review we found 25articles, providing data from 489 patients. Maternal-perinatal outcomes, including termination ofpregnancy rates, vary depending on the country, gestational age and tumour stage at diagnosis. PAM isusually detected at advanced stages, even with metastasis affecting the placenta and the foetus.Conclusions: When diagnosed at early stages, melanoma does not seem to alter the evolution of gestation,whereas patients with advanced stages of melanoma frequently deliver prematurely, by caesareansection, with lower neonatal weight, higher neonatal morbidity and mortality rates.(c) 2017 Elsevier B.V. IntroductionMelanoma is the most common malignancy in women between25 and 29 years of age [1] and one of the most commonlydiagnosed malignancies during pregnancy, along with breastcancer, cervical cancer, leukaemia and lymphoma [2-5]. Pregnan-cy-associated melanoma (PAM) is reported between 2.8 and 5.0per 100,000 pregnancies [6], and approximately 35% of womenwith melanoma are of childbearing age [2]. The incidence ofmelanoma is rapidly rising and it is foreseen that 1 out of 68Americans born in 2002 will develop an invasive melanoma duringtheir lifetime [7].Most of the publications on PAM are retrospective and analyzethe effect of gestation on the course of melanoma, the maternalprognosis and the survival. However, there are few publicationsevaluating the effect of melanoma on the course of pregnancy andon the obstetrical and perinatal outcomes.The aims of our study are, firstly to describe the maternal andperinatal outcomes of two pregnant patients diagnosed with PAMin our Department; and secondly to perform a systematic review ofthe literature to assess the effect of melanoma on the course ofpregnancy.MethodsStudy populationWe retrospectively collected the records of women who werediagnosed with PAM in the Department of Obstetrics andGynaecology in Gregorio Maranon University General Hospital* Corresponding author at: Maternal Fetal Medicine Unit, Department ofObstetrics and Gynecology, Hospital General Universitario Gregorio Maranon,Calle O'Donnell, 48, Planta 0, Bloque C. 28009, Madrid, Spain.E-mail address: (J. Althoughgestational cancer is defined as malignancies diagnosed duringpregnancy and up to one year postpartum, we excluded the latterbecause we could not evaluate the influence of the malignancy onthe maternal and perinatal results. Two investigators of our centre(a Gynaecologic Oncology specialist and a Maternal-FoetalMedicine specialist) retrieved information concerning the mater-nal and perinatal variables as well as the evolution of pregnancyand postpartum from our hospital electronic database.The PRISMA Statement (Preferred Reporting Items for System-atic Reviews and Meta-Analysis) was followed in this review [8]
Information sources and search strategyWe obtained all the articles related to our topic from theinternational electronic bibliographic databases PUBMED, ISI Webof Knowledge and Cochrane. The articles were identified usingcomprehensive search criteria and a combination of MeSH termsincluding the keywords "pregnancy" and "melanoma". We selectedthe articles published between January 1st 2004 and May 31st2015, matching the period in which we collected data from ourown patients (Fig. 1). The search was limited to studies reported inthe English language. Finally, and once an article was consideredrelevant, the full text was retrieved. The references included in theselected articles were also reviewed for related citations.Study selection and data retrieval Two independent researchersscreened the titles and abstracts obtained to select the mostrelevant articles. If the title and abstract did not provide enoughinformation, the full text was retrieved. Duplicates, letters toeditors, editorials and review articles were excluded. We onlyincluded articles that followed our eligibility criteria. Bothresearchers selected the final studies to be included in the reviewafter applying the eligibility criteria independently. Any conflictbetween researchers was resolved by mutual agreement.Studies were considered eligible for review if they investigatedobstetric outcomes in pregnant women with a diagnosis of PAM,with special reference to maternal, foetal or perinatal outcomes.
We excluded those studies analysing data from pregnant womenwho had had melanoma, but were in complete remission duringpregnancy, as well as those with melanoma diagnosed during thepuerperium. The latter were excluded due to the impossibility ofdetermining the influence of melanoma on the pregnancyoutcomes.Due to the small amount of studies including a large number ofpatients diagnosed with PAM, we also checked articles concerningsingle cases of melanoma where both maternal and foetalprognosis was reflected.We collected the following data from the selected articles:authors, year of publication, type of study, geographic location,number of patients (N), median maternal age at diagnosis, mediangestational age (weeks) at diagnosis, melanoma characteristics,American Joint Committee on Cancer (AJCC) stage, treatmentduring pregnancy (if stated) and data regarding perinatal variablessuch as: gestational age (weeks) at delivery, type of delivery, dataregarding neonatal outcomes, birth weight and congenitalanomalies.ResultsPopulation studyDuring the study period we attended 66,265 deliveries, and wecollected two cases of PAM. The prevalence of PAM in our series is3,1/100.000 deliveries.The cases attended in our centre are described here below.CASE 1A 36 years old pregnant woman with two previous pregnancieswas referred to our hospital during her 26th week of gestation dueto clinical suspicion of chorioamnionitis. At hospital admission, shereported to have been experiencing low abdominal pain, asthenia,epistaxis and gingival bleeding for the last two weeks. Her bloodpressure was 104/45 mmHg. The obstetrical examination and theultrasound were normal. A cardiotocography showed regularcontractions with normal foetal heart rate.
The blood tests showed leukocytosis, thrombocytopenia, andincreased liver enzymes and C-reactive protein. Urinalysis showedpyuria, bacteriuria and positive nitrites.The differential diagnosis included urinary sepsis, chorioam-nionitis, thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura, acute fatty liver ofpregnancy and HELLP syndrome.Due to the suspicion of severe maternal illness, labour inductionwas deemed necessary. We administered corticosteroids for foetalpulmonary maturation, magnesium sulphate for foetal neuro-protection, and antibiotics. We attended a normal vaginal delivery.The newborn weighted 1000 grs. The Apgar score at minutes 1 and5 was 6 and 8, with an arterial blood cord pH of 7.34. The neonaterequired orotracheal intubation and was admitted in the neonatalintensive care unit.The patient presented a postpartum haemorrhage that requireduterotonic drugs and a Bakri balloon placement.The patient remained haemodynamically stable, but theanalyses worsened. An abdominal sonography and a computerizedtomography (CT) showed hepatosplenomegaly and ascitis. Periph-eral blood smear revealed a severe leucoerythroblastic bloodpicture. Bone marrow biopsy showed metastases of a malignantsolid tumour, suggesting melanoma metastases.During the third day of puerperium, the patient had complainedof an itchy cutaneous lesion on her left shoulder that had beengrowing during the last four months (Fig. 2). The lesion was dark,asymmetrical with uneven borders, suspicious of malignancy. Acutaneous biopsy confirmed the diagnosis of melanoma (Fig. 3).The hystopathological study of the placenta showed melanomametastases (Fig. 4).In the subsequent days, the patient presented a worsening inher biological parameters and neurological alterations. A thoracicCT revealed the presence of pulmonary metastases. The patientdied due to multiple organ failure secondary to stage IV malignantmelanoma.The neonate developed hyaline membrane disease, pulmonaryhaemorrhage and necrotizing enterocolitis and died two weekslater. The hystopathological study did not find melanomametastases in the neonate despite the placental invasion.

DiscussionMelanoma is one of the five most frequent malignancies duringgestation, representing between 8% and 25% [5,19-21] of allmalignancies diagnosed during pregnancy. The prevalence of PAMin our centre (3.1/100.000) is consistent with the literature (2.8 to5.0/100.000 pregnancies [6]).We present two cases of special interest. The first one shows achallenging situation, since the differential diagnosis included awide spectrum of illnesses in a patient without a previousdiagnosis of melanoma, resulting in maternal and neonatal death.The second case presents a patient with metastatic melanomadiagnosed prior to pregnancy who received no treatment duringpregnancy and delivered a healthy neonate after 34-weeks ofgestation.We also present a systematic review of the articles published inthe last 10 years including maternal, perinatal and oncologicaloutcomes in PAM.The articles that had the largest amount of patients are fromcountries with a high prevalence of melanoma. Lee et al. [22] whosestudy was performed in Australia and Eibye et al. [23] from Denmarkstate that melanoma is the most common malignancy associatedwith pregnancy. The incidence of melanoma in Caucasianpopulationis inversely related to the country's distance from the equator [24].New Zealand and Australia have the highest incidence of worldwide[25]. In Europe, the highest incidence inwomen is found in Denmark,Iceland, Norway, and Switzerland [26].It is noteworthy that even though the articles were publishedwithin the last ten years, the cases were registered between 1970
and 2015, which should be taken into account due to the advancesin oncological and obstetrical treatments in the last 45 years.Nowadays, USA patients with melanoma have a 5-year survivalrate of 91.5% when the disease is detected in the early stages.To diagnose PAM is challenging since the changes that happento a melanocytic lesion could be misdiagnosed as physiologicpregnancy-related changes (hyperpigmentation or enlargement ofthe melanocytic lesions due to weight increase) and thiscontributes to a delayed diagnosis [10].Melanoma treatment should include tumour resection, sentinelnode biopsy and lymphadenectomy if necessary, following thestandard procedures outside pregnancy [27]. Technetium-99instead of Blue dye is recommended during pregnancy because,although rare, there is a 1:10,000 risk of anaphylactic reactionassociated with isosulfan blue, which could threaten the life ofboth mother and foetus. The alternative, methylene blue dye, isknown to be teratogenic and contraindicated in pregnancy [28].The literature review shows high heterogeneity in the treatment ofadvanced melanoma. Unfortunately, the two studies with thelargest number of cases [10,13] do not give details of theoncological treatment(s) administered. Indeed, it varies withgestational age, presence of metastases, local legislation, hospitalprotocols and maternal preferences. Melanoma management mayinclude no pharmacological treatment, TOP, preterm labourinduction or the administration of chemotherapy, radiotherapyor immunotherapy.It has long been believed that hormonal and immunologicalchanges associated with pregnancy worsen the prognosis andaccelerate melanoma progression [29-33]. Pigmentation changesthat occur during pregnancy, contraceptive treatment or hormonereplacement therapy [34], along with the detection of oestrogenreceptors in melanoma cells [35,36], generate doubts about thepossible influence of pregnancy on the evolution of the disease.However, many authors defend a similar prognosis of melanoma inpregnant and non-pregnant women at a similar stage of disease[37-41].On the other side, very few recent articles analyze the influenceof melanoma on obstetrical and perinatal results. With theexception of the article published by Pages et al. [12] (whichanalyses pregnant women with advanced melanoma), most of thearticles included in our review did not find differences inobstetrical and perinatal outcomes in PAM when compared withthe general population. This can be related to the fact that most ofthese articles analyze patients with early stages of melanoma. Inour review, patients with advanced stages have more prematuredeliveries with lower neonatal weight, higher C-S rate and moremiscarriages, consistent with the data presented by Lee et al.[22,42]. In fact, they found that women with cancer have higherprobabilities to deliver in a tertiary centre, higher amount ofprenatal hospitalizations, induced deliveries, preterm births,elective C-S and low birth weight compared with women withoutcancer.Transplacentary metastatic dissemination is rare, but melano-ma is the malignancy that metastasizes more frequently to theplacenta [42]. In our results, placental metastases were present in6 patients with advanced disease. This represents 1.22% of all thepatients included in this review, and 16.66% of the patients withmetastatic disease. One case [18] presented neonatal metastases(2.77%). The foetal risk of melanoma metastases with placentalinvolvement is 16.7%, similar to that described by Alexander et al.[42] who stated a risk of foetal and placental involvement of 22%.However, they found a 83% of neonatal mortality and, in ourreview, the only case with neonatal melanoma metastases had asubsequent tumour remission while on palliative treatment.To conduct our systematic review, we selected the articles thatanalyzed the obstetrical and materno-foetal results of patients
with PAM. We only included patients with active melanoma duringpregnancy, excluding those diagnosed during the first or secondyear after delivery. However, some authors also include this periodsince they consider that the disease was probably already presentduring pregnancy [9,10,13].We also excluded 620 patients from the Langergaard study [13]who had melanoma diagnosed before pregnancy, even thoughsome of these patients had complete remission before pregnancy.The analysis of the variables studied was also limited by theheterogeneity of the articles, since some authors did not describethe variables we included in our review, and when included, theywere not always similarly reported.Furthermore, the three articles that include most of the patients[9,10,13] (92.11% of the cases presented in Table 1), do not describethe pregnancies terminated before the twentieth week ofgestation. This could create a selection bias as we do not knowthe amount of miscarriages, foetal malformations or TOPs thatcould be related to the melanoma, which could underestimate theadverse obstetrical results. In fact, Eibye et al. [23] published that19.9% of the patients with PAM suffered a miscarriage duringpregnancy, of which 12.5% were TOP.Despite these limitations, our study presents the first system-atic review of the literature analysing the effect of melanoma onobstetrical and neonatal outcomes, by gathering data from 489patients.Conclusion
Melanoma is one of the most common malignancies diagnosedduring gestation. Diagnosis is usually made at early stages andpatients with PAM should be controlled in a tertiary centre with amultidisciplinary approach. Surgical treatment should not bealtered by the pregnancy, but the treatment of the metastaticdisease should be individualized. Prognosis of melanoma does notseem to be altered by pregnancy. However, obstetrical andmaterno-perinatal outcomes seem to vary depending on the stageof the disease. Advanced melanoma is associated with a risk ofprematurity, low birth weight and higher rates of C-S and neonatalmorbidity and mortality.Transplacental transmission of malignant melanoma to thefoetus is very rare and has been described in women with distantmetastases.With the growing incidence malignancies during pregnancy, itwould be interesting to create an international database in orderto minimize differences in the recorded data, to compare thetreatments used and to evaluate globally the evolution of themalignancies and the obstetric and materno-perinatal outcomes.Disclosure of interestsWe have read and understood the BMJ policy on declaration ofinterests and declare the following: noneEthical approvalConsent to publish has been provided for the two casesrepresented in the paper.Contribution to AuthorshipEM and JDL conceived and designed the study, extracted data,performed the systematic review and wrote the paper, NR, LJ, PP,PR, VO and SL analyzed the data and contributed analysis tools.FundingThis study received no specific funding or grant.Appendix A.
