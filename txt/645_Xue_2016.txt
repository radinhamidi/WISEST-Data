IntroductionMycosis fungoides (MF) is a relatively rare, extranodal, non-Hodgkin's lymphoma with primary cutaneous involvement, which
is generally indolent in behavior. It is a low-grade cutaneous T-celllymphoma (CTCL) characterized by small to medium sized cells* Corresponding author at: Department of Dermatology, Peking Union MedicalCollege Hospital, Peking Union Medical College and Chinese Academy of MedicalSciences, 1 Shuaifuyuan, Wangfujing, Beijing 100730, China.E-mail address: (Y. Liu).with cerebriform nuclei that usually express mature peripheralCD4+ T-helper cell immunophenotypes and infiltrate the skin char-acteristically with epidermotropism [1]. The incidence rate of MFhas been reported to be 4.1/1, 000, 000 by a SEER (Surveillance, Epi-demiology and End Results) based study which used data from 16registries in the United States to analyze 3884 cases of CTCL from2001 to 2005 [2]. This makes MF  the most common cutaneous T-cell lymphoma (CTCL) subtype (54%). The risk of MF is associatedwith age, gender and race, with relatively higher prevalence amongthe elderly, blacks and males [3]. Occupational exposure, geneticdisposition, radiation, and viral infection have been speculated as 2016 Elsevier B.V. All rights reserved.etiologic factors in relation to MF, while corresponding pathologicalmechanism is yet to be elucidated [4].The appropriate treatment of MF  is primarily based on prog-nostic factors, Tumor-Node-visceral Metastases-Blood (TNMB)classification, and overall clinical stage at diagnosis [5]. The majorprognostic factors reported by a large cohort of 1502 patentsincluded increased age, male sex, increased lactate dehydrogenase(LDH), large-cell transformation and folliculotropic MF. These fac-tors all reduce survival and increase the risk of disease progression(RDP). Positive prognostic factors include hypopigmented MF, MFwith lymphomatoid papulosis, and poikilodermatous MF. These
show improved survival and reduced RDP [6]. The latest versionof staging and classification was updated on 2008 by the WorldHealth Organization (4th edn) and revised in 2010 [7,8].MF constitutes the protracted clinical evolution of patches,plaques, and tumors with a wide spectrum of potential clinicalmanifestations. MF patients are usually treated with a multimodalapproach. However, many patients go on to develop resistanceagainst at least one type of treatment or have relapsing symp-toms after a period of improvement. The treatment of relapsed ortherapies available. In the past decade, clinical success has beenreported using photodynamic therapy (PDT) as a possible alterna-tive non-invasive, target-specific therapy to treat MF. PDT utilizesreactive oxygen species generated by light-activated photosensi-tizer to oxidize essential cellular components and thereby causesapoptosis and necrosis restricted to target sites.In 1994, Svanberg et al. reported first successful case of using
topical -amino levulinic acid (ALA)-based PDT to treat an MFplaque [9]. In 1994, Boehncke et al. found that the capability of PDTto inhibit proliferation of T cells was similar to that of PUVA bothin vitro and in vivo [10]. In 1995, Rittenhouse-Diakun et al. demon-strated that malignant CTCL cells with increased CD71 expression, amarker of relative intracellular iron deficiency, preferentially accu-mulated endogenously generated protoporphyrin IX (PpIX) andwere highly susceptible to ALA-PDT [11]. In 2001, Gad et al. showedan increase in caspase-3-like activities and increase in the percent-age of DNA fragmentation in malignant T cells following ALA-PDT,which indicated that ALA-PDT could induce apoptosis and caspaseactivation [12]. Orenstein et al. observed different patterns of PpIXfluorescence in patients with early and advanced stages of the dis-ease using 5-aminolevulinic acid topical application: in stage I MFtoirradiation (580-720 nm), while in thick stage III lesions, PpIXfluorescence disappeared after an additional 10-15 min irradiation[13]. All these studies suggest the ability of PDT to selectivelydestroy the malignant lymphocytes in MF  lesions.2. MethodWe  performed a systematic review of literature on May 25,2016 and updated the search on September 10, 2016. We  searchedkeywords "mycosis fungoides" and "photodynamic therapy" inPubMed (National Library of Medicine), Google-Scholar, Scopus,to be eligible for inclusion if it met the following criteria: (1)therapy-resistant mycosis fungoides; (2) clinical PDT application;(3) English language. Review articles were excluded. A total of 20papers were included in this review.2.1. ALA-photodynamic therapyIn 1990, Kennedy and colleagues discovered and reported theporphyrin precursor ALA. This finding was an important milestonein the development of PDT in dermatology, as the small moleculescould easily penetrate the epidermis due to relatively low molec-
ular weight [14]. ALA can be metabolized into photoactivatableporphyrins by most cells as part of the heme cycle, and the levels ofintracellular accumulation are relatively higher inside altered cellsconstituting the diseased tissue [15].The first case using ALA-PDT for successfully treating MF lesionswas reported in 1994 [9], and since then many reports of success-ful ALA-PDT for patch/plaque stage MF  have been published withgood results. However, a number of limitations hinder the furtherapplication of ALA in PDT. For instance, because of poor tissue pen-etration, ALA cannot reach tumor tissue thicker than 3 mm  in orderto induce sufficient necrosis. Moreover, ALA requires an incubationperiod between application and light exposure to be metabolicallyconverted into the active PpIX, which causes the patients to waitfor a long time [16].2.2. MAL-photodynamic therapyA derivative of ALA, methy ester methyl aminolevulinate (MAL),is reported to have increased lipophilicity and deeper skin penetra-tion when compared with ALA. This allows a shorter incubationtime and results in a better selectivity toward tumor cells [17].In 2013, Ariel et al. reported the first case of tumor-stage MFlesions that responded successfully to topical MAL-PDT treatment[18]. MAL-PDT is therefore considered as an alternative option inselected cases of MF and even in tumor-stage MF.2.3. Light sourcescontinuous-wavelength light sources (red, blue, white, or green),lasers, incoherent light sources and so on. To obtain desired thera-peutic effects, the photosensitizing porphyrins should be activatedby light of an appropriate wavelength. The porphyrins or relatedphotosensitizers exhibit a very typical absorption spectrum withthe highest peak at approximately 405 nm in the blue wavelengthregion, the so-called Soret-band, and several Q-bands with anabsorption peak at 635 nm in the red wavelength region [19]. It
has been reported that compared with the blue spectrum, the redlight shows better tissue penetration [20].Moreover, laser and incoherent light sources can be used in PDT.The advantage of laser is shorter exposure time compared withincoherent light sources, though the laser cannot ideally matchthe porphyrin absorption spectrum. The development of intensepulsed light (IPL) devices (e.g. PDT 1200l, Waldmann Medizintech-nik) enables application of incoherent light sources for PDT, withan emission spectrum ranging from 500 to 1300 nm. At present,the preferred light in topical PDT is incoherent light sources withwide illumination fields because this can accomplish the simul-taneous irradiation of larger areas. Several studies have reportedsuccessful treatments of mycosis fungoides with IPL device for PDT[21,22]. In addition, the LED system of incoherent light sources (e.g.AktiliteTM, Galderma, Alby- sur-Che 'iran, France; Omnilux PDTTM,Phototherapeutics, Altrincham, UK) is a promising choice becauseit emits only a narrow band of wavelengths that perfectly matchesthe absorption spectrum of porphyrins.In addition, there is another type of photosensitizer for mycosisfungoides treatment under investigation. The silicon phthalocya-nine Pc4, a second-generation photosensitizer, allows deeper tissuepenetration of red light (= 675 nm;  = 2 x 105) when compared toPpIX or photofrin whose peak absorption in the red region is at630-635 nm [23]. Pc4 accumulates in mitochondria to damage theanti-apoptotic proteins Bcl-2 and Bcl-xL, releases cytochrome c intothe cytosol, and induces cellular apoptosis [24]. ResultWe  found and reviewed 20 published articles to show that PDTwas useful as a skin-limited treatment in localized forms of MFthat responded poorly to regular treatments. Reported cases of suc-cessful treatment of relapsed or refractory MF  plaques using PDTare summarized in Table 1. These studies included a total of 81patients previously treated with topical steroids, photochemother-apy, nitrogen mustard and so on. These patients had 117 relapsed orrefractory MF  lesions which were treated with PDT, 78.6% (92/117)patch-stage MF, and the remaining 6%(7/117) of which were tumor-stage MF.Since the first case was reported in 1994 [26] and through 2006,20% 5-ALA was used as the photosensitizer for PDT with 4-16 hocclusion time. There were 9 studies using ALA, and 11 studies usingMAL. 5-ALA has been substituted by MAL [30] with 3 h occlusiontime. MAL has increased lipophilicity and resulted in deeper skinpenetration. The majority of these studies used long wavelengthsaround 600 nm by various light sources (red light, laser, and inco-herent light), which could penetrate deeper and be more effectivefor the treatment of thicker lesions. The frequency of the treatmentdescribed the need for multiple sessions. Number of sessions wasassociated with total light doses, type of lesions, and sensitivity to
the treatment. Repeated sessions of PDT seemed to be mandatoryin treating MF.The overall complete response (CR) rate of the 117 MF lesionswas 63.2% (60.9% in plaques, 72.2% in patches, and 71.4% in tumors,respectively). In theory, the correlation between CR rate and infil-trate depths of lesions should be negative. However, the publicationbias could account for these observations. Edstrom et al. observed alower rate of response in tumor stage lesions, though all the tumorlesions showed regression [31]. Follow-up period of each studyvaries from 3 months post-treatment to 87 months. Therefore, PDTcan be a valuable treatment option for relapsed or refractory MFat different clinical stages, especially for patch or plaque-stage,although optimal parameters of PDT have not yet been defined.4. DiscussionFor mycosis fungoides, multiple treatment modalities such asso additional localized therapies may be required. PDT is a recentlyintroduced therapeutic modality that has been proven effective andwell tolerated in patients with localized relapsed or refractory MFlesions, even in tumor-stage. The advantages of PDT include excel-lent cosmetic results, noninvasive nature, excellent selectivity, lowrisk of toxicity accumulation, and negligible generalized photosen-sitivity, and low carcinogenic potential. These advantages makeMF, such as relapsed or refractory MF, or even in sensitive areassuch as the face and neck [34].In conclusion, PDT is a promising treatment option for relapsedor refractory mycosis fungoides. Despite the relative simplicity ofthe technique, established treatment protocols have not yet beenoptimized for PDT in MF  with respect to ALA or MAL applicationtime, light dosimetry, delivery, frequency of treatment, and numberof sessions. Therefore, larger clinical studies are required and theoptimal treatment regimens need to be clarified.
