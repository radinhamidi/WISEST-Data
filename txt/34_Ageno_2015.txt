Nadroparin for the prevention of venous thromboembolismin nonsurgical patients: a systematic review and meta-analysisWalter Ageno1 * Jacqueline Bosch2 * Michel Cucherat3 * John W. Eikelboom2 Springer Science+Business Media New York 2015AbstractAnticoagulant thromboprophylaxis with low
molecular weight heparin is widely used in nonsurgicalsettings. To obtain best estimates of the effects ofnadroparin for the prevention of venous thromboembolism(VTE) in nonsurgical patients, we conducted a systematicreview and meta-analysis. Data sources were Medline,Embase, and Cochrane Library supplemented with con-ference abstracts, without language restrictions. Selectioncriteria were randomized controlled trials with nadroparinat prophylactic dose in adult nonsurgical patients. Mainefficacy outcomes were major VTE (the composite ofsymptomatic deep vein thrombosis, symptomatic pul-monary embolism, asymptomatic proximal deep veinthrombosis and VTE-related death) and symptomaticVTE. The main safety outcome was major bleeding. Weexpressed treatment effects as risk ratios. Ten studies (4vs. placebo or no treatment, 4 vs. UFH, 1 vs. fonda-parinux and 1 vs. warfarin) enrolling a total of 7658patients were included. In comparison with placebo,nadroparin reduced major VTE by about one-half (RR0.48, 95% CI 0.24-0.97) with a consistent effect onsymptomatic VTE (RR 0.69, 95% CI 0.46-1.05) and noincrease in major bleeding (RR 1.51, 95% CI 0.40-5.79).In comparison with other pharmacological prophylaxis,nadroparin was similarly efficacious for prevention ofmajor VTE (RR 1.14, 95% CI 0.63-2.10) and symp-tomatic VTE (RR 1.10, 95% CI 0.51-2.35) and producedsimilar effects on major bleeding (RR 0.60, 95% CI0.25-1.50). Five studies were open label, and for three ofthese the adjudication method was not described or notblinded. In nonsurgical populations at risk of VTE,nadroparin reduced VTE by about one half compared withplacebo or no treatment and appeared similarly effectiveand safe as other prophylactic anticoagulants.KeywordsLow molecular weight heparin  Nadroparin VTE prevention  Major bleeding  Medical patients Nonsurgical patients
InroductionVenous thromboembolism (VTE) is a prevalent andavoidable complication in nonsurgical patients with pre-disposing risk factors. International guidelines recommendanticoagulant prophylaxis with LMWH in high risk acutelyill medical patients and in other nonsurgical populations[1].Nadroparin calcium was the first LMWH approved foruse and is the second most widely used LMWH world-wide. However, the available data with nadroparin forVTE prevention in nonsurgical patients are limited. We attempted to identify all randomized, controlled trials,irrespective of language, that described anticoagulant pro-phylaxis with nadroparin in nonsurgical patients.We searched the literature to identify relevant publishedand unpublished trials, including trials only presented asabstracts. We searched Medline, Embase and the TheCochrane Controlled Trials Register through to July 2015and the American Society of Hematology and InternationalSociety of Thrombosis and Haemostasis congress pro-ceedings using the ISI Web of Knowledge database.To identify any additional studies, we searched thefollowing databases: Web of science, Core YOUR Inspec Archive--Science Abstracts; International Pharmaceutical abstracts(EBSCO); and ScienceDirect.We also searched the internet, including Medscape, theUS Food and Drug Administration (FDA) Web site, andthe Web sites maintained by the drug manufacturers.We hand searched reference lists of potentially eligiblestudies identified from our search of Medline.Study selectionTwo reviewers independently assessed eligibility of studiesbased on pre-specified inclusion and exclusion criteria.Questions or disagreements were to be resolved by dis-cussion and consensus.We included a study if it was a randomized, controlledtrial that compared nadroparin at prophylactic dose with acomparator (active treatment or placebo/no treatment) inadult patients not undergoing surgery and if it reportedpulmonary embolism (PE) and/or deep-vein thrombosis(DVT) of the lower extremities. Trials for the prevention ofclotting during hemodialysis or for the treatment of VTE orfor the prevention of pregnancy-related complications (e.g.,miscarriage) unrelated to VTE were excluded.The dose of nadroparin was considered to be prophy-lactic if administered at a total daily dose of 5700 AXa IUor less. Trials at higher doses were excluded. We includedstudies regardless of treatment duration.Study data extraction
This systematic review and meta-analysis followed themethods recommended by Cochrane and the PRISMAguidelines [2].Data regarding inclusion criteria and treatment type andduration were abstracted, when available, from eachindividual study. We extracted data on the followingtreatment efficacy outcomes during the treatment period:any PE, fatal PE or VTE-related death, symptomatic DVT,asymptomatic proximal DVT, death due to any cause andmajor bleeding. We accepted the reported definitions ofmajor bleeding and did not attempt to reclassify theseevents.For binary outcomes, numerators were defined as thereported event count and denominators were based on theintent-to-treat (ITT) population (as reported) or on the as-treated population for major bleeding if different.Study quality assessmentWe conducted a risk of bias assessment using the criteriarecommended by The Cochrane Collaboration [3]. Tworeviewers carried out ''risk of bias'' assessments, withconsensus.To assess quality, we evaluated studies for potential biasrelated to random sequence generation, the adequacy ofallocation concealment, blinding and blind assessment,incomplete outcome reporting, performance of the primaryefficacy analysis according to the ITT principle, as well asother potential sources of potential bias.Individual criteria were scored as ''high risk'' if absent,''low risk'' if present, or ''unclear risk'' if absence orpresence was ambiguous and reported these assessments ina ''risk of bias'' table.We defined a study with a low risk of bias as fulfillingall six of the above criteria and having no major (fatal)flaws. We judged the likelihood of fatal flaws following theCochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interven-tions recommendations [3], including a dropout rate greaterthan 50% at the follow-up measurement.
Data synthesis and analysesAll statistical analyses were carried out separately forstudies with an active comparator and for studies withplacebo or no treatment as control.The treatment effect was measured by the risk ratio(RR). We pooled the RRs on the log scale through thegeneric inverse variance approach, using both the fixed-effect and the random-effects models.In the case of a trial reporting no events in both groups,the trial was not taken into account for that outcome sinceneither the relative risk nor the corresponding variancecould be calculated.To the furthest extent possible and depending on howthe data were reported, we carried out analyses on an ITTbasis, attempting to include all participants randomized toeach group in the analyses, except for major bleedingwhich was analyzed on an as-treated basis.TrialStudied treatmentControl treatmentPatientsPlannedtreatmentdurationSystematic search ofVTESample sizenadroparin/comparatorFONDACAST, 2013Nadroparin 2850 anti-factor Xa IUFondaparinux 2.5 mg(1.5 mg in patients with acreatinine clearancebetween 30 and50 ml min-1)Patients requiring rigid or semi-rigidimmobilization for isolated non-surgical below-knee injuryAt least21 days, upto 45 daysCompression ultrasoundand clinical examination584/586 (ITT)

We identified 223 potentially relevant records from theliteraturesearchthroughMEDLINE,EMBASE,andmanual bibliography searches after exclusion of duplicatecitations. We excluded 189 abstracts during the initialscreening, using the predefined inclusion criteria. Theremaining 34 records were retrieved for more detailedreview and screening. Of these, ten trials met the eligibilitycriteria and were included in the review and meta-analysis[5-14]. Table 1 shows the main characteristics of theincluded studies. Reasons for exclusion are summarized inFig. 1. In one included study [10], VTE outcomes werereported but could not be categorized according to pre-specified definitions and no major bleeding was reported ineither arm. As a result, this study did not contribute to thestatistical analyses for any outcome.Study qualityQuality assessment is summarized in Table 2. All trialswere randomized, but the method for generating the allo-cation sequence was adequately reported in only two of tentrials. Seven trials reported concealed treatment allocation.Five studies were double blind. Among the five otherstudies, two reported an adequate blind outcome assess-ment. All trials except two provided a description of patientwithdrawals.
