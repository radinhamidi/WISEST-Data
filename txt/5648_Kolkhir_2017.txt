Background and objective: Numerous autoimmune diseases (AIDs) have been linked to chronic spontaneous ur-ticaria (CSU). Here, we provide the first extensive and comprehensive evaluation of the prevalence of AIDs in pa-tients with CSU and vice versa.Methods: A Pubmed and Google Scholar search was performed to identify studies reporting the prevalence of var-ious AIDs in CSU and vice versa published before April 2017.Results: The prevalence of individual AIDs in CSU is increased (>=1% in most studies vs <=1% in the general popula-tion). AIDs with relatively high prevalence in the general population are also quite common in CSU patients,whereas those with low prevalence remain a rare finding in CSU. The rates of comorbidity in most studieswere >=1% for insulin-dependent diabetes mellitus, rheumatoid arthritis (RA), psoriasis and celiac disease (CD),>=2% for Graves' disease, >=3% for vitiligo, and >=5% for pernicious anemia and Hashimoto's thyroiditis. Organ-spe-cific AIDs are more prevalent in CSU than systemic (multiorgan or non organ-specific) AIDs. N2% of CSU patientshave autoimmune polyglandular syndromes encompassing autoimmune thyroid disease (ATD) and vitiligo orpernicious anemia. Antithyroid and antinuclear antibodies are the most prevalent AID-associated autoantibodiesin CSU. N15% of CSU patients have a positive family history for AIDs. The prevalence of urticarial rash in AID pa-tients is N1% in most studies. This rash is more prevalent in eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis, ATD,systemic lupus erythematosus, RA and CD.Conclusions: CSU patients have an increased risk of AIDs, especially adult female patients and those with a posi-tive family history and a genetic predisposition for AIDs, who should be screened for signs and symptoms of AIDs.(c) 2017 Elsevier B.V. 1. IntroductionChronic spontaneous urticaria (CSU) is a mast cell-driven diseasecharacterized by the development of wheals, angioedema (AE), orboth for N6 weeks [1]. The etiopathogenesis of CSU is thought to beassociated with autoimmune mechanisms [2]. Numerous autoim-mune diseases (AIDs) have been linked to CSU [3], most of whichare complex chronic inflammatory disorders that involve a plethoraof cells and mediators, affect many tissues and organs, and causesubstantial morbidity and mortality, mostly in women aged 40 to50 years [4,5].AIDs can be classified as systemic (multiorgan or non organ-specific)and organ-specific [6,7]. Some systemic AIDs are primary, i.e. they occuralone, while secondary AIDs develop during the course of other AIDs,e.g. Sjogren's syndrome in patients with rheumatoid arthritis or system-ic lupus erythemasosus. The etiopathogenesis of AIDs involves manyfactors that include, but are not limited to, predisposing genes, epige-netic alterations, hormonal effects, and environmental triggers, leadingfrom premorbid B and T cell dysregulation to the development of clini-cally evident disease [8].Recently, we systematically reviewed the association of CSU andautoimmune thyroiditis [9] and of CSU and systemic lupus erythe-matosus [10], and we found that CSU is strongly linked to both ofthese AIDs. Here, we provide the first extensive and comprehensiveevaluation of the prevalence of AIDs in patients with CSU and viceversa.
2. MethodsA Pubmed search was performed to identify studies published be-fore April 2017 with the keywords "urticaria" and "chronic urticaria"in combination with the terms that are listed in Table 1. No languagefilter was applied. We also examined the reference lists of the re-trieved articles and we searched Google Scholar with the same key-words to identify additional studies. In total, 64 studies wereincluded in this systematic review. Most of these studies looked atwhether or not the patients included had AID at the time they wereassessed (point prevalence). A few studies reported whether pa-tients had a history of AID or not. In some studies, it was not specifiedif the patients still had the AID at the time they had their urticaria.Some studies evaluated the prevalence of more than one AID. The se-lection process of relevant studies is summarized in Table 1. In stud-ies identified, we did a search for the prevalence of autoantibodies(antinuclear, rheumatoid factor, anti-dsDNA, anti-parietal cell, anti-transglutaminase, anti-smooth muscle, anti-mitochondrial, anti-SSA, anti-SSB, anti-Sm, anti-cardiolipin) and a family history ofAIDs. The term "urticarial rash" was used for studies where CSUwas not clearly differentiated from acute urticaria, chronic inducibleurticaria and/or urticarial vasculitis.3. Results3.1. All investigated autoimmune diseases occur in N1% of CSU patients inmost studies
In CSU patients, the prevalence of the 21 investigated individualAIDs ranged from 0% to 27.5% (Tables 2, 5). Almost all studies (95%)found each investigated AID in one or more CSU patient. AIDs with rel-atively high prevalence in the general population [11] proved to be alsoquite common in CSU patients, whereas those with low prevalenceremained a rare finding in CSU group (Tables 2, 5). For example, theprevalence of comorbidity in most studies were >=1% for insulin-depen-dent diabetes mellitus (IDDM), rheumatoid arthritis (RA), psoriasis andceliac disease, >=2% for Graves' disease (GD), >=3% for vitiligo, and >=5% forpernicious anemia and Hashimoto's thyroiditis (HT) (Fig. 1).3.2. Organ-specific AIDs are more prevalent in CSU than systemic AIDsPrevalence of individual organ-specific AIDs and systemic AIDs inCSU ranged from 0 to 27.5% in 60 studies (including 24 studies on ATDpreviously analyzed [9]) and 0-4.7% in 40 studies, respectively (Tables2, 5). In 48 of 60 studies (80%), the investigated organ-specific AIDs oc-curred in >=1% of CSU patients. In contrast, this was the case in only onethird of the studies that assessed comorbidity rates of systemic AIDs.The most common organ-specific autoimmune comorbidities in CSUwere endocrine (most prevalent: HT), hematological (most prevalent:pernicious anemia) and skin AIDs (most prevalent: vitiligo). Amongthe systemic AIDs, the most common CSU comorbidites were connec-tive tissue diseases, the most prevalent being RA. In CSU patients, co-morbid systemic autoimmune connective tissue diseases were foundby 35 studies as compared to only one study describing comorbid auto-immune vasculitis in CSU [12].3.3. N2% of CSU patients have autoimmune polyglandular syndromeencompassing ATD and vitiligo or pernicious anemia
The rates of comorbidity of two AIDs, ATD and another autoimmunedisorder, in CSU patients ranged from 0.9-6.1% (Table 6). Autoimmunepolyglandular syndromes (APS) type 3 with ATD and vitiligo (APS type3C) or ATD and pernicious anemia (APS type 3B) were found in 1.2-5.5%and 5.5-6.1% of CSU patients, respectively (N2% in most studies). Theprevalence of overlap syndromes and mixed connective tissue diseasesappears to be low and was 0.4-0.5% according to two studies (Table 2).3.4. 4. Discussion4.1. Do CSU patients have an increased risk of autoimmune comorbidity?The question whether CSU patients are at risk of AIDs, e.g. whetherAIDs are more common in CSU patients than individuals without CSU,can not be answered by the studies we analyzed, since most of the stud-ies did not investigate control groups. Hence, we can only address thisquestion by comparing prevalence rates of AIDs in patients with CSUand the general population. This approach, however, has several limita-tions. For example, many AIDs and CSU are more common in adult fe-males [3,5], and prevalence rates from population studies do not takethis into account.AIDs have an overall estimated prevalence of 4.5% in the generalpopulation, with 2.7% for males and 6.4% for females [5]. For individualAIDs, the prevalence is <=1% [5,16]. In CSU, the prevalence rates of mostindividual AIDs are >=1%, i.e. increased (Table 5). ATD, the most frequentorgan-specific AID globally (0.62-0.86%) [5], is also the most frequentautoimmune comorbidity in CSU (>=2%).In a systematic review and a meta-analysis, IgG antithyroid AAbswere found to be higher in CSU patients than in controls (includinghealthy controls) [9,17]. In a large retrospective study, CSU patientswere at significantly higher risk of having ATD, SLE, RA, IDDM, Sjogren'ssyndrome, and celiac disease compared to the control group [3]. Thelevels of ANA, RF, anti-TG, anti-parietal cell antibodies, but not anti-dsDNA and anticardiolipin antibodies, were much higher in comparisonto controls [3]. In this study, AIDs were diagnosed within 10 years afterthe diagnosis of CSU in N77% of patients with CSU [3]. Moreover, SSUpatients were shown to have a higher risk of having celiac diseasethan controls in some studies [3,18], but not all [19].
4.2. Do CSU patients with one comorbid AID have an increased risk of devel-oping a second comorbid AIDs?Patients with one AID have a higher probability of being affected by asecond one [20,21]. For example, Somers and co-workers conducted aseries of population-based cohort studies and found the risk of a secondAID such as RA, ATD and IDDM to be increased in patients who alreadyhave one of these AIDs [21]. As of now, the risk for having a second AIDin patients with CSU is unknown. In one study, 12.5% of 12,778 CSU pa-tients had 1 comorbid AID, and an additional 2.2% had 2 or more comor-bid AIDs [3], which is a higher prevalence than in the generalpopulation. For example, in the general population of Italy among25,885 people, 5.1% had one AID and only 0.2% were affected by twoor more AIDs [20].
Polyautoimmunity can be seen in the setting of autoimmunepolyglandular syndromes (APS), multiple autoimmune syndromes(MAS) and overlap syndromes [8]. APS are characterized by autoim-mune activity against endocrine and non-endocrine organs [15,22]. Afew reports considered CSU as an element of APS type 3C, which also in-cludes ATD together with vitiligo and/or alopecia and/or myastheniagravis and/or multiple sclerosis [15,22-25]. The fact that CSU patientsalso exhibit increased rates of comorbid ATD and non-vitiligo AID suchas IDDM, pernicious anemia, RA and Sjogren's syndrome (Table 6)may indicate that APS type 3A, 3B and 3D could also include CSU. Asof yet, CSU has not been included in the classification of APS 3A, 3Band 3D [15,23]. The prevalence of MAS, i.e. three or more well character-ized coexistent AIDs, appears not be increased in CSU patients (Table 6).Confino-Cohen and coworkers reported that <=0.1% of CSU patients havethree or more accompanying AIDs. Overlap syndromes (mixed connec-tive tissue diseases), i.e. the presence of signs and symptoms of two ormore diverse connective tissue diseases [5,8], appear to be as rare inCSU as they are in the general population.4.3. Why is the risk increased in CSU patients to develop comorbid AID?
Women are more likely to develop CSU [3,26,27], and they have ahigher prevalence of polyautoimmunity [28]. Female CSU patientshave a higher risk of developing comorbid AIDs such as SLE and ATDthan men [3,5,9,10], and the prevalence of CSU is higher in femalethan male ATD patients [29].The age at onset of AIDs varies widely. For many of them it is typ-ically between 20 and 40 years of age [5], which is the most commonage of onset of CSU [30]. 4.4. Is the prevalence of CSU increased in patients with AIDs?The point prevalence of CSU is <=1% [30], which is lower than theprevalence of urticarial rash in many AIDs (>=1%, Table 5). Lindegardfound that the prevalence of urticaria is significantly higher in pa-tients with psoriasis than in the general population of Sweden [42].A nationwide, population-based study from Korea reported an in-creased risk of CSU in patients with ATD compared to the controlgroup [29]. In a study of 28,900 patients with biopsy-verified celiacdisease, Ludvigsson and co-workers found that patients with celiacdisease were at an increased risk of having chronic urticaria beforeand after the diagnosis of celiac disease [43]. Zhang and others ob-served that the prevalence of chronic urticaria was significantly ele-vated in generalized vitiligo patients and their first-degree relativescompared with the population prevalence [44,45]. In summary,there is reasonably strong evidence that the prevalence of urticarialrash is increased in many AIDs.4.5. Limitations
There is considerable heterogeneity in the design of the studieswe analyzed as well as in the patient demographics they included.Many studies were small, some were uncontrolled for referral biasand did not clearly differentiate CSU from acute urticaria, chronic in-ducible urticaria and/or urticarial vasculitis. Furthermore, in manystudies the diagnostic criteria for AIDs were not described. The eval-uation of CSU prevalence in AIDs or vice versa was not the main aimin many studies. Therefore, the number of patients with CSU or AIDsincluded in such studies may not be exhaustive. Some AIDs, for ex-ample sarcoidosis, ulcerative colitis, Behcet's disease and Crohn'sdisease, currently discussed as disorders with autoinflammatory fea-tures. Our systematic review revealed a high variability in the prev-alence of AIDs in CSU between the studies. This imprecision islikely to be due to design-specific issues or a variability in the base-line patient characteristics.
