ABSTRACTBackground: Laser resurfacing is used to minimize wrinkles, solar scars and sequelae of acne. Objective:Purpose of the systematic review was to compare resurfacing outcomes of CO2 laser and erbium: yttriumaluminium garnet (erb:YAG) laser therapies. Materials and methods: Medline, Cochrane Library,EMBASE and Google Scholar databases were searched until 9 April 2015 using the following terms:laser, carbon dioxide/CO2, facial wrinkles, rhytides and erbium-doped yttrium aluminium garnet/erbium:YAG/Er:YAG. Two-armed controlled split faced studies that compared CO2 laser and erbium:YAG laser inpatients with mild-to-moderate facial wrinkles or rhytides were included. Results: The pooled data inthis study and findings of other studies support the greater efficacy with the CO2 laser in improvingfacial wrinkles, but the erb:YAG laser was associated with a better complication profile compared withthe CO2 laser. Except one case of hypopigmentation, other complications (i.e., erythema, hyperpigmen-tation and crusting) and their rates were reported by studies examining both lasers. Conclusion: Ingeneral, the CO2 laser appeared to be more efficacious then the erb:YAG laser in treating facial wrinkles.Both lasers treatments were well tolerated.ARTICLE HISTORYReceived 9 August 2016Accepted 25 January 2017KEYWORDSCO2; erbium:YAG laser;rhytides; wrinklesIntroduction
Laser resurfacing is used to minimize wrinkles, solar scars andsequelae of acne (1,2). High-energy pulsed (10,600 nm) scanningcarbon dioxide (CO2) lasers are employed to remove rhytidesand other effects of photo damage and acne damage (2-4). TheCO2 laser uses coagulative thermal damage for skin resurfacingand rejuvenation (5). The laser-tissue interaction is typicallyshort (<1 ms) resulting in limited thermal diffusion during apulse (5). However, a drawback of the CO2 laser is that thetreatment is associated with adverse effects, particularly dyschro-mia and prolonged erythema associated with photothermolysis(6-8). The level of erythema and time for re-epithelialization cankeep patients at home for more than 2 weeks (3).The erbium:yttrium aluminium garnet (erb:YAG) laser thatuses lower energy (2940 nm) is also used in facial resurfacing. Ithas the advantage that it ablates tissue with little thermaldamage (9,10). The residual thermal damage with the erb:YAG laser is about 50 um compared with 80-150 um com-monly seen with multiple passes of pulsed CO2 laser exposure(3). The 2940 wavelength is closer to the absorption peak ofwater (3000 nm) than that of the CO2 laser, resulting in most ofthe energy being absorbed in the epidermis and papillary der-mis; consequently, there is more superficial ablation with lessthermal damage (11). The erb:YAG laser has been found toassociate with shorter post-operative recovery period and lesserythema than the CO2 laser (12).There have only been a very limited number of systematicreviews or meta-analyses that compared the efficacy and sideeffects of pulsed CO2 and erb:YAG lasers. The purpose of thissystematic review was to present and compare outcomes ofthe two laser therapies for resurfacing and collagen remodel-ling in treating facial skin damage.MethodsSearch strategy
This systematic review was performed according to the PRISMAguidelines. Medline, Cochrane Library, EMBASE and GoogleScholar databases were searched until 9 April 2015 using thefollowing search terms: laser, carbon dioxide/CO2, facialCONTACT Chiehfeng (Cliff) of Public Health, School of Medicine, College of Medicine, Taipei Medical University, No.111, Section 3, Hsing-Long Rd, Taipei 116, Taiwan.(c) 2017 Taylor & Franciswrinkles, rhytides, erbium-doped yttrium aluminium garnet/erbium:YAG/Er:YAG. Two-armed controlled split faced studiesthat compared CO2 and erb:YAG lasers in patients with mild-to-moderate facial wrinkles or rhytides were included. One-armstudies were excluded, as were letters, comments, editorials, casereports, proceeding and personal communication. Only Englishpublications were included. Study eligibility was determined bytwo independent reviewers. When there was uncertainty regard-ing eligibility, a third reviewer was consulted.Data extractionThe following information/data were extracted from studiesthat met the inclusion criteria: the name of the first author,year of publication, study design, number of participants ineach study group, participants' age and gender, skin condi-tions and facial wrinkle types, types of intervention and laserparameters used for each study/control group and the out-comes (efficacies reported and rates of various complications).Results
Search resultsThe initial search identified 42 potential studies of which 36were excluded for either not being relevant or being dupli-cates, the study used a different type of laser than that ofinterest, the study only evaluated one type of laser or thereport was a commentary (Figure 1).Six studies were included in the systematic review. All sixstudies were split-face randomized controlled trials (RCT, seeTable 1) (3,13-17). Across the studies, the population ofpatients was small, ranging from 13 to 28 patients. Themean age ranged from 19 to 90 years, and most patientswere women. The location of the laser treatment was primar-ily in the periorbital or perioral region in five of the studies(3,14-17), with one study treating the upper lip region (13).The types of wrinkles were mild to moderate, and rangedfrom Fitzpatrick type I to type IV. The energy of lasers usedand spot areas are described in Table 1. The follow-up timeacross the studies was 2-6 months.
Several outcomes of laser treatment were evaluated includingpost-operative erythema, hypopigmentation, hyperpigmentation,crusting, Fitzpatrick wrinkle score following treatment and degreeof improvement (Table 2). Three of the six studies reportedfindings on post-operative erythema (3,14,17). Ross et al. foundthat the CO2 laser therapy was associated with lower-gradeerythema (grade 2.7 at two weeks and 1.5 at six weeks) comparedwith erb:YAG laser treatment (grade 3.4 at two weeks and 1.8 atsix weeks). However, Khatri et al. found that a greater percentageof patients with CO2 laser treatment experienced erythema at twoand six weeks (95% and 62%, respectively) compared with erb:YAG laser therapy (67% and 24%). McDaniel et al. found thatwith both lasers, the erythema was reversible, and the durationwas comparable between therapy groups (9.8 weeks each). Thesefindings indicate that the CO2 laser therapy had a higher fre-quency of erythema, although of lower grade, compared witherb:YAG.Only one study reported hypopigmentation, and the inci-dence was higher with CO2 laser treatment (43%) then erb:YAG laser therapy (5%) (Table 2) (3). Three of the six studiesreported hyperpigmentation, and the incidence and severitywere similar between treatments (3,14,16). In the three studiesthat reported findings on crusting, the intensity and length ofcrusting was greater with CO2 than erb:YAG laser therapy(3,13,16). Both the hyperpigmentation and crusting werereversible for both laser treatments. Overall, CO2 laser therapywas associated with a higher degree of wrinkle improvementthan erb:YAG laser treatment across all the studies (Tables 2,3 and Figure 2).
DiscussionIn this study, the erb:YAG laser was associated with a slightlybetter complication profile compared with the CO2 laser. TheCO2 laser was associated with a higher incidence of erythema,although of lower grade, compared with the erb:YAG laser, andwith a greater frequency and intensity of crusting. The laserswere similar in the incidence of hyperpigmentation. Except forone case of hypopigmentation, other complications (i.e.,erythema, hyperpigmentation and crusting) were reversible fol-lowing treatment with either laser. Hyperpigmentation is acommon longer-term adverse effect of laser resurfacing treat-ment with both lasers (18). Crusting and erythema are unavoid-able short-term side effects of fractional laser treatments (18). Itis not possible to statistically compare the findings among thestudies due to a lack of homogeneity in the methods of reportingresults used by included studies.In the three studies that provided comparable databetween the two laser treatments, the CO2 laser was asso-ciated with greater efficacy than the erb:YAG (3,12,13). Thetrend for better efficacy with the CO2 laser treatment wasalso apparent in the other three studies, but authors ofthose three studies concluded that the two treatmentsweresimilarintheirabilitytotreatfacialwrinkles(14,15,17). With CO2 and erb:YAG lasers, the inci-dence of long-term complications has been low, particularlyin experienced hands (19,20). For CO2 lasers, the short-term complications include transient erythema, pruritus,milia formation, acneiform, pustules and transient scarring.Hypopigmentation is sometimes observed in CO2 lasertreatment (18), and is a long-term complication that canpotentially become irreversible. A major complication asso-ciated with erb:YAG is the transient hyperpigmentation,whichistypicallyshortterminnature(18).Hyperpigmentation appears to be related to the ablationdepth, which is less with the erb:YAG than the CO2 laser.In general and as seen in our study, CO2 lasers are asso-ciated with higher incidences of post-surgery morbiditythan erb:YAG (18). There are also combined erb:YAG/CO2 dual-mode laser systems available, notably the DermaK(Lumenis,SantaClara,California)andtheSciton
Contour (Palo Alto, California), which supposedly combinethe advantages of both laser systems, but controlled com-parative studies of these systems are still lacking.This systematic review has several strengths. All includedstudies were split-face RCTs that evaluated same patients withsimilarly mild-to-moderate facial wrinkles (ranged Fitzpatrickscores I-IV, but most were I-III). Among included studies,patients with lesions of malignancy or hypertrophic keloid, orscar formation were typically excluded. However, there aresome limitations to this study that should be considered. Thenumber of included studies was small. In addition, the studiesdiffered in the method used to evaluate efficacy, with a mix-ture of subjective and objective criteria. This heterogeneityprecluded the ability to perform a meta-analysis. The methodof reporting adverse effects also differed across the studies,making a statistical comparison between treatments impossi-ble. There was also heterogeneity in the laser parameters (laserenergy, spotting size, fluencies, number of passes, etc.) usedamong the studies, and the skill of physicians who performedthe laser treatment may have varied across the studies, whichcould have impacted both efficacy and complication rates.Lastly, Karsai et al. (15) describe the type of laser used (abla-tive fractional) clearly, whereas the other studies did notspecifically state the type of laser. However, we believe wecan assume that the other studies used ablative nonfractionallasers (21,22).In conclusion, the CO2 laser appears to have better efficacythan the erb:YAG laser in treating facial wrinkles.
