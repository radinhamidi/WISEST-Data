Venous Thromboembolism Prophylaxis in Hospitalized Medical Patientsand Those With Stroke: A Background Review for an American Collegeof Physicians Clinical Practice GuidelineFrank A. Lederle, MD; Dylan Zylla, MD; Roderick MacDonald, MS; and Timothy J. Wilt, MD, MPHBackground: Venous thromboembolism prophylaxis has been rec-ommended for nonsurgical patients, but its effectiveness remainsuncertain.Purpose: To assess the benefits and harms of prophylaxis in hos-pitalized adult medical patients and those with acute stroke.Data Sources: MEDLINE and the Cochrane Library from 1950through April 2011, reference lists, and study authors.Study Selection: English-language randomized trials were includedif they provided clinical outcomes and evaluated therapy with low-dose heparin or related agents or mechanical measures comparedwith placebo, no treatment, or other active prophylaxis in thetarget population.Data Extraction: Two independent investigators extracted data onstudy characteristics and clinical outcomes up to 120 days afterrandomization. The primary outcome was total mortality.Data Synthesis: In medical patients, heparin prophylaxis did notreduce total mortality but did result in fewer pulmonary embolisms(PEs) (odds ratio [OR], 0.69 [95% CI, 0.52 to 0.90], but withevidence of publication bias) and an increase in all bleeding events(risk ratio [RR], 1.34 [CI, 1.08 to 1.66]). Heparin prophylaxis hadno statistically significant effect on any outcome in patients withacute stroke except for an increase in major bleeding events (OR,1.66 [CI, 1.20 to 2.28]). When trials of medical patients and thosewith stroke were considered together (18 studies; 36 122 patients),heparin prophylaxis reduced the incidence of PE (OR, 0.70 [CI,0.56 to 0.87]; absolute reduction, 3 events per 1000 patients
treated [CI, 1 to 5 events]) but increased the incidence of allbleeding (RR, 1.28 [CI, 1.05 to 1.56]) and major bleeding events(OR, 1.61 [CI, 1.23 to 2.10]), with an absolute increase of 9bleeding events per 1000 patients treated (CI, 2 to 18 events), 4 ofwhich were major (CI, 1 to 7 events). A reduction in total mortalityapproached statistical significance (RR, 0.93 [CI, 0.86 to 1.00]; P 0.056; absolute decrease, 6 deaths per 1000 patients treated [CI, 0to 11 deaths]). No statistically significant differences in clinical out-comes were observed in the 14 trials that compared unfractionatedheparin with low-molecular-weight heparin. No improvements inclinical outcomes were seen in the 3 studies of mechanical prophy-laxis in patients with stroke, but more patients had lower-extremityskin damage (RR, 4.02 [CI, 2.34 to 6.91])--an increase of 39events per 1000 patients treated (CI, 17 to 77 events).Limitation: NonEnglish-language studies were not included, butthese were few and small.Conclusion: Heparin prophylaxis had no significant effect on mor-tality, may have reduced PE in medical patients and all patientscombined, and led to more bleeding and major bleeding events,thus resulting in little or no net benefit. No differences in benefits orharms were found according to type of heparin used. Mechanicalprophylaxis provided no benefit and resulted in clinically importantharm to patients with stroke.Primary Funding Source: American College of Physicians.

Venous thromboembolism (VTE) prophylaxis withlow-dose subcutaneous anticoagulants (such as unfrac-tionated heparin [UFH], low-molecular-weight heparin[LMWH], or fondaparinux) or mechanical leg compres-sion has been recommended for many of these patients(2) and adopted as an optional core measure by theJoint Commission on accreditation of hospitals (3), butits effectiveness remains uncertain. Several randomizedtrials (4-7) have reported reductions in asymptomaticdeep venous thrombosis (DVT) from heparin prophy-laxis in hospitalized medical patients, but this surrogateoutcome is much more common than clinically evidentdisease, and its value has been questioned (8-10). Aquasi-randomized study (11) reported that heparin pro-phylaxis reduced mortality in hospitalized medical pa-tients, but subsequent properly randomized trials havenot found statistically significant reductions in mortalityand have generally reported no improvement in clini-cally evident outcomes.We systematically reviewed randomized trials that ad-dressed the benefits and risks of VTE prophylaxis in hos-pitalized adult nonsurgical patients, including trials donein various categories of medical patients and those done inpatients with acute stroke. METHODSWe developed and followed a standard protocol for allsteps of the review process.Data Sources and SelectionWe searched MEDLINE from 1950 through April2011 for articles listed in all 3 of the following MedicalSubject Heading groupings: pulmonary embolism, thrombo-embolism, thrombosis, thrombophlebitis, venous thromboem-bolism, venous thrombosis, or thromboembolism as keywords;primary prevention, prevent as a keyword, or subheading"prevention and control"; and publication type (randomized,controlled trial; controlled clinical trial; phase 3 or 4 clinicaltrial; or meta-analysis [used to identify additional trials]). Sim-ilar terms were used to search the Cochrane Library for clini-cal trials of VTE prophylaxis. The titles and abstracts of iden-tified references were reviewed, and reference lists of pertinentrandomized trials and systematic reviews were used to identifyadditional reports. When necessary, additional informationwas requested from the original authors.
Study SelectionWe included randomized, English-language trials thatstudied treatments commonly recommended and used toprevent VTE, such as subcutaneous low-dose UFH(20 000 U/d) or similar prophylactic dosages of LMWHor related agents (such as fondaparinux) or graduated com-pression stockings or other mechanical measures (such asintermittent pneumatic compression). Eligible comparatorsincluded placebo, no treatment, or other active prophy-laxis. Studies were limited to those that provided separatedata for hospitalized medical patients or those with stroke(excluding surgical, trauma, obstetric, or pediatric patients)in acute or intermediate care facilities. Quasi-randomizedtrials were excluded, as were trials in which patients wereroutinely treated with another anticoagulant (such as war-farin or drotrecogin alfa) or thrombolytic agent. Studies ofheparin (but not mechanical) prophylaxis that were donespecifically in patients with acute myocardial infarctionwere also excluded because full-dose anticoagulation hasbeen shown to be beneficial in acute coronary syndromesfor reasons other than VTE prophylaxis; however, we didnot exclude studies of medical patients that may have in-cluded some patients with the acute coronary syndrome.Data Extraction and Quality AssessmentTwo reviewers extracted the data. Discrepancies wereinfrequent and were resolved through discussion with allauthors. Our primary outcome was total mortality up to120 days after randomization. Secondary outcomes (all to120 days) included symptomatic DVT; all pulmonary em-bolism (PE); fatal PE; all bleeding events; major bleedingevents; and, for mechanical prophylaxis, skin damage. Wechose 120 days to include most events likely to be related
to both the intervention and any rebound effect after itsdiscontinuation (12) without excessive dilution by unre-lated later events. Because some studies reported PEs thatwere believed to be fatal (5), others reported all PEs de-tected at autopsy (13), and still others reported both cate-gories (8), we analyzed both "fatal PE" and "all PE associ-ated with death." "All bleeding" and "major bleeding"events were, of necessity, included as defined and reportedby the original authors. Studies that gave criteria for majorbleeding events usually included bleeding that was fatal,required surgery, was in a critical location or caused per-manent injury, or was overt and involved transfusion ofmore than 2 units of blood. A recent systematic review (14)found that major bleeding events, similarly defined, had asimilar risk for death to that of recurrent symptomaticVTE. For studies of stroke, we followed the practice ofmost of the original authors and included hemorrhagictransformations of stroke as "bleeding" and, if symptom-atic, as "major bleeding." When possible, we included allevents after randomization according to intention to treat,even if the original authors had excluded them.We excluded surrogate outcomes, such as asymptom-atic DVT or thrombocytopenia (a very rarely reportedfinding) detected by routine testing of study patients, aswell as diagnoses of VTE that were based only on historyand physical examination without confirmatory testing.Study quality was assessed on the basis of the adequacy ofallocation concealment (15), blinding methods (patient, in-vestigator, or outcome assessor), intention-to-treat analysis,adequate details of study withdrawals and dropouts, andsource of funding. The overall strength of evidence for therandomized trials was evaluated by using methods developedby the Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality and theEffective Health Care Program (16). The strength of the evi-dence was evaluated on the basis of 4 required domains: riskfor bias (whether the studies for a given outcome or compar-ison had good internal validity), consistency (the degree ofsimilarity in the effect sizes of the included studies), directness(whether the studies showed a single, direct link between theintervention of interest and the outcome), and precision (thedegree of certainty surrounding an effect estimate of a givenoutcome).
Data Synthesis and AnalysisWe conducted meta-analyses on comparisons thatwere made in multiple studies, including prophylaxis withheparin and related agents versus no heparin, prophylaxiswith LMWH versus UFH, and mechanical prophylaxisversus no mechanical prophylaxis. Studies of patients withstroke were analyzed both separately from and togetherwith studies of medical patients. Data were pooled andanalyzed by using Cochrane Collaboration Review Man-ager software (The Nordic Cochrane Center, Copenhagen,Denmark). Heterogeneity between studies was assessed byusing chi-square and I2 tests, with a P value less than 0.100and an I2 value greater than 50% considered high (17).Downloaded from  by Ruhr Universitat Bochum on 09/26/2023.Because of the frequent zero rates for various outcomes insmall studies, Peto odds ratios (ORs) were used exceptwhen event rates exceeded 1% (for mortality, all bleedingevents, and skin damage), in which case risk ratios (RRs)from random-effects models were used (18, 19). Absolutemeasures of intervention effects were calculated from ORs orRRs and pooled risk in control participants by usingGRADEpro, version 3.2 ( sensitivity analyses were performed to assessthe robustness of our findings. The analyses of heparintrials were limited to only double-blinded studies to eval-uate the importance of subjectivity in the ascertainment ofoutcomes other than death. Analyses were also repeatedafter excluding heparin dosages that were no longer recom-mended for prophylaxis and with follow-up limited to 90days. We examined funnel plots and performed Egger in-tercept tests (20) to assess for publication bias.Role of the Funding SourceThe American College of Physicians Clinical GuidelinesCommittee supported this project. Committee members as-sisted in the formulation of the questions, reviewed drafts ofthis report, recommended changes, and approved submission.
RESULTSIdentified StudiesOur search for randomized trials yielded 4340 refer-ences (Appendix Figure 1, available at www.annals.org).After exclusions based on a review of the abstracts, 302articles were obtained for further review; 40 unique ran-domized trials that met our inclusion criteria, involvingmore than 52 000 patients, were ultimately identified. Ofthese, 21 were conducted in medical patients, of which 10compared prophylaxis with heparin or related agents withno heparin (4-8, 10, 13, 21-23), 9 compared LMWHwith UFH (24-32), and 2 compared different durations ofLMWH prophylaxis (33, 34). Nineteen trials were con-ducted in patients with acute stroke, of which 8 comparedheparin prophylaxis with no heparin (35-42), 5 comparedLMWH with UFH (43-47), 3 compared mechanical withno mechanical prophylaxis (48-50), and 3 had unique de-signs (51-53). Appendix Table 1 (available at www.annals.org) shows the characteristics of these studies.We identified 3 pairs of studies (24, 26, 28, 29, 35,36) for which dual publication of some patient dataseemed possible on the basis of similarities in design, loca-tion, and personnel. In all 3 instances, the original authorsconfirmed that no dual publication occurred, and all 6studies were included. For several studies (4, 27), we in-cluded deaths after randomization that the original authorshad excluded. We excluded outcomes without confirma-tory testing (3 cases of "fatal PE") from 1 study (22). Onestudy (5) randomly assigned patients to receive placebo or1 of 2 prophylactic doses of enoxaparin, which we com-bined into 1 treatment group. In 2 studies that compared
patients receiving low- or high-dose heparin with controlparticipants (41, 42), the high-dose group was excludedbecause it exceeded our threshold for prophylactic dosing.Entry criteria were typically designed to enroll patientsbelieved to be at increased risk for VTE, but none of thestudies used risk assessment scales or scores. Patients in themedical trials usually had to be above a minimum age, typi-cally 60 years, but the mean age was usually more than 70years (Appendix Table 1). Studies varied on whether any pa-tient admitted to a medical service was eligible or only patientswho received certain diagnoses, and whether a minimum du-ration of immobilization or hospital stay was expected.Patients were generally excluded from these trials ifthey had a definite indication for heparin (such as currentthromboembolism or atrial fibrillation) or contraindicationto it (such as high bleeding risk or thrombocytopenia).Other usual exclusions were recent surgery, recent myocar-dial infarction or stroke (if eligibility was not already lim-ited to specific diagnoses), and expected short survival. Tri-als of patients with stroke had similar entry criteria to thoseof medical patients, frequently specified the duration (typ-ically no more than 1 to 3 days) or severity of stroke, andusually excluded patients who had hemorrhagic stroke orwere comatose. Studies of mechanical prophylaxis had noexclusions related to coagulation or bleeding status. Themean or median time receiving prophylaxis was 6 to 14days in included studies, although outcomes were oftencollected for a longer period.Several trials examined subgroup effects, includingage, sex, medical diagnoses, and characteristics of stroke(29, 42, 47, 50, 54, 55), but none reported significantinteractions or differences among any subgroups. Severalmedical studies enrolled only patients with 1 condition,including cancer (23), infectious disease (8), or mechanicalventilation due to exacerbation of chronic obstructive lungdisease (6), so they provided indication-specific results.Of the 18 trials that compared heparin with no hepa-rin, 44% reported adequate allocation concealment, 61%were double-blind, 56% analyzed by intention to treat,78% adequately reported reasons and proportions of with-drawals or dropouts, and 39% reported nonindustry fund-ing (Appendix Figure 2, available at www.annals.org). Ofthe 14 trials of LMWH versus UFH, 36% reported ade-quate allocation concealment, 71% were double-blind,29% analyzed by intention to treat, 86% adequately re-ported reasons and proportions of withdrawals or drop-outs, and 1 reported funding by a nonindustry source.

Some (59, 60) have focused on asymp-tomatic DVT, which was the primary outcome of many ofthe individual trials. A systematic review of prevention ofsymptomatic VTE in hospitalized medical patients (57)differed from ours by considering only events that occurredduring prophylaxis and excluding those that occurred af-terward. The findings of these reviews have varied with thestudies included, although the conclusions have tended tomore strongly favor prophylaxis when the focus was onasymptomatic DVT. One recent review (61) comparedprophylaxis with 5000 U of UFH given 2 versus 3 timesdaily, and it found more benefit but also more bleedingwith the 3-times-daily dosage. Because we excluded asymp-tomatic DVT and studies of acute myocardial infarction,our data were insufficient to compare these 2 dosages.Which outcomes should be preferred when judgingthe effectiveness of VTE prophylaxis remains a difficultquestion. The problems with surrogate outcomes in gen-eral (62), and with asymptomatic DVT in particular (8-10), have been emphasized elsewhere. Symptomatic DVTis also problematic because it is uncommon and in manycases does not precede PE. The diagnosis of PE is increas-ingly made by computed tomography angiography, includ-ing many small PEs that are found incidentally in 2% to5% of hospitalized patients and are of unknown impor-tance (63). However, the studies that we included did notscreen patients with computed tomography, so the findingspresented herein should reflect clinical disease. Fatal PE hasbeen used as an outcome in some studies (8, 57), but the
role of PE in a patient's death is often difficult to ascertain(63). The comparison of outcomes has been clarified re-cently by a systematic review finding that risk for death issimilar between major bleeding events and symptomaticVTE (14). Long-term sequelae of VTE, such as pulmonaryhypertension or the postthrombotic syndrome, are clini-cally important but impractical for use in trials becausedifferences in these outcomes are extremely difficult to de-tect. Fewer than 4% of survivors of PE develop pulmonaryhypertension within 2 years (64), and the clinical manifes-tations that define the postthrombotic syndrome occuronly 1.5 times as often in patients who have had asymp-tomatic DVT as in control participants (65). Total mor-tality is clinically important and can be accurately ascer-tained but requires a large number of patients todemonstrate a difference because of dilution by deathsnot related to VTE. Overcoming this problem is a prin-cipal reason for conducting a meta-analysis, but a statis-tically significant reduction in mortality from heparinprophylaxis could not be demonstrated even after alltrials of hospitalized medical patients and those withstroke were combined (36 000 patients). A very largerandomized trial would be needed to properly evaluate adifference in total mortality, and 1 such trial (10) endedin the pilot phase because only a small proportion ofhospitalized medical patients were candidates for enroll-ment. These data suggest that the rebound thromboticevents reported after stopping full-dose heparin therapy(12) may also occur after stopping low-dose heparintherapy.On the basis of our findings, if heparin prophylaxisis to be used in hospitalized nonsurgical patients, thechoice of agent will probably depend on such factors asconvenience and cost rather than comparative effective-ness and harms. All of the prophylactic anticoagulantsexamined in our review are given as subcutaneous injec-tions, 2 or 3 times daily for UFH and once daily forLMWH or fondaparinux. Average wholesale drug costsare about $10 per day for UFH, $35 per day forLMWH, and $60 per day for fondaparinux. Althoughfew data on effect size from randomized trials are avail-able to refine patient selection, several trial reports (34,54, 55) have suggested that heparin is less beneficial inpatients younger than 75 years.We conclude that in hospitalized medical patients,low-dose heparin prophylaxis reduced PE but not totalmortality or other clinical outcomes and resulted in moreepisodes of bleeding. In patients with acute stroke, wefound no statistically significant benefit from heparin pro-phylaxis but found increased risk for major bleeding.When considering medical patients and those with stroketogether, low-dose heparin prophylaxis may have reducedPE and increased risk for bleeding and major bleedingevents and had no statistically significant effect on mortal-
ity. We interpret these findings as indicative of little or nonet benefit. No significant differences in clinical benefits orharms were found between UFH and LMWH.
