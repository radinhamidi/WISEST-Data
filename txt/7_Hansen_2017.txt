INTRODUCTION Type 2 diabetes is associated with obesity, hypertension, dyslipidemia and an increased risk of cardiovascular disease. Glycemic control is monitored by glycated hemoglobin A1c (HbA1c), which, in addition to reflecting overall plasma glucose concentration, is an independent predictor of cardiovascular disease (Gall, Borch-Johnsen, Hougaard, Nielsen, & Parving, 1995; Stratton et al., 2000). However, the benefit of intensive glycemic control on macrovascular complications is controversial and individualized glycemic targets are recommended as the best way to manage the disease (ADVANCE Collaborative Group et al., 2008; Inzucchi et al., 2015). Dyslipidemia exacerbates the risk of cardiovascular 
complications associated with type 2 diabetes and treatment with statins reduce cardiovascular mortality in type 2 diabetes (Gaede et al., 2003). Numerous antidiabetic drugs and lipid-lowering drugs are used in the management of type 2 diabetes, but not all only few patients reach the treatment goals (Cheung et al., 2009; Inzucchi et al., 2015).  Bile acid sequestrants (BASs) constitute a class of drugs that have been used for cholesterol-lowering treatment in decades (Lyons, Webster, Fowler, & Petrie, 1994; Zieve, Kalin, Schwartz, Jones, & Bailey, 2007). The BASs comprise first-generation cholestyramine and colestipol and second-generation colesevelam hydrochloride (referred to as colesevelam) and colestimide (also known as colestilan, marketed only in Japan). BASs form a nonabsorbable complex with bile acids in the intestine, which prevents reabsorption and increases fecal excretion of bile acids (Rosenbaum, Petersen, Ducharme, Markham, & Goldberg, 1997). Consequently, bile acid synthesis is increased, which diverts circulating low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol into bile acids thereby reducing plasma LDL cholesterol. Cholestyramine was the first lipid-altering drug to show, in a randomized trial, a reduced risk of myocardial infarction and death by coronary heart disease.("The Lipid Research Clinics ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPTCoronary Primary Prevention Trial results. II," 1984; "The Lipid Research Clinics Coronary Primary Prevention Trial results. I," 1984). In 1994, Garg and Grundy described that cholestyramine may also reduce fasting plasma glucose in patients with type 2 diabetes (Garg & Grundy, 1994). Fourteens years later, in 2008, colesevelam was approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for the treatment of hyperglycemia in type 2 diabetes and colesevelam is now included in diabetes management algorithms (Garber et al., 2013; Inzucchi et al., 2015).   Published data suggest that BASs improve glycemic control. However, not all trials report this outcome in a consistent manner, which may suggest reporting bias. In order to describe the effects of BAS treatment in patients with type 2 diabetes we gathered published and unpublished data and performed a systematic review with meta-analyses, subgroup and sensitivity analyses.   2. MATERIALS AND METHODS This systematic review was based on a registered (PROSPERO CRD42012002552) and 
published protocol (Hansen et al., 2012) prepared and reported based on previous recommendations ("Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions"; Moher, Liberati, Tetzlaff, & Altman, 2009). Our main objective was to evaluate the effects of BASs versus placebo or no interventions on glycemic control and adverse events in type 2 diabetes. We originally planned to compare BAS versus other antidiabetic interventions, but only identified one trial comparing BASs versus rosiglitazone or sitagliptin (Rigby et al., 2010). We decided not to include data from the rosiglitazone arm in our analyses because the drug has been associated with increased risk of myocardial infarction but not cardiovascular or all-cause mortality (Nissen & Wolski, 2010). Our primary endpoint was glycemic control ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPTACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT(HbA1c). Secondary endpoints included adverse events and changes in fasting plasma glucose, total cholesterol, high-density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol, LDL cholesterol, triglycerides and body weight. 2.1 Data Sources and Searches  We included randomized controlled trials, irrespective of date, publication status, language, 
bias control and predefined outcome. Eligible trials were identified by electronic searches in The Cochrane Library, Medline, Embase, the Web of Science and clinicaltrials.gov. The last search was made on 4 August 2016. The search terms in the Cochrane Library were ("bile acid sequestrant" or "cholestyramine" or "colestimide" or "colestipol" or "colesevelam") and "type 2 diabetes". The search strategy was adjusted to fit the other databases and combined with the Cochrane Search String for the identification of randomized clinical trials ("Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions"). We also searched the WHO International Clinical Trial Registry Platform and conference proceedings from the annual scientific meetings of the American Diabetes Association (ADA) and the European Association for the Study of Diabetes (EASD). The reference lists of relevant articles were manually examined and relevant trials identified. Additional information on trials was sought through correspondence with authors and pharmaceutical companies.  2.2 Study Selection Two authors independently reviewed the trial inclusion and exclusion criteria. Excluded trials were listed with the reason for exclusion. We included trials of adult patients of both genders with type 2 diabetes as defined by the authors of the included trials. The BAS groups included 
cholestyramine, colesevelam colestimide or colestipol, irrespective of daily dose or duration of the trial. The control groups were placebo, no intervention or other active comparators. Co-interventions were allowed if administered equally to the intervention groups. ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPTACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT2.3 Data extraction and Quality Assessment Data extraction was performed independently by three authors based on standardized extraction forms. Disagreements were resolved through discussion. Corresponding authors were contacted if the required information was not described in the published reports. The risk of bias was assessed by separate domains: random sequence generation, allocation concealment, blinding of participants, personnel and outcome assessment, incomplete outcome data, selective reporting and other bias ("Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions"). 2.4 Data Synthesis and Analysis Analyses were performed using STATA version 13 (Stata Corp, College Station, TX, USA). The meta-analyses were performed using random-effects based on expected clinical 
heterogeneity. We repeated the analyses using fixed-effect models. In all analyses, the overall conclusion of the two models concurred, but random-effects models provided the most conservative estimate of the intervention effect. We therefore only report the result of the random-effects meta-analyses. Results of the meta-analyses were expressed as mean differences (MD) for continuous outcomes and relative risks (RR) for dichotomous outcomes, both with 95% confidence intervals (CI). We used I2 values as markers of heterogeneity with the following thresholds: 0% to 40%: not important; 30% to 60%: moderate heterogeneity; 50% to 90%: substantial heterogeneity; 75% to 100%: considerable heterogeneity (Higgins & Thompson, 2002). Funnel plots and regression tests for funnel plot asymmetry in meta-analysis were performed for meta-analyses with at least ten trials to assess small study effects (Egger test for continuous and Harbord for dichotomous outcomes). The effects of interventions were examined using subgroup and sensitivity analyses. These analyses were performed according to type of BAS, type of control, risk of bias, type of data (published or ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPTACCEPTED MANUSCRIPTunpublished) and country of origin. We performed a post-hoc subgroup analysis on the 
country of origin to evaluate the potential influence of ethnicity. Differences between subgroups were expressed as P values (test for subgroup differences).   3. Information about allocation sequence generation could not be retrieved in the remaining 2 trials (Kato et al., 2011; Suzuki et al., 2012). Accordingly, we classed the allocation sequence as "low risk of bias" in 15 trials and "uncertain risk of bias" in 2 trials (Kato et al., 2011; Suzuki et al., 2012). In 16 trials, the allocation was concealed using central randomization, sealed serially numbered opaque sealed envelopes or administration of 
identically appearing coded drug bottles/containers. The allocation concealment was classed ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPTACCEPTED MANUSCRIPTas "low risk of bias" in these trials and "uncertain risk of bias" in the remaining trial (Suzuki et al., 2012). Twelve trials that were double-blinded using a placebo (blinding of participants, personnel and outcome assessors) were classed as low risk of performance and detection bias. The remaining five trials were open and classed as high risk of performance and detection bias (Kato et al., 2011; Neda et al., 2012; Rigby et al., 2010; Suzuki et al., 2012; Takebayashi et al., 2009). All patients were accounted for in 16 trials, which were classed as low risk of attrition bias (incomplete outcome data). One trial did not account for patients with incomplete outcome data (Kondo & Kadowaki, 2010). All 17 trials defined and reported clinically relevant outcome measures, and we did not identify differences between the protocoled outcome measures (published in e.g. trial registries) and reported outcomes measures. Accordingly, all trials were classed as low risk of reporting bias. No other biases were identified (Table 3).  3.3 Hemoglobin A1c

DISCUSSION This systematic review with meta-analyses includes data from randomized clinical trials evaluating the glucose-lowering effect of the BASs colesevelam and colestimide in patients ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPTACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT
with type 2 diabetes. Our key finding is that colesevelam and colestimide induced clinical relevant reductions in HbA1c by 0.40% to 0.55% (4.4 mmol/mol to 6.0 mmol/mol) and fasting plasma glucose by 0.65 mmol/L to 1.07 mmol/L compared to the control group. Also, we confirm that colesevelam and colestimide reduce LDL cholesterol, although to a lesser extent than statin treatment, and increases the risk of constipation. These findings were confirmed by analysis of the trials with low risk of bias in all domains.  Some of the identified trials reported postprandial glucose concentrations obtained after different interventions such as 75-g oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) (Brufau et al., 2010; Henry et al., 2012; Kato et al., 2011), varying types of mixed meal tolerance tests (Beysen et al., 2012; Marina et al., 2012; Schwartz et al., 2010; Smushkin et al., 2013; Zieve et al., 2007) and in one trial the intervention was not reported (Kondo & Kadowaki, 2010). While postprandial glucose was not a pre-specified endpoint in the study protocol and the results were obtained under different conditions precluding meaningful meta-analysis, the results did provide insights into the potential mode of action of BAS. With the exception of the trial by Kato et al., no difference was observed in postabsorptive glucose concentrations between BAS and placebo in the trials using OGTT, whereas the trials applying mixed meal tolerance 
test reported lower postprandial glucose concentration with BAS compared to placebo. While this systematic review with meta-analysis was not designed to investigate the mode of action of BAS, these results could suggest that gallbladder emptying and subsequent release of bile acids into the intestine observed after a mixed meal test as opposed to after an OGTT (Sonne, Rehfeld, Holst, Vilsboll, & Knop, 2014), is mediating the at least part of the glucose-lowering effect of BASs - potentially via reduced gastric emptying and/or the enteroendocrine hormones glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1) and cholecystokinin (CCK) (Hansen, Sonne, & ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPTACCEPTED MANUSCRIPTIn patients with type 2 diabetes, tight glycemic control may come at the price of weight gain as typically observed when sulfonylurea compounds or insulin treatment are used (Inzucchi et al., 2015). Our results suggest that colesevelam and colestimide are weight neutral, although weight data were only available in a minority of the included trials. However, the relative modest glucose lowering effect of colesevelam and colestimide should be seen in the light of its body weight neutral effect when comparing colesevelam and colestimide to sulfonylurea compounds and insulin treatment.  
As aforementioned, the first-generation BAS cholestyramine, in a dose of 24 g daily, has been shown to reduce cardiovascular events in otherwise healthy subjects with hypercholesterolemia (mean LDL cholesterol ~5 mmol/L) not treated with statins ("The Lipid Research Clinics Coronary Primary Prevention Trial results. II," 1984, "The Lipid Research Clinics Coronary Primary Prevention Trial results. I," 1984). The dose may be comparable with the recommended dosing of colesevelam and colestimide, as they are believed to be 4-6 times more potent than cholestyramine (Braunlin W et al., 2000). However, it is difficult to predict the effect of colesevelam and colestimide on cardiovascular events in a population of which a substantial proportion is treated with a statin (Association, 2015). Whether the increase in serum triglycerides associated with BAS treatment is clinical relevant is unknown (Berglund et al., 2012). However, severe hypertriglyceridemia is known to increase the risk of pancreatitis, and may be a risk factor for cardiovascular disease and BAS treatment should be used with caution in patients with high serum triglyceride concentrations and in patients with a history of hypertriglyceridemia-induced pancreatitis (Emerging Risk Factors Collaboration, BASs are not absorbed in the intestine and therefore lack systemic toxicity. Also, they are not dependent on liver and kidney function (Lyons et al., 1994). This is in contrast to metformin 
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPTACCEPTED MANUSCRIPTand sulfonylurea compounds, which should be used with caution in patients with impaired liver and kidney function (Abe, Okada, & Soma, 2011; Marquess, 2011).  In the present analysis, BAS treatment was associated with more serious adverse events than the control group although the absolute number was low. Gastrointestinal adverse events, especially constipation, were prevalent, but did not seem to affect the attrition rate. Poor compliance has been reported with the first-generation BASs cholestyramine and colestipol (Avorn et al., 1998; "The Lipid Research Clinics Coronary Primary Prevention Trial results. II," 1984), but we did not find evidence of differences in attrition rates between patients treated with BASs and the control group.  4.1 Strengths and limitations This systematic review is the first to include all types of BASs in a systematic review. This provides validity for the study and allows us to make conclusions about the drug class as a whole despite the increased inter-trial heterogeneity. At the same time, by subgroup analysis, we were able to compare the individual BASs. In contrast to a previously published review 
(Ooi & Loke, 2012), we were able to compile unpublished data from the majority of published trials. These data allowed us to perform rigorous bias control, and to increase the amount of data included in the individual analyses. As outcomes are likely to be presented in a favorable way in published articles, the retrieval of unpublished outcome data may reduce the risk of bias from selective reporting. However, we did not find evidence of publication bias.  Patients lost to follow-up and inadequate randomization can lead to overestimated benefits of a treatment. The majority of the included trials had deficiencies in the reported randomization methods, which is a limitation to the internal validity of our findings. However, all of the ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPTACCEPTED MANUSCRIPTincluded trials but one did account for the intention-to-treat population including losses to follow-up. The companies marketing colesevelam and colestimide, respectively, funded the vast majority of the included trials. It has been described that financial interests could lead to bias in comparisons of intervention (Wood et al., 2008), but also that funding may be associated with 
adequate control of bias (Kjaergard & Gluud, 2002). A study of randomized clinical trials showed that industry funding was associated with more positive author conclusions, but not with the quantitative results of the included trials (Als-Nielsen, Chen, Gluud, & Kjaergard, 2003). We did rigorous assessments of the quality of included trials. None of our analyses showed evidence of selection, assessment, attrition, or outcome reporting bias. These analyses support the validity of the results. 4.2 Conclusions The present systematic review with meta-analyses of randomized controlled trials found that BAS treatment improves glycemic control with a mean reduction in HbA1c of 0.55% (95% CI -0.64 to -0.46) in trials reporting end of treatment and 0.40% (95% CI -0.49 to -0.30) in trials reporting mean change. Also, treatment with BASs reduces LDL cholesterol, although to a lesser extent than statin treatment, and appears to be weight neutral. BAS treatment is associated with gastrointestinal adverse events, especially constipation. Further, BAS treatment is associated with increased serum triglyceride concentrations, but the clinical implications of this are unknown. Thus, colesevelam and colestimide with their dual - albeit modest - actions on glucose and LDL cholesterol could be relevant as add-on treatment in 
metformin and statin-treated patients with type 2 diabetes, who do not reach the treatment goals of glycemic control and LDL cholesterol, or in patients who are statin intolerant. Also, BASs could, given the non-absorbable nature, be used in the treatment of patients with type 2 ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPTACCEPTED MANUSCRIPTdiabetes and end-stage renal disease. However, colesevelam and colestimide have not been evaluated in cardiovascular outcome trials, which since 2008 has been required by the FDA for new antidiabetic drugs (FDA, n.d.). Cardiovascular outcome trials are essential to establish the safety and potential of BAS treatment. To our knowledge, no such trials have been planned. Finally, we were only able to identify one trial comparing BAS treatment with other antidiabetic treatments (Rigby et al., 2010). Such trials are also needed to establish the place of BASs in the management of type 2 diabetes.
