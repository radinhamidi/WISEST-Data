REVIEW ARTICLETreatment of necrobiotic xanthogranuloma - a systematicreviewD. Miguel,* J. Lukacs, T. Illing, P. ElsnerDepartment of Dermatology, University Hospital, Jena, Germany*Correspondence: D. Miguel. E-mail: AbstractNecrobiotic xanthogranuloma (NXG) is an uncommon non-Langerhans cell histiocytosis involving skin and extracuta-neous tissues. The lesions are usually asymptomatic and commonly appear in the periorbital area. Paraproteinemia isclosely associated with NXG and its pathogenesis remains unclear. NXG prognosis is poor with several treatmentsshowing variable results. Treatment of monoclonal gammopathy with alkylating agents does not necessarily influencethe activity of the skin disease and vice versa. The aim of this systematic review is to summarize all reported treatmentsof necrobiotic xanthogranuloma of the skin, with or without underlying malignant condition and based on articles fromthe PubMed database using the query 'necrobiotic xanthogranuloma treatment', both in English and German, about'human' subjects and published between 1980 and 2014, documenting adequate treatment for NXG. Mainly individualcase reports, small case series and retrospective studies were found. Treatment options include topical and systemiccorticosteroids, thalidomide, high-dose intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG), chlorambucil, cyclophosphamide, fludara-bine, rituximab, melphalan, infliximab, interferon alpha, cladribine, hydroxychloroquine, azathioprine, methotrexate, lasertherapy, radiotherapy, surgery, PUVA, plasmapheresis and extracorporeal photopheresis. Randomized controlled trialsand studies on long-term outcomes after treatment were not found and are necessary to focus on in the future.
Received: 9 September 2015; Accepted: 2 May 2016Conflicts of interestNone declared.Funding SourcesNone.IntroductionWith only approximately 100 cases reported in the literature,necrobiotic xanthogranuloma (NXG) is a rarely reported disor-der first described in 1980.1 This unusual, progressive andchronic, non-Langerhans cell histiocytosis is characterized bymultiple indurated asymptomatic yellowish to reddish brownpapules and nodules that slowly develop into large plaquesinvolving the dermis and subcutaneous tissue. Ulceration,telangiectasia, atrophy and pruritus may also be present, and itcommonly appears in the periorbital region (>80%). It is fre-quently associated with haematologic alterations, with the mostcommon being monoclonal gammopathy of the immunoglobu-lin G-kappa type (65%), followed by IgG lambda (35%). Otherreportedassociationscomprisenon-Hodgkinlymphoma,WaldenstrEURom's macroglobulinemia, amyloidosis and myelodys-plastic syndrome. Some patients are diseased by NXG withoutthe presence of paraproteinemia. Nevertheless, all patientsshould be examined for haematological and lymphoproliferative
disorders, as the manifestation may come after the onset of skinmanifestations. Laboratory findings may reveal hypocomple-mentemia, leucopenia, cryoglobulinemia and hyperlipidemia.NXG has no gender propensity and first symptoms of the diseaseoccur frequently in the fifth or sixth decade of life.2-7 NXGinvolves most commonly, the periorbital region (Fig. 1) mani-festing in the skin or in the anterior orbit, leading to proptosis,blepharoptosis and limitation of ocular motility. Vision changes,diplopia, episcleritis, keratitis, iritis, conjunctivitis, cornealperforation, involvement of the extraocular muscles and fat andenlargement of the lacrimal glands may also be present. Lesionsof NXG can appear anywhere and after initially involving theperiorbital area, most commonly, trunk and extremities areaffected. In over 90% of the patients, NXG involves morethan one body site. Giant-cell myocarditis should also beexcluded.2,7-13The pathogenesis of NXG has not yet been fully cleared up,and there are some reports on the occurrence of NXG within a
scar, after trauma or radiation.14-16 A research group whodetected spirochetal microorganisms in NXG, postulated aninfectious aetiology.17 Another study disclosed that NXG withmonoclonal gammopathy is featured by an impaired HDL phe-notype, associated with a single inflammatory pattern, the preva-lenceofaspecificmonocytesubsetandanabnormalmacrophage lipid homoeostasis, perhaps due to interactionsbetween monoclonal immunoglobulin and lipoproteins. How-ever, the answer to the reason why lipid-loaded macrophage cellsusually deposit in the skin without a significant vascular tropismremains unsettled.18The histopathologic diagnosis of NXG can be challenging dueto its occasionally uncharacteristic features and similarities withnecrobiosis lipoidica diabeticorum. An atrophic or normalepidermis may be present and granulomatous inflammation canaffect dermis and subcutaneous tissue. Granulomas are normallyband-like and consist of foamy histiocytes, lymphocytes, foreignbody-type multinucleated giant cells, Touton giant cells withperipheral cytoplasmatic xanthomatous change and areas ofnecrobiosis. Granulomas can be associated with nodular lym-phoid aggregates. Presence of extracellular lipid as cholesterolclefts can be found within the necrobiosis areas and is helpful toexclude other inflammatory granulomatous conditions.4,7,13Management of NXG may be difficult with several treatmentregimens including physical modalities, immunomodulatorydrugs, immunosuppressive agents, high-dose corticosteroids,alkylating agents, plasmapheresis and local radiotherapy showingvariable dermatological responses.10This review wants to summarize the treatment of necrobioticxanthogranuloma of the skin, helping other physicians inthe choice of adequate treatment according to each patient'scondition.
Materials and methodsWe performed a systematic review based on PubMed, Medlineand Web of Science databases using the query 'necrobiotic xan-thogranuloma treatment'. Eligibility of the studies was assessedby two independent reviewers and divergences were arbitratedand resolved by a third experienced reviewer. Our search waslimited to 'English' and 'German' language, 'human' subjectsand publications from 1 January 1980 to 31 December 2014,
documenting adequate treatment for NXG. Furthermore, wemanually searched the references of the retrieved articles for anyadditional studies. We reviewed the reference lists of the full-length articles to identify additional articles meeting the pre-defined inclusion criteria. A total of 90 articles were identifiedfrom the initial search in Pubmed, and 28 additional articleswere found through the manual review. After searching in Med-line and Web of Science databases, one more article was addedto our initial search. After reviewing all full-text articles andexcluding reviews, papers about other diseases and articles with-out reporting on treatment, a total sum of 70 studies, 57 fromthe initial search and 13 from additional papers, were analysed.For each study included, a summary of author/study year, coun-try, study design, number of patients, therapies, results andhaematological or internal malignancies is given in Table 1. Thecase series from Kossard et al.1 were excluded, since we sus-pected that some of the patients are included in the retrospectivestudy from Finan and Winkelmann.19 For the same reason, wedecided to not include the review of Mehregan and Winkelmannet al. from 19928 and the case series from 199820 because thepatient population may be the same as in Ugurlu et al. In theretrospective study from Wood et al.,6 one patient case wasalready published and may have been repeatedly reported. In thestudy from Ugurlu et al.9 five patients were excluded, but wesuspected that some of them may be already included in thestudies from Wood et al. or Finan and Winkelmann.

Treatments were often reported to have beenunsuccessful. The disease course after treatment tends to beprogressive with occurrence of new lesions. Systemic corticosteroids Systemic steroids are not enthusiasti-cally recommended as monotherapy, rather combined, i.e. withalkylating agents, as there is a high relapse rate after treatmentdiscontinuation. Despite this, systemic corticosteroid monother-apy should be considered when other modalities are unsuccess-ful,contraindicatedorifanunderlyinghaematologicalcondition does not exist.32 Chave et al. reported a patientresponding to a high-dose pulsed oral dexamethasone therapywith maintenance with oral prednisolone.33Immunomodulatory drugs These novel agents used in the treat-ment of haematologic diseases are potentially promising inNXG, perhaps due to their wide spectrum effects on the immunesystem. Thalidomide and lenalidomide have been used sepa-rately or combined with dexamethasone showing satisfactoryresults. The length of systemic treatment is not defined and fur-ther reports are needed.13,18,34High-dose intravenous immunoglobulin Known for havingdiverse immunoregulatory effects including decreasing the acti-vation of autoreactive T cells, intravenous immunoglobulin(IVIG) showed rapid therapeutic efficacy with almost completeremission in three patients suffering from NXG with underlyinghaematologicdisease(multiplemyeloma,MGUS).11,35,36High-dose IVIG treatment should be considered when othertreatment modalities have failed.
Alkylating agents (Chlorambucil, cyclophosphamide, melpha-lan, nitrogen mustards) About 50 patients with NXG under-went treatment with alkylating agents either alone or combined,with the most popular combination being Chlorambucil,cyclophosphamide or melphalan with corticosteroids (Table 2).Alkylating agents are currently the most common treatment forNXG. Chlorambucil is usually used in low-doses (2-4 g/day)and in NXG with paraproteinemia. However, cases without asso-ciated haematologic disease treated with Chlorambucil have alsobeen reported.7,37,38 The disadvantages of this treatment are themultiple complications of an increased risk of secondary malig-nancy and bone marrow suppression. Ryan et al. suggested theuse of Chlorambucil as first-line therapy in selected patients withNXG.37 Cyclophosphamide is a potent immunosuppressiveagent, which affects cells in all phases of the cell cycle and causesas main side-effects, nausea, vomiting, alopecia, leucopenia andurotoxicity with haemorrhagic cystitis. Emesis prophylaxis andbladderprotectionwithmesnashouldbeprovided.39-41Cladribine This cytotoxic agent also called 2-Chlorodeoxyade-nosine is a purine nucleoside analogue with high activity in lym-phoidandmyeloidmalignanciesandsomeautoimmunedisorders. Due to the important role ofmacrophages in NXG and the favourable results of infliximab,addressing necrobiosis lipoidica diabeticorum, physicians shouldbe alert to this potential therapy option.48 Another patientshowed significant improvement of skin lesions but treatmentwas discontinued due to repeated infections.49
Other therapies Isolated cases using different treatment sche-mas including rituximab, cyclosporine, fludarabine, bortezomib,vincristine, etretinate, clofazimine and dapsone among othershave been reported.Physical modalitiesLaser therapy The use of Q-switched 650-nm wavelength laserin atypical facial hyperpigmentation associated with NXGresulted in whitening of the skin and no recurrence after a 5-month follow-up.50 Persistence of skin lesions occurred in onepatient despite treatment with Ng-YAG laser26 and greatimprovement was described in a patient with buttock and peri-orbital disease after treatment with dioxide carbon laser.51Char et al., reported a patient successfully treated after 22, 5 Gyirradiation of the left orbit, remaining in remission after 2.5years.52 Radiotherapy may be used preferably in patients withperiorbital affection not carrying a haematologic condition.
Surgery Due to its high recurrence rate (42%) within 6-12 months, surgical removal of NXG lesions is not recommendedoutside the diagnostic biopsy. Besides that, in majority of thecases, the recurrent skin manifestations are larger than the formerones.9 Local management of ulcers and secondary infections isencouraged.2 Five different studies reported successful treatmentshowing no relapse 1 year after surgery in three patients, 2 yearsafter intervention in one patient, 19 months after procedure inone patient and 4 years after in another patient.53-57 Otherauthors propose cycles of melphalan and prednisone after surgeryto maintain remission.58 Mechanisms of recurrence are unclearand relapse of skin disease after surgery mainly occurs duringphases of high disease activity.59 Thus, we recommend surgeryapproach in NXG of the eyelid or limited skin affection inpatients with controlled haematological condition.PUVA therapy Due to the successful use of PUVA treatment inmany necrobiotic conditions, such as granuloma annulare andnecrobiosis lipoidica, its use in NXG was proposed. A recentreport discloses very satisfactory results after 47 treatments ofPUVA using 8-methoxypsoralen in a patient with isolatedNXG.24 Another group reported no therapeutic effect after bath-PUVA in a patient with disseminated NXG associated with IgG-kappa paraproteinemia.42 More studies covering this topic areneeded since this could be a good alternative compared to othersystemic agents.Plasmapheresis Paraproteinemia has been successfully man-aged with plasmapheresis, leading to its rational use in treatmentof NXG. Finelli and Ratz reported a case of NXG associated withan IgG paraproteinemia treated with plasmapheresis, 10% ben-zoyl peroxide and hydroxychloroquine resulting in an almostcomplete healing of skin lesions and improvement of parapro-teinemia. We should not assign the results only to plasmaphere-sis; more cases about this medical procedure in NXG arerequired.60
Extracorporeal photopheresis In Extracorporeal photopheresis(ECP), about 10-20% of the body's leucocytes are isolated fromthe patient's blood, then extracorporally exposed to photoacti-vated 8-methoxypsoralen and reinfused back to the patient.While the exact mechanism of ECP is still not completely under-stood, it seems that exposure of the immune system to physicallymodified T-cell clones may lead to a specific inhibition of T-cell-mediated autoimmunity in diseases where T cells play a majorrole. A recent report showed regression of the skin lesions in apatient with NXG without paraproteinemia during the 2 yearsof receiving ECP. Photopheresis should be considered in patientswith recalcitrant NXG, not responding to other therapies or withother diseases due to its good tolerability and safety when com-bined with other modalities.46,49DiscussionNXG is a chronic, progressive and destructive granulomatousdisease of the skin and extracutaneous tissues. In our sys-tematic review, clinical response or 'results' is mainly based onthe individual report of each author. This makes comparisonbetween studies more difficult and limits the reliability of ourfindings. Therapy of NXG is frequently unsuccessful, althoughmany different modalities have been used. Low successful ratesin treatment of NXG may likely be a reason for the small num-ber of cases published in literature. We encourage authors toreport their cases, whether treatment objectives are accom-plished or not, in order to provide information about whichoptions should be preferred/excluded. Evaluation of therapeuticoutcomes is difficult and information about remission durabilityis scarce. Studies about long-term outcomes after treatment areneeded. Another point of relevance in our work is that patient'sresponse rates may be overestimated, as most authors prefer topublish reports for which some treatment response was accom-plished. Thereby, and since NXG is rare and treatment is diffi-cult, important data may remain concealed. Due to rarity of thecondition, randomized controlled trials are difficult to performand up to now no first-line therapy has been recommended. Wehereby suggest an approach to NXG therapy based on the cur-rent review(Fig. 2).Treatment decision should be made according to diseaseextent, underlying haematologic conditions, related diseases, sizeand location of skin lesions, and side-effects. Physicians shouldadvise patients that some of the proposed treatment modalitiescurrently available, are off label use, and that most of their deci-sions concerning this issue are made based on current casereports and actualized reviews. Randomized clinical trials or N-of-1 trials (CENT) are required in order to define treatment ofchoice.
