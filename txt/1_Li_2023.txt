ORIGINAL ARTICLEComparative efficacy and safety of uricosuric agents in the treatment of gout or hyperuricemia: a systematic review and network meta-analysisYa-Jia Li1 * Li-Rong Chen1 * Zhong-Lei Yang1 * Ping Wang1 * Fang-Fang Jiang1 * Yu Guo1 * Kai Qian1 * Mei Yang1 * Sun-Jun Yin1 * Gong-Hao He1 Received: 4 July 2022 / Revised: 17 August 2022 / Accepted: 20 August 2022 (c) The Author(s), under exclusive licence to International League of Associations for Rheumatology (ILAR) 2022
AbstractObjectives The current world witnesses a greatly increased prevalence and incidence of hyperuricemia and gout with unfortunately the comparative efficacy and safety of present available uricosuric agents remaining uncertain. We herein aimed to investigate the most appropriate uricosuric agent for gout or hyperuricemia patients.Method PubMed, Embase, Cochrane Library databases, and ClinicalTrials.gov from inception to 2 July 2022 were searched to retrieve eligible studies assessing efficacy and safety of uricosuric drugs in hyperuricemia or gout patients. Network meta-analysis was carried out using the Stata 16.0 software.Results Twelve randomized controlled trials comprising 1851 patients were eventually included. Network meta-analysis showed that dotinurad 4 mg once daily, verinurad, dotinurad 2 mg once daily, dotinurad 1 mg once daily, and benzbromarone were the top 5 effective treatments to achieve target serum uric acid. Furthermore, dotinurad 4 mg once daily was more effective at achieving urate-lowering targets (RR of dotinurad 4 mg once daily vs. probenecid: 1.68, 95% CI [1.13; 2.50]) and safer (RR of probenecid vs. dotinurad 4 mg once daily: 1.77, 95% CI [0.69; 4.56]) than probenecid.Conclusions This network meta-analysis demonstrated an important absolute benefit of dotinurad 4 mg once daily to achieve target serum uric acid and low risk of adverse events for drug treatment of gout or hyperuricemia patients. Additionally, verinurad might be used as an alternative uricosuric therapeutic option to dotinurad. These findings provided further comprehensive insight into the treatment value of current uricosuric agents for gout or hyperuricemia.
Key Points1. This is the first systematic review and network meta-analysis examining the efficacy and safety of currently available uricosuric agents in gout or hyperuricemia patients.2. Recommended doses of dotinurad 4mg once daily used for the treatment of gout or hyperuricemia patients can significantly decrease serum uric acid levels.3. The present findings will provide further comprehensive insight into the treatment value of certain uricosuric agents for gout or hyperurice-mia.Keywords Gout * Hyperuricemia * Network meta-analysis * UricosuricIntroductionGout is the most prevalent inflammatory arthritis world-wide. and hyperuricemia may increase the risk of cardiovascular disease and chronic kidney disease, they have become one of the most important cause factors of reduced life qual-ity and increased care cost as well [2-4]. Hence, urate-lowering therapy (ULT) is urgently needed to prevent gout attacks in clinical practice.Pharmacotherapy of ULT can be classified into xan-thine oxidase inhibitors (XOIs) and uricosurics based on pharmacologic mechanism [5]. XOIs such as allopuri-nol or febuxostat are effective for reducing the produc-tion of serum uric acid (sUA), but they are also associ-ated with considerable side effects, which leads to much debate about their safety [6]. Therefore, as an alternative to XOIs, uricosurics have drawn increasing attention from all over the world. Compared with XOIs, uricosurics are also highly effective in lowering sUA levels although they are often used as second-line urate-lowering agents. Ben-zbromarone and probenecid, the representative drugs of uricosurics, were reported to be potent in enhancing renal excretion of sUA [7]. Other uricosuric drugs such as ver-inurad, SHR4640, and dotinurad are undergoing clinical trials that might play important roles in the development of ULT [8]. Therefore, appropriate clinical use of uricosurics would be essential for the optimal treatment of hyperurice-mia and gout.However, although previous studies regarding uricosuric drugs demonstrated their efficacy and safety in urate-lowering therapy, certain major limitations have restricted their extensive clinical application. As a matter of fact, previous studies focused more on the general population of patients with gout or hyperuricemia with few attentions being paid to special populations such as XOI intolerance or allergy patients, which was unable to provide recommendations on how to select ULT agents for these patients [9, 10]. Furthermore, uricosurics in combination with XOIs are increasingly concerned in recent years, which was proved to be efficacious in lowering sUA levels. But the combination therapy approach is also not applicable to XOI intolerance or allergy patients [11, 12]. In addition, previous pairwise meta-analyses evaluated single ULT agents in comparison with standard care [13-18]. Nevertheless, clear research evidence to simultaneously estimate relative efficacy and safety among currently available uricosuric agents is still missing, resulting in little information on a comprehensive ranking of these agents for the treatment of gout or hyperuricemia [19].Based on this background, we herein conducted a network meta-analysis that investigated the most appropriate uricosu-ric agent with high benefits (lowering sUA levels) and low risk of adverse events (AEs) for the abovementioned special populations, hoping to achieve rational and individualized use of uricosurics in the clinical treatment.
Materials and methodsSearch strategyThe PubMed, Embase, Cochrane Library databases, and ClinicalTrials.gov were searched from their inception to 2 July 2022. According to the search principle of PICOS (participants, interventions, comparisons, outcomes, and study design), we conducted a search strategy that combined the free text and medical subject heading terms of the following keywords: "hyperuricemia," "gout," "uricosuric" (Supplementary Table S1). We also checked reference lists of relevant reviews and eligible studies to ensure a comprehensive search. There was no restriction of language or year of publication.Inclusion criteria and exclusion criteriaStudies meeting the following criteria were included: (a) participants (>= 18 years) were diagnosed with hyperurice-mia with or without gout; (b) intervention group taken one of the seven uricosuric agents (probenecid, benzbromarone, lesinurad, dotinurad, verinurad, arhalofenate, or SHR4640); (c) comparison group taken placebo or another uricosuric agent different from the treatment group; (d) outcomes of interest were reported; and (e) study design was randomized controlled trial (RCT), cohort study, or case-control study. The exclusion criteria were as follows: (a) studies that com-bined uricosuric agents with XOIs; (b) studies that included pregnant woman, lactating women, or patients with severe comorbid conditions; (c) studies that without a designated intervention or comparator arm; and (d) not human research.Data extraction and quality assessment
Two investigators (L.R.C. and Y.J.L.) independently extracted all data according to a data extraction form. The extracted information mainly included the first author's name, the publication year, basic characteristics of patients (gender, age, and baseline serum concentration of uric acid), treatment protocol, treatment duration (months), and study design. In the present study, the primary efficacy outcome was defined as the percentage of patients achieving serum concentration of uric acid <= 6.0 mg/dL at the final visit. The primary safety outcome was defined as the incidence of any AEs during the period of the trial (including abnormal liver function, renal impairment, rash, gastrointestinal disorders, joint-related signs). Besides, the secondary outcomes were the incidence of gouty arthritis and gouty flares.After extracting the data, the methodological quality was assessed by using the Cochrane bias risk assessment tool for included studies. The certainty of network evidence was graded by using the Grade of Recommendations Assessment, Development, and Evaluation (GRADE) for primary outcomes. Any disagreement between the two investigators was resolved by discussion and consensus. This systematic review and network meta-analysis is reported following the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-analyses (PRISMA) extension statement for network meta-analysis [20].
Statistical analysesWe performed random-effects pairwise meta-analyses and a network meta-analysis in a frequentist framework to obtain network estimates for the aforementioned out-comes. Before running the network meta-analysis, we first assessed the transitivity assumption to explore whether basic characteristics were sufficiently similar between comparisons by applying narrow inclusion criteria. Sec-ondly, we evaluated the heterogeneity by conducting a pairwise meta-analysis to generate direct estimates for primary outcomes. Finally, we evaluated inconsistency assumption (the statistical disagreement between direct and indirect evidence in a closed loop) locally using a loop specific approach, and globally using a design by treatment interaction model. After the three assumptions above were assessed, we used surface under the cumulative ranking (SUCRA) to rank the efficacy and safety of intervention in the network meta-analysis. We also performed sensitivity analyses to assess the robustness of the efficacy and safety outcomes by excluding studies at high risk of bias. In addi-tion, we compared the results of the base case analysis with "all missing failure" analysis to explore the impact of missing data; this analysis assumed that all missing patients had a negative event. To explore potential sources of publication bias, we assessed the presence of small-trial effects for primary outcomes with the comparison adjusted funnel plot.We calculated risk ratios (RRs) for binary outcomes and presented with 95% confidence intervals (95% CIs) in the pairwise meta-analysis and network meta-analysis. All analyses were done in Stata 16.0 software using the metan and network packages for the pairwise and network meta-analysis, respectively.Ethical approval and informed consentAll results and analyses were based on previous ethically approved studies; thus, for this type of study, formal consent is not required.
ResultsCharacteristics of eligible studiesOur systematic search identified 1321 citations published between 1979 and 2022. Finally, we included 12 studies [21-32] that related to 12 RCTs in the network meta-analysis. The selection process and exclusion reasons are shown in Supplementary Figure S1, and the characteristics of included studies are presented in Table 1. The publica-tions were from 2008 to 2022, and 58.33% (n = 7) of stud-ies were conducted in Asia. The study sample sizes ranged from 14 to 319 patients, while patient age ranged from 26 to 75 years, 96.87% were male. The duration of follow-up ranged from 1 to 8.5 months. Distribution of baseline char-acteristics was generally balanced in the included studies. In the present study, there was one five-arm study that compared dotinurad (0.5/1/2/4 mg once daily) with pla-cebo [22]. Six studies had a low overall risk of bias [22, 24, 27-29, 31]. Five studies had a moderate overall risk owing to other bias [21, 23, 26, 30, 32]. DiscussionTo our knowledge, we conducted the first network meta-analysis investigating uricosuric agents for efficacy and safety in special gout or hyperuricemia patients. We ana-lyzed 13 uricosuric interventions reported in 12 RCTs with 1851 participants. Overall, our results demonstrated that dotinurad 4 mg once daily provided higher efficacy and safety than other uricosuric agents in gout or hyperuricemia patients. As the present study was based on available uri-cosurics data, these findings may also be applied to special populations such as XOI intolerance or allergy patients and provide further comprehensive insight into the treatment value of certain uricosuric agents for gout or hyperuricemia that might be largely neglected previously.According to a 2014 Cochrane review, uricosurics could be considered as second-line agents in the treatment of gout [33]. As a result, both the 2016 European League Against Rheumatism (EULAR) guideline and the 2020 American College of Rheumatology (ACR) guideline recommended to use uricosurics when gout or hyperuricemia patients were unable to tolerate or had contraindications to XOIs [34, 35]. However, these guidelines were mainly based on certain pre-vious investigations that were limited by small sample sizes, uncontrolled trial designs, and lack of systematic clinical evidence regarding their benefit and safety [36-39]. As a matter of fact, considering that renal underexcretion of uric acid is the most frequent mechanism for the development of 
hyperuricemia and gout [10], uricosuric agents would have much wider application prospects in terms of their mecha-nism of action [40] and the potential treatment value of uri-cosurics might largely be neglected. In this regard, findings of the present network meta-analysis provided relatively large sample size and much more comprehensive evidence that may be used to inform further clinical decision-making in prevention and treatment of gout or hyperuricemia.In the present study, we specifically evaluated each uri-cosuric agents to determine the most suitable treatment for gout or hyperuricemia, which suggested that dotinurad 4 mg once daily was more efficacious and safer than other uricosu-ric agents for achieving sUA target. Therefore, dotinurad can be considered as a promising therapeutic option in combina-tion to XOIs if the treatment with XOI monotherapy fails to achieve target sUA. Furthermore, dotinurad might also be regarded as a potent uricosuric agent in the development of future evidence-based clinical practice guidelines for gout or hyperuricemia. This uricosuric treatment option will help to further improve the management of gout or hyperuricemia patients if used appropriately although further large-scale experiments and clinical studies are still needed to verify the present findings.As for other uricosurics, the efficacy of verinurad was second only to dotinurad and its AE incidence was lower than dotinurad according to the present results, which might also be employed as an ideal uricosuric agent. However, although probenecid was the most widely used drug in gout or hyperuricemia patients in current clini-cal practice [41, 42], it did not show high efficiency in lowering sUA levels and ranked the worst in safety in the present study, which might mainly be ascribed to its wide interactions with other coadministered drugs [42]. In this regard, probenecid should be used cautiously with par-ticular care especially for gout or hyperuricemia patients simultaneously administered with other drugs [43]. Addi-tionally, benzbromarone showed moderate efficacy but also had relatively high risks of AEs, such as gout flares. As benzbromarone had broad application in clinic, further investigations regarding its clinical safety are still greatly warranted. In general, these findings provided advanta-geous evidence on the optimal option of uricosuric agents although more individualized treatment decisions should be made based on weight of the benefits against the AEs and costs of these therapy options to promote the compre-hensive development of ULT.Our findings have the following limitations. Firstly, the available data for many comparisons are based on few stud-ies (in some cases just one or two studies), leading to rela-tively thinly connected networks and low statistical power to detect possible differences. Secondly, although we applied stringent inclusion criteria and identified no clear transitiv-ity problems, there was heterogeneity for some outcomes. Therefore, the results should be interpreted with caution. Thirdly, due to limited original data, we were unable to per-form subgroup analysis according to hyperuricemia type. Furthermore, most of the studies were of short duration (< 12 weeks) and the assessment of only short-term effects might overemphasize the therapeutic effects of uricosuric agents without considering special interest and serious AEs such as uric acid stone. Thus, the effects of long-term treat-ment require further investigation. Therefore, further studies, especially large-scale double-blind randomization trials, are required to provide more credible data for uricosuric agent treatment in gout or hyperuricemia.
ConclusionThe results of this network meta-analysis demonstrate an important absolute benefit of dotinurad 4 mg once daily to achieve target sUA and low risk of AEs for drug treatment of gout or hyperuricemia patients. Additionally, verinurad might be used as an alternative therapeutic option to dotinu-rad for second-line management of uricosuric agents.
