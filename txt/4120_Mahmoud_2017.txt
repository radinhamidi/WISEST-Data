Efficacy and Safety of Rituximab in the Management of PediatricSystemic Lupus Erythematosus: A Systematic ReviewInes Mahmoud, PhD1, Manel Jellouli, MD2, Imen Boukhris, PhD3, Rim Charfi, MD4, Aicha Ben Tekaya, MD1, Olfa Saidane, MD1,Maryem Ferjani, MD2, Yousra Hammi, MD2, Sameh Trabelsi, PhD4, Narjess Khalfallah, PhD3, Rawdha Tekaya, PhD1,Tahar Gargah, PhD2, and Leila Abdelmoula, PhD1
Objectives To evaluate the efficacy and safety of rituximab for treating pediatric systemic lupus erythematosus(pSLE).Study design We performed a systematic review to evaluate the efficacy and safety of rituximab in children withpSLE. Data from studies performed before July 2016 were collected from MEDLINE, the Cochrane Library, Scopus,and the International Rheumatic Disease Abstracts, with no language restrictions. Study eligibility criteria includedclinical trials and observational studies with a minimal sample size of 5 patients, regarding treatment with rituximabin patients with refractory pSLE (aged <18 years at the time of diagnosis). Independent extraction of articles wasperformed by 2 investigators using predefined data fields.Results Twelve case series met the criteria for data extraction for the systematic review with a good quality as-sessment according to an 18-criteria checklist using a modified Delphi method. Among them, 3 studies were mul-ticenter and 3 were prospective. The total number of patients was 272. Studies collected patients with active diseaserefractory to steroids and immunosuppressant drugs. Refractory lupus nephritis was the most common indication(33%). Acceptable evidence suggested improvements in renal, neuropsychiatric and haematological manifesta-tions, disease activity, complement and anti-double stranded Desoxy-Nucleo-Adenosine, with a steroid-sparing effect.However, there was poor evidence suggesting efficacy on arthralgia, photosensitivity, and mucocutaneous mani-festations of SLE in children. An overall acceptable safety profile with few major adverse events was shown.Conclusion Rituximab exhibited a satisfactory profile regarding efficacy and safety indicating that this agent is apromising therapy for pSLE and should be further investigated. he incidence of pediatric systemic lupus erythematosus (pSLE) is 0.3-0.9/100 000 children per year, with a prevalenceof 3.3-8.8/100 000 children.1 pSLE is associated with more severe and active disease compared with systemic lupus ery-thematosus (SLE) in adults. In particular, there is a higher incidence of renal and central nervous system involvement.2,3Immunosuppressive therapies are widely used but fail to show efficacy in severe forms of the disease.B-lymphocyte function is a major component of SLE pathogenesis.4,5 Rituximab (RTX) is a chimeric monoclonal IgG1 kappaantibody that binds specifically to the CD20 antigen and mediates B-cell depletion, thereby preventing the renewal of autoan-tibodies and antigen presentation by pathogenic B cells.6,7 Recent reports have suggested the use of RTX for patients with SLEwho are refractory to standard treatment approaches; however, RTX therapy has not been approved for this indication.8 To un-derstand and interpret the available evidence, we conducted a systematic review to evaluate the efficacy and safety of RTX therapyin children with pSLE.MethodsThis systematic review was conducted in accordance with the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) guidelines. The search strategy was performed in the digital databases of MEDLINE, Scopus, Web of Knowl-edge, and the Cochrane Library. We also searched the electronic databases of the American Society of Rheumatology, the EuropeanLeague Against Rheumatic Disease, and the French Society of Rheumatology. Weperformed a hand search by reviewing the references of the included studies. Wesearched all databases from their earliest records up to July 2016. Keywordsincluded terms and synonyms for RTX, lupus, and child.

RTX Administration and Disease IndicationRefractory lupus nephritis was the most common indicationfor RTX therapy (7 studies). Otherwise, children were treatedfor various disease manifestations, including cytopenia (5studies), vasculitis (3 studies), arthritis (3 studies), serositis(2 studies), neuropsychiatric disease (4 studies), skin disor-ders (2 studies), pulmonary hypertension (2 studies), ophtalmicdisorders (1 study), and general symptoms (1 study). RTXdosage data were reported in 11 studies (264 patients). Therewas wide heterogeneity in reported RTX regimens. Themost common regimen was RTX infusion at a dose of375 mg/m2/week.10-12,14,17,18,20 The number of doses per courseranged from 2 to 4, and the number of courses ranged from1 to 12. Four studies reported a RTX dose of 750 mg/m2(maximum 1 g) given as 2 intravenous infusions separatedby approximately 14 days.9,13,15,19 Watson et al15 reported thehighest number of courses (n = 104). Regarding concomi-tant therapies, 7 studies9,11-13,15,19,20 reported patients with severedisease receiving RTX combined with intravenous cyclophos-phamide. In most studies, glucocorticoids and nonbiologicalimmunosuppressive therapy were associated. Table III pres-ents indications for RTX and concomitant treatments.

SafetyTen studies reported adverse events in a total of 78patients.9-13,15,17-20 The most frequent adverse events were in-fusion reaction (n = 14) and viral infections (n = 10). Twenty-two patients experienced a serious adverse event, includingsevere cytopenia (n = 11), central nervous vasculitis (n = 1),and life-threatening infusion reaction (n = 2). Six patients de-veloped severe infections that could be associated with RTX,including 2 patients with opportunistic infection.10,11,17,20 Olfatet al11 reported persistent hypogammaglobulinemia in severalpatients and progressive interstitial lung disease in 1 patient.DiscussionIn this systematic review, we assessed the efficacy and safetyof RTX in the management of pSLE. Although numerous clini-cal trials of biological therapy have been conducted in adultswith SLE,22 few studies have been conducted in children, andnone of these were randomized controlled trials.RTX has been shown to be safe and effective for treating therenal and hematologic manifestations of pSLE, especially interms of disease activity, immunologic measures, and steroid-sparing effect. Few published studies have focused on arthral-gia, photosensitivity, and mucocutaneous involvement of SLEin children, and those found little evidence of efficacy.There was a wide heterogeneity in RTX regimens. Age andsex had no apparent effects on the pharmacokinetics of RTX.6Relapse prevention is a major goal of treatment for SLE. Re-viewing the efficacy of RTX in nonrenal adult SLE, relapseswere reported in many studies, but the severity of relapses wasmilder, rates of relapses were fewer, and time to relapse waslonger in the patients who received RTX therapy.22 Resis-tance to B-cell depletion by RTX may be related to antibod-ies against the human-mouse chimeric protein.23 It also maybe genetically influenced by polymorphisms of the FcRIIIa gene,which is responsible for the Fc-g receptor required for RTXbinding to phagocytes.24
In our review, 33% of patients had nephritis before receiv-ing RTX therapy. Therapeutic options included hydroxy-chloroquine,corticosteroids,cyclophosphamide,mycophenolatemofetil, azathioprine, and methotrexate in both adult andchildren.8,25,26 Treatment failure was reported in up to 43% ofAugust 2017ORIGINAL ARTICLESpatients in some studies.27,28 In addition, even if remission isattained with immunosuppressive drugs,nephritis relapse canoccur in 35% of responders and seems to be a determiningfactor in end-stage kidney disease in children.28 RTX therapyreduced proteinuria even before immune changes becameevident.This reduction seemed to be induced by glomeruli re-modeling owing to RTX binding to podocyte membranes.29In adult lupus nephritis, the Lupus Nephritis Assessmentwith Rituximab trial tested RTX as a first-line therapy andfound negative results. Also in adults, the Exploratory PhaseII/III SLE Evaluation of Rituximab trial, a randomized trial ofRTX therapy in nonrenal lupus, reported negative results aswell.5 RTX is not a suitable treatment for moderate muscu-loskeletal,mucocutaneous,or general SLE,in which the diseasemanifestations are poorly correlated with SLE-related auto-antibodies. For instance, anti-Ro (SSA) and anti-La (SSB)antibodies were associated with cutaneous lupus and photo-sensitivity but were not down-regulated by RTX.Although primary endpoints were not attained in theLUNAR and EXPLORER trials, the clinical benefit of RTX inSLE has been noted in daily clinical practice and in open-label and retrospective studies.22,30
In this systematic review, RTX seemed to be effective in termsof a steroid-sparing effect. Most studies showed that childrenwith pSLE tended to have more severe disease and worse health-related quality of life scores compared with adults. Com-pared with adults, children with SLE were at increased risk fornephritis, received more intensive immunosuppressive thera-pies, and had more adverse events related to glucocorticoidtherapy.8,31-35 Reviewing the efficacy of RTX for treating adultSLE suggested sufficient evidence of the effectiveness of RTXtherapy in reducing steroid doses.In our review, RTX appeared to be safe. Adverse events werenoted in 21% of patients. In adults, adverse events were re-ported in a systematic review of 1 RCT (LUNAR trial),5 2 openstudies, and 20 cohort studies.22 The most frequent adverseevents were infusion reactions and infections. The LUNAR trialreported no differences compared with placebo in terms ofoverall adverse events, infections, or serious infections, but dif-ferences in terms of infusion reactions, neutropenia, and her-pesvirus infection.5 The incidence of serious infections wasestimated at 6.6-12.6/100 patient-years.5,36,37 #The authors express their sincere thanks to Asma Hammami for herEnglish expertise and the improvements she made to the manuscript.Submitted for publication Oct 7, 2016; last revision received Jan 31, 2017;accepted May 1, 2017Reprint requests: Ines Mahmoud, PhD, Charles Nicolle Hospital, ManarUniversity, Boulevard 9 Avril, 1006 Bab Souika, Tunis, Tunisia.
