MED-LINE, Cochrane, LILACS and SciELO databases were scanned, and the Abstractsfrom Brazilian, American and European Neurology and Stroke Congresses werescrutinized for clinical trials. Moreover, the reference lists of articles and reviews weresearched. A pooled analysis of two large studies with aspirin was made. Both un-fractionated heparin and low molecular weight heparins/heparinoids (LMWH) arepartially effective for VTE prophylaxis after CI, and should be routinely used inpatients with motor deficit and reduced mobility and no contraindications. Reductionof deep venous thrombosis is better established than the effect over pulmonaryembolism or mortality. Some evidence points to a greater efficacy of LMWH. Theavailable evidence does not support the use of mechanical methods or dextran. Aspirinmay have a mild protective effect. Low-dose Warfarin might be useful in the reha-bilitation setting. Strict recommendations cannot be made in patients with haemor-rhagic stroke but intermittent pneumatic compression merits further study. There areimportant limitations of current VTE preventive strategies following stroke. Addi-tional studies on the combination of methods after CI and of low doses of anti-coagulants following cerebral haemorrhage are urgently needed.
IntroductionImmobility is the most important risk factor for venousthromboembolism (VTE) [1]. It is hence not surprisingthat VTE constitutes one of the most important com-plications of all types of stroke. Deep venous throm-bosis(DVT)isfrequentlydetected(15-80%)inprospective studies using fibrinogen uptake or venousDuplex-scan in patients with cerebral infarcts (CI)without prophylaxis. Although asymptomatic DVTmay have long-term consequences such as the post-phlebitic syndrome and a predisposition for futurerecurrent VTE, its clinical relevance in the acute settingis doubtful, especially when confined to the calf veins.Most studies show that pulmonary embolism (PE)seems to be much more common in patients withproximal and symptomatic DVT [1]. Studies evaluatingonly clinically evident DVT report consistently lowerrates - between 0.2-10%. However, many of thesestudies did not evaluate suspected VTE events pro-
spectively and are probably clear underestimates oftheir true incidence. The same restriction applies to thereported 0.2-5.7% frequency of PE in acute strokepatients [2-6].The best strategy to prevent DVT and PE afterstroke is not well established. The use of antithrom-botic drugs seems to reduce the frequency of DVTand PE but is counterbalanced by an increased riskof extra and intracranial bleeding. Aspirin should beroutinely used soon after CI but its role as anadjunctive method to prevent VTE is not clear.Mechanical methods are frequently used in patientsconsidered to be at high risk of bleeding but thescientific support for their recommendation is at leastfragile. Recent guidelines and reviews recommend theuse of low doses of unfractionated heparin (UFH) orof low molecular weight heparins (LMWH) but alsostate that this practice is associated with some risk ofsystemicorintracranialbleeding,whichmaybecatastrophic [7-9].The purpose of this study is to systematically re-view clinical studies of VTE prophylaxis in strokepatients. The emphasis is in acute CI but we alsoanalysed studies addressing the rehabilitation phase ofthe disease and patients with acute cerebral haemor-rhage (CH) or subarachnoid haemorrhage. We performed a systematic review of the literature onthromboprophylaxis in stroke. Articles were includedonly if they evaluated one or more active treatmentsversus placebo, no intervention or other treatmentmodalities in adult (minimum 18 years) stroke patients.Clinical trials and systematic reviews were searched for inMEDLINE, COCHRANE, LILACS and SciELO dat-abases. The search strategy was developed for MED-LINE (available from the authors upon request) andadapted for the other databases. Studies presented inNeurological and Cerebrovascular Diseases Congressesand only published in abstract form from 1998 to August2004 were also searched for in the following journals:Stroke, Cerebrovascular Diseases, Circulation, Journal ofNeurology, Neurology, Arquivos de Neuro-psiquiatria.The reference lists of all included articles and other per-tinent published articles and reviews were also scrutin-ized in search for additional clinical studies of interest.Two authors (CA and GRF) independently reviewedall titles and abstracts of the initially found studies forfurther assessment. Unfractionated heparin versus placeboSeven studies directly compared UFH and placebo inCI or CH patients [5,17-22]. There is also a systematicreview evaluating most of these studies [2].All clinical studies (most with small numbers andnon-significant differences found) showed trends fordecreased incidence of VTE in acute CI patients, with adramatic reduction of DVT with UFH 5000 IU t.i.d. -incidence of 22% in treated patients versus 73% in theplacebo group - and on the combined rate for DVT, PEand death clearly demonstrated in the largest publishedstudy to date [20]. In general, the effect on DVT is moreclearly shown than that on PE. This was confirmed inthe published meta-analysis [2].Two trials evaluated patients with CH [21,22]. Allhave major limitations. One study compared UFH(5000 IU every 8 h) initiated 4 or 10 days after symp-tom onset in only 46 patients [21]. There were no sig-nificant differences in the rates of DVT (1 patients vs. 2patients), PE (5 vs. 9) or rebleeding (1 vs. 2) as evalu-ated in the 10th day. The same group later reportedsimilar findings with no bleeding complications in pa-tients receiving UFH from the second day on [23]. Theother study (only published in abstract form) retro-spectively evaluated clinical DVT in 458 patients [22].UFH (2500-5000 IU b.i.d.) was given to 200 patients(no details on the time elapsed from symptom totreatment onset). Only one patient (in the controlgroup) developed DVT in the whole series and re-bleeding was also exceptional (one case in the treatmentgroup). Taken together, these studies suggest the risk ofbleeding is not prohibitively high, but no significanteffect on VTE rates was detected.Low molecular weight heparins and heparinoids versusplaceboSeven studies evaluated LMHW (essentially fraxiparin)or the heparinoid Orgaran against placebo [3,6,24-28].There is an additional meta-analysis published and
evaluating many aspects of the use of LMWH/hepari-noids in acute stroke patients [29]. All but one study[27] revealed trends for reduced rates of DVT or PE intreated patients. The low number of haemiparetic pa-tients included in studies systematically screening forDVT limits their power for final conclusions.Studies with no prospective evaluation support theidea of a reduction of PE and DVT in patients withreduced mobility. However, these relatively large stud-ies point against the use of LMWH/heparinoids inunselected populations of CI patients because of theincreased risk of systemic and intracranial bleeding.The same conclusion is derived from the publishedmeta-analysis [29].Lowmolecularweightheparins/heparinoidsversusunfractionated heparinFour clinical trials comparing LMWH or heparinoids(Orgaran) with UFH [30-33] and one meta-analysis ofinterest [34] were found. There is a non-significant trendsuggesting increased efficacy of LMWH/heparinoids forthe prevention of DVT in CI patients with limitedmobility. However, the number of deaths, PE episodesand bleeding episodes are low and do not allow for afirm conclusion on a differential impact of the twoclasses of drugs over these more important clinicalendpoints. There is no proof that the effect over DVT inunselected patients with acute CI is translated into re-duced mortality or PE events and in fact there is asignificant increase of extracranial bleeding episodesand a tendency towards increased risk of intracranialbleedings and mortality [34].
AspirinAspirin is the only drug whose routine use has beenconfirmed to reduce mortality and early (14 days)stroke recurrence rates in unselected patients with acuteCI [4,5,35]. However, its use is not usually considered areasonable single strategy for the prevention of VTE inthis setting. Four studies - two large studies withoutprospective evaluation [4,5], one with insufficient datareported [36] and one observational [16] - suggest amodest beneficial effect on DVT and PE rates, with verylarge numbers of patients needed to treat in order toprevent a single episode. The observational study sug-gested that aspirin plus IPC is not effective [16]. Aspirinalso seemed ineffective in a large retrospective study ofpatients undergoing rehabilitation for stroke [37].We performed a pooled analysis of data from twolarge prospective trials evaluating the effects of aspirinin patients with acute CI (Fig. 1) [4,5]. No prospectiveevaluation for VTE was made in these studies. PEevents were clinically diagnosed, with no formal pre-definition of diagnostic criteria; and physicians were 2006 EFNS European Journal of Neurology 14, 21-32
aware of the allocation of patients either to aspirin orno treatment. Despite these limitations and the relat-ively low number of reported events, the results of thepooled analysis strongly suggest a mild protective effectof aspirin against VTE or at least PE, its most severemanifestation. There was a significant reduction of thefrequency of any apparent PE [69/20055 (3.4%) vs. 97(4.8%) in 20035 controls - RR 0.71 (0.52-0.97)] and anon-significant reduction of fatal PE [39 patients(1.9%) vs. 56 controls (2.8%) - RR 0.70 (0.46-1.05)].However, it should be remembered that the beneficialeffects of aspirin on DVT prophylaxis were counter-balanced by a significant rise in the rate of majorextracranial haemorrhages (fatal or requiring transfu-sion) - RR 1.69 (1.35-2.13) and a non-significant in-crease in the rate of CH - RR 1.22 (0.95-1.56).Low molecular weight heparins versus aspirinTwo studies directly comparing aspirin and LMWH -tinzaparin and dalteparin - in acute CI patients and onemeta-analysis were found [38-40]. One study specific-ally evaluated patients with presumed cardio-embolicstrokes related to atrial fibrillation [38]. The primaryaim of these studies was to evaluate mortality andstroke recurrence (non-significant differences in both)and VTE was not evaluated systematically. However,analysis of pooled data from these studies clearly indi-cates a reduction of symptomatic DVT episodes and arise in major extracranial bleeding complications inpatients receiving LMWH [40].
Other antithrombotic drugsWe found three additional studies of interest. One smalltrial did not show significant differences between CIpatients receiving dextran and controls - 54% vs. 50%[41]. A non-significant reduction of fatal PE was found- 1 episodes vs. 3 episodes.The other controlled study was well-conducted butonly included patients in the rehabilitation phase fol-lowing CI and was flawed by a significant number oflosses to final evaluation [42]. Use of Warfarin in fixedlow dose (2 mg) was associated with a non-significantreduction of any DVT, proximal and clinically detectedDVT but not with any bleeding complication. The au-thors suggest that this fixed low dose is almost neverassociated with a prothrombin time/international nor-malized rate (INR) above 2.0 and that a single INRmeasurement at the end of the first week of treatment isenough to warrant safety with this regimen.Moreover, in the rehabilitation setting, a large ret-rospective study suggested that patients with reducedmobility secondary to CI or CH and who underwentoral or parenteral full anticoagulation exhibit a lowerrate of VTE episodes when compared with thosereceiving SC low-dose LMWH or antiplatelet drugs[37]. Although the results of this non-randomized studyshould be taken with reservations, an increased risk ofbleeding complications could not be detected with anyof the multiple antithrombotic strategies studied.Discussion
In patients with reduced mobility, stroke is stronglyassociated with an increased risk of VTE. Differentseries prospectively evaluating VTE with ultrasound orfibrinogen scan reveal an incidence between 15% and80%, with higher figures in the side of paralysis and insevere cases [12-14,16-18,20,36,43,44]. Studies evalu-ating only clinically evident DVT find much lowernumbers (0.2-10%) [19,22,37,45] that are clear under-estimates of the true incidence. Even patients underdifferent forms of prophylaxis may be at risk (0-46%)[12,15,16,36]. A recent study using direct magneticresonance imaging of thrombus confirmed a high inci-dence of any VTE (40%), proximal DVT (18%) and PE(12%) in unselected patients with acute CI [16]. Mostcases were asymptomatic or unrecognized: DVT andPE were recognized in only 1% and 3% of patientsrespectively, with many other clinically evident casesnot recognized by the attending physicians.The central role of reduced mobility in the patho-genesis of DVT is highlighted by consistently higherrates in the paretic limbs [33,46]. Additional risk factors havenow been identified and include severity of the illness,age, dehydration along the hospitalization period,increasing time between stroke onset and the intro-duction of preventive measures, haemorrhagic stroke,cryptogenic ischaemic stroke [33,44,47-52]. Currentguidelines and reviews also mention other factorsincluding previous VTE, morbid obesity and knownpro-thrombotic conditions including contraceptive use(Table 2) [7-9].We confirmed that there is strong scientific evidencethat low-dose UFH and LMWH are partially effectivein the prevention of DVT after acute CI. In mostprospective studies with systematic search for VTEepisodes, between 2 and 10 patients need to be treated
for one episode being prevented (Table 3). The doseregimen used in diverse studies is remarkably variable.UFH is usually given in two or three daily SC doses of5000 IU, with no obvious differences in efficacy orbleeding risk. Many LMWH formulae are availablewith most studies evaluating 60-175 IU/kg/day SCinjections. A typical regimen would consist in 55-100 IU/kg/day given in one or two SC injections.Higher doses tend to be associated with more markedpreventive effect but also an increased risk of bleedingcomplications.However, the associated risks seem to overcome anypotential benefit when unselected CI patients are trea-ted. Patients without reduced mobility - usually relatedto lower limb paresis - should not be routinely givenlow-dose anticoagulants, as their VTE risk is probablyvery low and they may still suffer bleeding complica-tions.Mechanical methods, although favoured by some,are not effective in acute stroke patients with IC andreduced mobility [53]. Even in patients receiving low-dose aspirin, IPC is associated with a high frequency ofVTE [16]. The use of IPC devices in patients with clearcontra-indications for anticoagulant use - increasedbleeding risk in CI patients, patients with CH or sub-arachnoid haemorrhage - has not been adequatelystudied and its recommendation is based mainly in ex-pert opinion. There is now some evidence that IPC pluselastic stockings may be more effective in the reductionof asymptomatic DVT (in both distal and proximalveins) in acute CH patients than the use of elasticstockings only [11]. However, even inpatients that cannot be initially treated with low-doseanticoagulants the bleeding risks and the possibility ofinstituting them should be frequently reassessed alongthe hospital stay.The use of low-dose UFH after CH was notassociated with significant bleeding risk in low qualityclinical series [21-23]. However, the impact over VTEfrequency has been non-significant with very low ratesdetected in treated and control groups. The lowincidence of VTE in these trials is not in perfectagreement with figures from other studies. Patientswith CH may in fact be at higher risk for DVT thanCI patients. A retrospective cohort study revealed aRR for clinical DVT after CH of 4.0 (1.8% vs. 0.4%)[50]. The RR for PE was 3.5 (0.4% vs. 0.1%). Datacomparing patients with CH included in the IST withCI patients also suggest an increased risk of clinicalPE - 1.3% vs. 0.7% (P  1/4 0.06) - even after con-trolling for severity of the disease [48]. There isclearly a need for additional and well-designed trialsin this population.Unlike most reviews, we also evaluated the possiblerole of aspirin as a VTE preventive strategy. We founda modest preventive effect of aspirin over clinical orfatal PE when pooled data from 40 090 patients inclu-ded in IST and CAST were evaluated. Although thequality of the data is necessarily poor as neither studyprospectively evaluated PE or DVT and physicians andpatients were not blinded to treatment allocation,aspirin use was associated with a significant reductionof the frequency of any apparent PE and fatal PE.However, the clinical relevance of these findings isquestionable. This can be seen when number needed totreat are calculated - 714 (376-6946) for any PE and>1000 for fatal PE. Moreover, aspirin plus IPC seemedto be ineffective in a single observational study [16]. Thepossible harm (increase in severe extracranial haemor-rhages and possibly CH) associated with aspirin useshould also be taken account when deciding to use thedrug for VTE prophylaxis or reduction of early recur-rence.
The present systematic review includes a number oforiginal features including a thorough review of theBrazilian literature on the subject and the search ofdatabases usually absent in other reports. The reviewalso includes studies only published in abstract form.Even recognizing that some of these studies may havenon-evident methodological problems, as they were notsubmitted in complete form to peer reviewed scientificmagazines, the inclusion of these studies is consideredimportant to secure the completeness of the search [55].Some particulars of the present study should beacknowledged. No unpublished study was included.Pooled analysis of data was only made for the use ofaspirin. We believe meta-analyses should only be per-formed when there is adequate comparability of thetrials [55] and this was not the case with many questionshere addressed. We did not exclude studies with noprospective evaluation of VTE episodes and preferredinstead to classify the studies as to their general scien-tific validity. Lastly, we did not evaluate potential dif-ferences between diverse formulations of LMWH orbetween them and heparinoids.In conclusion, thrombo-prophylaxis following acutestroke seems far from ideal. A number of importantquestions remain unsolved and open to research. Whatis the role of IPC in association with antithrombotics -aspirin or low-dose anticoagulants? What are the risksand potential benefits with the association of aspirinand low-dose anticoagulants? When (if ever) shouldlow-dose UFH or LMWH be safely initiated in CHpatients? Additional unanswered questions pertain tothe management of CI patients with very high risk fordevelopment of VTE (under contraceptive use or withmarked obesity, congestive heart failure, disseminatedmalignancy, previous VTE) or of bleeding (e.g. imme-diately following administration of thrombolytics); theduration of antithrombotic prophylaxis in patients withreduced mobility (should they still receive the drugsafter discharge? What degree of recovered mobility isassociated with a decreased risk?); and the role of di-verse regimens of parenteral or oral anticoagulants inthe rehabilitation phase. All these questions can only beanswered by future adequately planned and sufficientlylarge clinical trials clearly distinguishing the effects overasymptomatic calf vein DVT and outcomes of greaterclinical relevance such as PE and proximal and symp-tomatic DVT.
