ABSTRACTObjectives: This paper aims to review the evidence to support the effectiveness of sympathectomy as atreatment for facial blushing in terms of relief of facial blushing, patient satisfaction, recurrence ofblushing, patients regretting treatment and its associated complications.Methods: A systematic search strategy was performed in Ovid-Medline, Embase, Cochrane library andNICE. Studies reporting outcomes of sympathetic interruption in the treatment of facial blushing wereretrieved.Results: Nine studies met the inclusion criteria with 1369 patients included in the final analysis. Theage range of patients was 8 to 74 years (from 7 studies) with 56% females. Mean follow up was21 months in 8 studies (range 6 to 30 months). The pooled proportion of patients who had goodrelief of facial blushing was 78.30% (95% C.I. 58.20% - 98.39%). Complete satisfaction was reportedin 84.02% (95% C.I. 71.71% - 96.33%). Compensatory sweating and gustatory sweating were thecommonest complications occurring in 74.18% (95% C.I. 58.10% - 90.26%) and 24.42% (95% C.I.12.22% - 36.61%) respectively. The estimated proportion of patients regretting surgery was 6.79%(C.I 2.08% 11.50%).Conclusion: Sympathetic interruption at T2 or T2-3 ganglia appears to be an effective treatment forfacial blushing. However, lack of randomized trials comparing sympathetic interruption with non-surgical methods of treatment and heterogeneity of included studies with respect to assessment ofoutcome measures preclude strong evidence and definitive recommendations.ARTICLE HISTORYReceived 30 October 2016Accepted 13 January 2017KEYWORDSBlushing; flushing;sympathectomy; autonomicnerve block1. Introduction
Facial blushing (syn. erythrophobia) is an involuntary condi-tion caused by autonomic dysfunction [1]. It presents as dis-proportionate redness of the face, ears, neck, and upper chestin response to heightened anxiety states. It usually occurs inyoung individuals and is a significant problem in the fairskinned. The exact prevalence of facial blushing is notknown, but, it forms one of the prominent conditions in socialphobia, the prevalence of which is around 10% [2,3].Various treatment strategies have been used to managefacial blushing. Social anxiety disorders are identified andtreatment targeted toward them using various forms of psy-chotherapy before offering treatment for blushing per se [4-10]. Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRI), like sertra-line[4] and beta blockers, such as atenolol[11], can be usefuladjuncts in the treatment of facial blushing before consideringsurgery. Surgical treatment is usually reserved for patientswho are severely affected in whom anxiety disorders areruled out. Thoracoscopic sympathectomy is the most commonchoice of intervention, although percutaneous techniquesemploying radiofrequency and chemical ablation of the sym-pathetic ganglia have also been described. There are nofocused systematic reviews addressing role of sympatheticinterruption in facial blushing alone [12]. This review considersthe evidence in support of sympathetic interruption in thetreatment of facial blushing, its outcomes and associatedcomplications.2. ObjectivesTo review the evidence to support effectiveness of sym-pathectomy or sympathetic blockade as treatment for facialblushing in terms of relief of blushing, patient satisfaction,recurrence of blushing, patients regretting treatment and itsassociated complications.3. Inclusion and exclusion criteriaStudies on patients with facial blushing, who underwent sympa-thetic interruption in which data for facial blushing could beseparately extracted, were included. All types of study designsuch as randomized controlled trials, nonrandomized controlledtrials, descriptive studies, and case series were included.In this review, sympathetic interruption includes sympathetect-omy and sympathetic clipping or blockade. As per the nomencla-ture for the level of sympathetic interruption, an expert consensuspublished in 2011 on management of hyperhidrosis advocatesadopting rib rather than vertebrae as reference level [14].Studies with patients who had previous sympathectomy orre-do procedures for facial blushing were excluded. Thisincluded secondary causes of blushing namely, neurogenic rosa-cea, Harlequin syndrome, Frey's syndrome, and menopause.Experimental animal studies and studies reporting insufficientpreoperative and postoperative data were also excluded.3.2. Search methods for identification of studies
The electronic databases searched to identify potential studieswere Ovid-Medline, Embase, Cochrane library, and NICE. Nolimits or filters were applied to any of the databases as wefound the literature on the topic of interest was limited. Thekey words used for the search were 'blushing,' 'flushing,' 'facialflush,' 'sympathectomy,' 'sympathetic clipping,' 'sympatheticblock,' 'sympathectomy,' 'nerve block,' and 'nerve clipping.'However, a tailor made search strategy was created for eachof the databases reviewed (Supplemental data).3.3. Selection of studiesWith the specified search strategy, each database wasexplored by two authors (GG and RD) independently. Titleandabstractscreeningwasdoneforallthearticlesobtained through the initial hit. The search results weremerged to remove duplication. Articles were rejected oninitial screen when the review authors determined from thetitle and the abstract that they were not relevant. When atitle or abstract was inconclusive to include or reject, fulltext was examined. The two review authors independentlyassessed the studies for inclusion and resolved the differ-ences by discussion and, when necessary, in consultationwith a third review author (Baker D M). In the case ofduplicate publications and companion papers of a primarystudy, all available data were obtained for complete infor-mation and the studies were linked together. In cases ofdoubt, the original publication (usually the oldest version)obtained priority and full text was examined for complianceof the study with eligibility criteria. When informationregarding any of the above was unclear, an attempt wasmade to contact the authors. Figure 1 (PRISMA chart) illus-trates the selection process of studies included in thereview.
3.4. Data extraction and managementData extraction form was constructed based on the reviewobjectives. Two review authors independently extracted datafrom included papers on to an Excel spread sheet. Items underwhich data were extracted include - authors, institution, coun-try, period of study, type of study, inclusion, exclusion, age(average, mean and range), total number of patients, genderdistribution, duration of symptoms, range of duration of symp-toms, initial assessment, medical treatment, procedure per-formed including number of cases, level of sympatheticinterruption, post-op result assessment tool, post-op result(relief of facial blushing and patient satisfaction), complica-tions, regret undergoing surgery, follow-up period, drop out,funding, conflict of interest, and ethical approval.3.5. Assessment of methodological quality of includedstudiesCase series comprised the majority of studies in the systema-tic review [15-21]. In this review, the tool from NationalHeart, Lung and Blood Institute of NIH [22] was used toassess the quality of case series designs. Accordingly, in thefinal analysis, we included four studies which were consid-ered 'good' [15-18] and three as 'fair' [19-21].
A randomized controlled trial by Licht et al. [23] wasassessed for risk of bias using the criteria outlined in theCochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions[24]. The study was found to have 'low risk of bias' in keydomains except the absence of control group which wasconsidered as 'high risk of bias' in the 'other bias' category.The intervention trial by Jadresic et al. [4] was categorized as'controlled before-and-after study.' For this particular study, theCochrane Collaboration's tool for assessing risk of bias wasadapted using EPOC's criteria for studies other than RCTs [25].According to this tool, the study had 'low risk of bias' in three keydomains and 'high risk of bias' in four key domains. However, forthe purpose of our review, the data from this interventional trial isconsidered as similar to a case series and hence included in thereview.3.6. Data analysisProportion of each parameter and its confidence interval wascomputed. Heterogeneity was assessed using Q statistics and I2.Meta-analysis was carried out using random effects model inSTATA 13. We included all the nine studies if they reported specificoutcomemeasures. Weattempted subgroup analysisbased on thestudy design, as we had seven case series and two interventiontrials. However, considering the methodological limitations of thetwo intervention trials, we combined all the study designs anddiscussed the results in detail. This approach is justified as our maininterest was on the outcome of sympathectomy and not in thecomparison of levels of sympathectomy [23] or sympathectomyversus medical treatment [4]. The data of outcome measuresrelated to sympathectomywere isolated from the twointerventiontrials [4,23] and were used for meta-analysis. Study characteristicsNine studies (Table 1) met the inclusion criteria with a total of2045 cases, of which 1369 patients were included in the finalanalysis after removal of non-blushers and those who werenot followed up or responded to questionnaires. Sympatheticblockade was achieved by clipping the second sympatheticganglion above and below, whereas sympathectomy inter-rupted the ganglia varying from T1 to T3 except in onestudy where the interruption was from T1 to T5. Two studiescompared the outcomes of sympathectomy at T2 and T2-3[17,23]. In one study [4], 2nd ganglion interruption was per-formed in pure blushers while the 2nd and 3rd ganglia wereinterrupted in those with facial blushing with palmar hyperhi-drosis. In this study, split data for facial blushing and hyperhi-drosis was available. Follow-up period was 1-60 months witha median of 20 months in one study [17] and mean of21 months in the remaining 8 studies. Eight of the nine studies
reported some formal method of preoperative assessment offacial blushing. All the nine studies used patient opinion,visual analog scores and/or a standardized questionnaire post-operatively to report outcome of surgery. Eight studies per-formed sympathectomy and one sympathetic blockade byclipping. All studies but one documented the procedure tobe bilateral.The two intervention trials[4,23] and the seven case series[15-21] were analyzed separately (Table 2). Interventionaltrials showed a higher rate of occurrence of compensatorysweating, recurrence of blushing, relief of blushing, and pro-portion of patients satisfied with the results from the proce-dure compared to the case series. Though most of theoutcome measures had a lower occurrence in case seriescompared to intervention studies, few of the outcomesnamely, gustatory sweating, Horner's syndrome, proportionof people partially satisfied were higher. Our focus is to ana-lyze outcomes of sympathectomy as mentioned earlier in dataanalysis.Aftercarefulmethodologicalconsideration,wedecided to combine all the studies for detailed discussion asthere is no clear justification for separating the trials.
4.2. Primary outcome measures (relief of facial blushingand patient satisfaction)A self-report measure which rates the relief of facial blushing(Table 3) was reported as good, partial, and no relief in fivestudies. Three studies reported it as relief and no relief andone reported only patients with no relief. Meta-analysis wascarried out for the primary outcome measures. The overallpooled estimate of proportion of patients who got relief offacial blushing was 78.30% (95% CI, 58.20-98.39%). There wassignificant heterogeneity in the results (I2 = 90.4) and hencewe used the random effect model for meta-analysis. Fivestudies reported the data on partial relief of blushing. Theoverall pooled estimate of proportion for partial relief was15.49% (95% CI, 6.32-24.66%). The significant heterogeneityin the results (I2 = 83.5) justified the random effect model formeta-analysis. Data regarding patients who did not have reliefafter surgery was pooled and the proportion was 8.01% (95%CI, 4.65-11.38%). Another primary outcome considered in thereview is the satisfaction of patients with regard to the surgicalprocedure (Table 3). The proportion of patients completelysatisfied in five studies was 84.02% (95% CI, 71.71-96.33%).In four studies, the proportion of partially satisfied patientswas 7.23% (95% CI, 3.46-11.0%). The pooled estimate forunsatisfied patients in five studies was 6.83% (95% CI, 3.32-10.33%). Flushing is devoid of a psychological component.Nonsurgical treatment in the form of psychotherapy suchas cognitive therapy, task concentration training [9,29,30],psycho-educational group intervention [31], and cognitivebehavioral group therapy [32], or pharmacotherapy withdrugs such as sertraline or beta blockers [4], Botulinum toxinA [33] are often ineffective and patients then seek surgicalintervention.Endoscopic transection or clipping of the sympatheticchain at T2 or T2-3 level is increasingly being offered topatients with facial blushing and/or facial sweating. NICEguidelinesrecommendendoscopicsympathectomyasOutcomeNo of studiesTotal sample sizeP(95% CI)I2treatment of patients with facial blushing [34]. The evidence tothis effect that we retrieved was mostly in the form of caseseries.
The success rate of sympathectomy or sympathetic interrup-tion, described as relief of blushing in various studies system-atically reviewed was 78.30%. Occasionally, relief of symptom isachieved on one side of the face whereas the other sideremains blushed requiring re-operation [15]. Another significantprimary outcome was the complete satisfaction reported by84.02%ofpatientswhounderwentsympathectomy.Comparison of the success rate of sympathectomy (78.30%) topatient satisfaction rate (84.02%) probably suggests a placeboeffect. Compensatory sweating was reported in most studies(74.18%, C.I 58.10-90.26%) but only 6.79% of patients regrettedhaving had the procedure. Jadresic et al[4] and Fibla et al. [19]have reported 13% and 8% of severe compensatory sweating,respectively, after sympathectomy for facial blushing. Thiswould reflect that thoracic sympathetic interruption is an effec-tive procedure for facial blushing.reported, but the incidence is much lower than compensatorysweating (24.42%). Horner's syndrome was reported in justunder 2% of cases. This was thought to be due to thermalinjury of stellate ganglion during transection of the chain bydiathermy. This may probably be reduced by using ultrasonicscalpel [23] or cutting diathermy instead of coagulation [16]and by clipping [4]. In a study by Chou et al. [35], which wasnot included in the analysis due to poor quality, 16.7% (9patients) regretted the procedure and underwent reversal byremoval of clips with variable improvement in compensatorysweating. To facilitate reversal of the procedure, recent studieshave promoted sympathetic clipping, but there is no clearevidence to support this [35-37]. An experimental study byLoscertales et al. [38] on an animal model concluded that theabsence of nerve regeneration suggests that clipping of nerveis not a reversible procedure. Cardiovascular complicationssuch as bradycardia, palpitations, and hypotension have
been reported after thoracic sympathectomy occasionallyand is usually asymptomatic. In a subgroup of 18 patientswho underwent T2-4 sympathectomy for palmar hyperhidro-sis, Drot et al. [39] reported a significantly reduced heart rateand systolic blood pressure. This had no effect on maximalworkload. In our review, the only cardiovascular complicationin the form of palpitations was reported by Drot et al. [18] in0.7% of their patients.The only RCT in this review [23] suggests there is no differ-ence between interruption at the second or the second andthird ganglia with respect to complications. Whereas, a retro-spective questionnaire-based study by the same authors in anearlier publication suggested T2 sympathectomy to be 'superior'to T2-3 sympathectomy because of lesser side effects [17]. TheRCT would obviously carry more significance. The society ofthoracic surgeons expert consensus for the surgical treatmentof hyperhidrosis [14] suggests interruption at the level of thethird ganglion for facial hyperhidrosis and we assume that thiswould be the case for blushers as well. Higher the sympatheticinterruption, greater is the risk of compensatory sweating [35].The new Lin-Telaranta classification [40] suggests sympatheticinterruption at the second ganglion for facial blushing.No deaths were reported in the selected studies in our
review. Ojimba [41] however reports mortality secondary tomassive intra-thoracic hemorrhage or anesthetic complicationfollowing endoscopic thoracic sympathectomy. Ojimba alsoreports residual pneumothorax requiring chest drain (0.4-2.4%)and surgical emphysema (2.7%) [41]. In our review, we found dryhands in 2 studies 61% and 26% [17,23], port site pain in 2studies 2% and 3.7% [15,17], postoperative chest pain in 2studies, 88% and 0.48% [18,21], non-gustatory facial sweatingin one occurring in 11.8% of patients [21] and pneumothorax,intraoperative bleeding and pulmonary embolism with paradox-ical embolism causing stroke occurring in one out of 18 patients(5%) in one study [16]. One study [18] also noted impairedphysical performance (1.3%), palpitations (0.7%), arm pain(0.6%), chills (0.6%), dizziness (0.36%), GI disturbances 0.36%,and headache 0.12%. Two studies [17,21] reported chest draininsertions in 1% (1/95) and 8.5% (5/589) patients, respectively.In view of invasiveness of endoscopic thoracic sympathect-omy, its potential complications and postoperative dissatisfac-tion, case selection is important. There is a compelling need todefine strict criteria in the diagnosis of these patients and selectappropriate cases for surgical treatment [42-44]. Facial blushingcan be self-perceived (fear of blushing) or observed [29] whichneed to be distinguished before invasive treatment is offered[42]. In cases of perceived facial blushing, various methods ofpsychotherapy are often effective [9,30-32]. High scoring insocial anxiety scoring systems may help identify patient whowould benefit from psychotherapy support [45]. Selection ofactual facial blushers for surgical treatment can be difficult aspatient reported blushing has not matched the objective facialblood flow [5]. Criteria to establish a firm diagnosis and when tooffer surgical treatment should be a topic for future research.
5.1. Limitations of this reviewThe review consisted of 4 good quality and 3 fair case series.The only RCT in this review was of level IIA and one nonran-domized study was of level IIB evidence. The case series wereof Level IV evidence. The paucity of good-quality studies couldbe seen as a limitation to making rigid conclusions.6.
