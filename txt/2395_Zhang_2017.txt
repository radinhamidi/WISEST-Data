Risk of Stroke/Transient Ischemic Attack or MyocardialInfarction with Herpes Zoster: A Systematic Review andMeta-AnalysisYanting Zhang, MSc,* Ganfeng Luo, MSc,* Yuanwei Huang, MSc,*Qiuyan Yu, MSc,+ Li Wang, MSc,* and Ke Li, MD*Background: Accumulating evidence indicates that herpes zoster (HZ) may in-crease the risk of stroke/transient ischemic attack (TIA) or myocardial infarction(MI), but the results are inconsistent. We aim to explore the relationship betweenHZ and risk of stroke/TIA or MI and between herpes zoster ophthalmicus (HZO)and stroke. Methods: We estimated the relative risk (RR) and 95% confidence in-tervals (CIs) with the meta-analysis. Cochran's Q test and Higgins I2 statistic wereused to check for heterogeneity. Results: HZ infection was significantly associ-ated with increased risk of stroke/TIA (RR = 1.30, 95% CI: 1.17-1.46) or MI (RR = 1.18,95% CI: 1.07-1.30). The risk of stroke after HZO was 1.91 (95% CI 1.32-2.76), higherthan that after HZ. Subgroup analyses revealed increased risk of ischemic strokeafter HZ infection but not hemorrhagic stroke. The risk of stroke was increasedmore at 1 month after HZ infection than at 1-3 months, with a gradual reducedrisk with time. The risk of stroke after HZ infection was greater with age lessthan 40 years than 40-59 years and more than 60 years. Risk of stroke with HZinfection was greater without treatment than with treatment and was greaterin Asia than Europe and America but did not differ by sex. Conclusions: Ourstudy indicated that HZ infection was associated with increased risk of stroke/TIA or MI, and HZO infection was the most marked risk factor for stroke. Furtherstudies are needed to explore whether zoster vaccination could reduce the riskof stoke/TIA or MI. IntroductionStroke is classically characterized as a neurological deficitattributed to an acute focal injury of the central nervoussystem by a vascular cause.1 All over the world, stroke,the second most common cause of death, has become asignificant public health issue. Stroke occurs in about 15million people worldwide per year, leading to 5 milliondeaths and an additional 5 million people disabled.2,3 Ath-erosclerosis and embolism are the most important causesof stroke. Common risk factors include smoking, atrial fi-brillation, diabetes, and hypertension. Infection-related strokeis differentiated from stroke due to other reasons becausesome infectious causes can be treated with antibiotics andprevented by vaccination for further control of stroke.Myocardial infarction (MI) is also a major cause of mor-bidity and mortality worldwide.3 Increased MI events afteracute infection are thought to be related to endothelialdysfunction, characterized by atheromatous plaque ruptureand development of a prothrombotic environment.4Herpes zoster (HZ) infection is a significant public healthproblem in aging populations, annually affecting 1 millionAmericans and more than 88,650 immunocompetent peo-ple older than 60 years in the United Kingdom.5-7 HZ, alsoknown as shingles, is a painful vesicular eruption that istypically restricted to 1 or 2 dermatomes and is inducedby the reactivation of dormant varicella zoster virus (VZV),a ubiquitous pathogen that persists asymptomatically inthe sensory ganglia after primary chickenpox in childhood.8HZ infection occurs in people with diminished cell-mediated immunity. HZ infection is often pernicious, withfrequent complications, the most common being postherpeticneuralgia leading to long-term pain, depression, anddisability.9 Other severe complications include vasculopathy,giant cell arteritis, myelitis, encephalitis, meningoencepha-litis, keratitis, ocular involvement, cranial nerve palsies, andmotor nerve involvement.10-13
Recent studies have proposed that the risk of stroke orMI may be increased in the year following an episode ofHZ, possibly also mediated by VZV replication in cere-bral arterial walls. The infection is spread along the nervefibers to the blood vessels and thrombotic responses areinduced, thereby resulting in cerebral vasculopathy.14,15 Inour analysis, we classified transient ischemic attack (TIA)as minor stroke because of the similar pathogenic mech-anism. However, the relationship between HZ infection andrisk of stroke/TIA or MI is still unclear. A positive asso-ciation was revealed by some epidemiologic studies,16-21but others indicated negative results.8,22-25We performed a systematic review and meta-analysisto summarize the published epidemiologic evidence onthe relationship between HZ infection and risk of stroke/TIA or MI or between herpes zoster ophthalmicus (HZO)infection and stroke. We also evaluated the associationbetween HZ infection and stroke by time since HZ, age,sex, ethnicity status, and treatment status.Materials and Methods
Search Strategy and Study SelectionTwo authors (Y.Z. and G.L.) independently searchedPubMed for articles published in English up to October20, 2016, by using the key words ("Stroke"[Mesh] ORStroke[tiab] OR ischemic stroke[tiab] OR ischaemicstroke[tiab] OR non-ischemic stroke[tiab] OR hemorrhag-ic stroke[tiab] OR poststroke[tiab] OR apoplexy[tiab] ORcerebral arterial disease[tiab] OR cerebrovascularaccident[tiab] OR "Myocardial Infarction"[Mesh] OR myo-cardial infarction[tiab] OR myocardium infarction[tiab]OR myocardic infarction[tiab] OR "Ischemic Attack,Transient"[Mesh] OR transient ischaemic attack[tiab] ORtransient ischemic attack[tiab] OR ischemic attack*[tiab]OR ischaemic attack*[tiab] OR brain ischemi*[tiab] ORbrain ischaemi*[tiab] OR transient brain ischemia[tiab] ORcerebral ischemia[tiab]), AND ("Herpes Zoster"[Mesh]OR Herpes Zoster[tiab] OR Shingles[tiab] OR Zona[tiab]OR Zoster[tiab] OR VZV[tiab]). Titles, abstracts, full texts,and reference lists of all identified reports were re-viewed in duplicate by the 2 authors, and extracted articleswere double-checked by K.L. Disagreements were re-solved by discussion among the 3 authors (Y.Z., G.L., andK.L.). Reference lists from related main studies and reviewarticles were checked for additional relevant reports.
Inclusion and Exclusion CriteriaArticles were considered eligible if (1) they includedpeer-reviewed data from an observational study; (2) thecriteria for confirmation of the HZ diagnosis in our in-cluded studies were as follows: (1) physician's diagnosisof HZ with clinical symptoms (vesicular rash or derma-tomal pain or others); (2) serological analysis (dried bloodspot analysis) or laboratory tests (Tzanck smear or viralculture or polymerase chain reaction); and (3) HZ-relateddiagnostic codes with the International Classification ofDiseases, Tenth Revision (ICD-10: B02), or Ninth Revision(ICD-9: 053). Only the cases satisfied with the above 3criteria in the meantime were included in our studies. Thus,those cases with vesicle symptoms not caused by VZVinfection were excluded in a stringent manner. Articleswere also considered eligible if (3) stroke was diagnosedwith clinical information and radiological investigation.Stroke was identified using diagnostic codes with ICD-10 I60-64 and G45 or ICD-9 430-438; (4) concerned therisk of stroke/TIA or MI after HZ infection; and (5) re-ported multivariate-adjusted relative risks (RRs) and 95%confidence intervals (CIs). We excluded (1) studies ofpatients with a diagnosis of stroke before HZ diagnosis;(2) reviews and letters; (3) duplicate publications; (4) non-English language articles; and (5) data insufficient forcalculating a risk estimate with 95% CIs, or unqualifieddata. No publications were excluded onthe basis of quality, sample size, or other objective criteriarelevant to study design and analysis.Quality AssessmentWe evaluated the quality of reports by the Newcastle-Ottawa quality assessment scale26 ( Twoindependent reviewers read and scored all included studies.The Newcastle-Ottawa scale contains 8 items, 7 applica-ble to our study question. Items were classified into 3domains: selection, comparability, and outcome. The totalscores of 0-3, 4-6, and 7-9 stars were assigned for low,moderate, and high qualities, respectively. Any discrep-ancies between the 2 reviewers were resolved by jointreassessment until consensus was reached.Data Extraction
We extracted data on (1) publication details (first author'sname, year of publication, and study design); (2) baselinecharacteristics of the study population (country, study pe-riod, number of observation groups, age, follow-up time);and (3) other details (infection types, endpoints). If thesedata were absent, we sent an email to the authors. Weused the results of the original studies from multivari-able models with the most complete adjustment for po-tential confounders.Statistical AnalysisWe extracted the RRs and their 95% CIs from studiesto evaluate the risk of stroke/TIA or MI after HZ infec-tion or risk of stroke after HZO infection. A pooledRR higher than 1 indicated greater risk for the group. Theeffect of stroke/TIA or MI after HZ exposure was con-sidered statistically significant with 95% CIs for the pooledRR not overlapping 1 in the forest plot. Cochran's Q testand Higgins I2 statistic were used to check for hetero-geneity of the combined result.27,28 Studies were consideredheterogeneous with Pheterogeneity less than .1 or I2 higherthan 50%. With heterogeneity, the random effects model(DerSimonian-Laird method) was used29; otherwise, thefixed effects model (Mantel-Haenszel method) was used.30We investigated potential sources of heterogeneity by sub-group analyses of time since HZ, area, age, sex, andtreatment. Sensitivity analyses involved excluding studiesone by one. We investigated publication bias by the Egger'slinear regression test31 and Begg's methods.32 The effectof potential publication bias on risk assessment was furtherevaluated by the Duval and Tweedie nonparametric "trim-and-fill" method.33 Two-sided P < .05 was consideredstatistically significant. All analyses involved use of STATA13.0 (STATA Corp., College Station, TX).
ResultsLiterature Search and Study CharacteristicsWe identified 392 relevant studies in the literature search;20 were removed as duplicates and 331 were excluded onthe basis of titles and abstracts. After manually screeningthe full text of 41 articles, 30 articles were excluded. Finally,our analysis included 11 studies8,16-25 (Fig 1). In our in-cluded studies, stroke included ischemic and hemorrhagicstrokes. Furthermore, intracerebral hemorrhage or sub-arachnoid hemorrhage was included in hemorrhagic stroke.Five studies report the RR of ischemic stroke and 3 studiesreport the RR of hemorrhagic stroke, while the rest reportthe RR of stroke of both subtypes. Among the 11 eligiblestudies, 3 were conducted in Asia, 3 in America, 4 in Europe,and 1 was an international collaboration. The articles werepublished between 2009 and 2016. Three were case-control studies, and the remaining were cohort studies. Ourmeta-analysis included 4,620,980 participants and the meanfollow-up duration ranged from 1 to 12.5 years. We observed a strongeffect for risk of stroke after HZO diagnosis, with a 1.9-fold increased risk.Several possible biological stroke mechanisms are in-voked with the association of risk of stroke/TIA or MIafter HZ infection. First, inflammation associated with sys-temic infection may injure the endothelium and lead toarterial thrombosis with the background of atheroscle-rosis and hypercoagulability.34,35 Second, reactivated VZVtravels along nerve fibers in the trigeminal ganglion anddamages the vessel wall, which leads to aneurysm andhemorrhage.36-38 In addition, patients with HZ often havepostherpetic neuropathic pain, which induces depres-sive symptoms and a weakened immune system, toincrease the risk of stroke.39,40On subgroup analysis, HZ infection was associatedwith increased risk of ischemic stroke but not hemorrhag-ic stroke. An explanation might be the complete difference
in pathological changes and etiology between ischemic strokeand hemorrhagic stroke. The etiology of ischemic strokeis mostly caused by inflammation and HZ infections arecorrelated with other major comorbidities, such as diabe-tes, cardiac disease, and hypertension,41-43 which is favorablefor ischemic than hemorrhagic stroke. Insufficient studiesfor statistical power might explain the RR of hemorrhag-ic stroke with no statistical significance. Further explorationinto this matter was suggested. We found the greatest in-crease in risk for stroke at 1 month after HZ diagnosis,with a 1.9-fold increased risk, then 1-3 months and grad-ually decreasing with time, which indicates the need forearly preventive measures. As well, patients less than 40years old had a twofold increased risk of stroke after HZdiagnosis, with a threefold increased risk for those 40-59and higher than 60 years old. This finding may be explainedin part by the voluntary screening of vascular risk factorsin patients older than 45 years in some countries such asthe United Kingdom.44,45 In addition, VZV is a risk factorfor stroke in people less than 40 years old, which was sup-ported by several studies.22,46 With the large samples inour analysis, we found no difference between men andwomen in stroke risk after HZ but did find that the riskdiffered by region, with different geographical and cli-matic situations, lifestyles, working habit, and genotypicmilieu. Asian populations had a 1.6-fold increased risk ofstroke after HZ exposure, which suggests that this groupshould receive more attention.47,48Whether antiviral therapy alters the risk of stroke afterHZ exposure is an issue. Third, we found publication bias in the HZO anal-ysis, which may be related to the search strategy restrictedto English language literature.
