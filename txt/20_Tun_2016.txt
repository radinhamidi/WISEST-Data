Benefit and risk of primary thromboprophylaxis in ambulatorypatients with advanced pancreatic cancer receivingchemotherapy: a systematic review and meta-analysis ofrandomized controlled trialsNay Min Tuna, Elizabeth Guevaraa and Thein Hlaing OobAs vascular thromboembolism (VTE) is a leading cause ofdeath in cancer patients, it has been postulated that primarythromboprophylaxis (PTP) in cancer patients might improvesurvival by reducing VTE occurrence. We performed asystemic review and meta-analysis of randomizedcontrolled trials (RCTs) to investigate the benefit and risk ofPTP with low-molecular-weight heparins (LMWHs) inambulatory advanced pancreatic cancer (APC) patientsreceiving chemotherapy. We undertook a literature searchusing MEDLINE and EMBASE databases through May 2015.RCTs with reduction in symptomatic VTE as a primary orsecondary endpoints were included. Mantel-Haenszelmethod was used to estimate the pooled event-based riskratio as well as the pooled absolute risk difference with 95%confidence interval (CI). Seven hundred and thirty-eightAPC patients were eligible for analysis. PTP lasted3-6 months. The crude VTE incidence was 2.1 and 11.2% inLMWH and in control groups, respectively (risk ratio, 0.18;95% CI, 0.083-0.39; P < 0.0001). The absolute riskdifference in VTE was S0.092 (95% CI, S0.127 to S0.057;P < 0.0001), with an estimated number needed to treat of 11patients to prevent one symptomatic VTE event. The pooledrisk ratio for major bleeding was 1.25 (95% CI, 0.48-3.3,P U 0.65). Although these findings are encouraging todeploy PTP in APC patients receiving chemotherapy,uncertainties remain as to its survival benefit, optimal PTPduration, type and dose of LMWH, and costs of care.Therefore, adequately powered randomized phase IIIstudies are warranted to address these questions. IntroductionPatients with cancer are at a significantly increased risk ofdeveloping venous thromboembolism as well as arterialthromboembolic events [1-3]. It is estimated thatapproximately 20-30% of all first venous thromboem-bolic events are associated with cancer [3]. Vascularthromboembolism (VTE) imposes significant morbidityand mortality in cancer patients. It is the second leadingcause of death in patients with cancer after cancer pro-gression itself [4]. The 1-year survival rate of cancerpatients with VTE was one-third that of cancer patientswithout VTE in one study [5]. In addition, cancerpatients have a higher incidence of VTE recurrenceand bleeding complications during anticoagulant therapythan patients without cancer [6]. As mortality is increasedin cancer patients who develop VTE compared withthose without VTE [2,5,7], it has been postulated thatprimary thromboprophylaxis (PTP) in cancer patientsmight improve survival and quality of life by reducingVTE occurrence. However, routine prophylaxis againstVTE in ambulatory patients with solid cancer remainsextremely controversial. Risk factors for cancer-associ-ated thrombosis could be divided into patient-specific,cancer-specific and treatment-specific risk factors [8]. Toname a few, these include certain types of cancer such aspancreatic and haematologic malignancies, advanced-stage cancer and antineoplastic therapy such as che-motherapy and radiotherapy [8,9]. The more risk factorsa cancer patient has, the more likely the patient is toexperience VTE [10]. It is therefore anticipated that sucha high-risk patient has a more favourable benefit/risk ratiowith PTP. Patients with advanced pancreatic cancerreceiving chemotherapy fall into this high-risk category,and thus, to investigate the benefit and risk of PTP insuch patients, we carried out a systematic review andmeta-analysis of randomized controlled trials (RCTs).
Materials and methodsLiterature searchWe undertook a systematic literature search using MED-LINE and EMBASE databases up to 20 May 2015. Bothdatabases were accessed through www.embase.com usingthe keywords 'thromboprophylaxis OR anticoagulationOR warfarin OR heparin AND ('cancer'/exp OR cancer)'using Embase mapping options (Map to preferred term inEmtree; Search also as free text in all fields; Explode270Original article Copyright (c) 2016 Wolters Kluwer Health, Inc. All rights reserved.using narrower Emtree terms; and Search as broadly aspossible). The search was limited to 'controlled clinicaltrials' and 'humans'. Studies written in both English andnon-English languages were searched. The titles andabstracts of the resulting citations were reviewed. Full-text articles of potential studies were retrieved to assesseligibility. References of the potential studies and clinicalguidelines on thromboprophylaxis in cancer patientswere also reviewed for any additional relevant studies.
Eligibility criteriaStudies that were eligible for inclusion in the meta-analysis had to fulfil all of the following criteria: RCTsof patients who were receiving chemotherapy for locallyadvanced or metastatic pancreatic cancer; RCTs compar-ing anticoagulation therapy and a control group; andsymptomatic VTE events (sVTE) during the anticoagu-lation therapy as a primary or secondary efficacy endpointof the RCT.Data extraction and study outcome measuresTwo authors (N.M.T. and E.G.) independently con-ducted data extraction from each eligible study. Thefollowing data were collected: first author's last name;year of publication; acronym of the study if available;study type; its primary and secondary endpoints; types ofmalignancy enrolled in the study; study drug; its dosageand duration; number of VTE events, death and majorbleeding episodes in the experimental and control arms;number of patients included in each arm; and overallsurvival data. Any discrepancies were addressed by a jointre-evaluation with the third author. The primary outcomeof our meta-analysis was sVTE. The secondary outcomewas major bleeding events.
Quality assessmentThe quality of the included studies was independentlyevaluated by two authors (N.M.T. and E.G.) using theNewcastle-Ottawa Scale (NOS) [11]. The NOS assigns amaximum of four points for selection, two points forcomparability and three points for exposure/outcome,and thus nine points on the NOS indicate the higheststudy quality.Data synthesis and analysisMantel-Haenszel method was used to estimate thepooled event based risk ratio as well as the pooledabsolute risk difference with 95% confidence interval(CI). Fixed effects model was applied because therewas no significant heterogeneity among the includedstudies. All statistical analyses and graphs were obtainedby using Comprehensive Meta Analysis Version 2.2.064(Biostat, Englewood, New Jersey, USA). The level ofsignificance was set at P less than 0.05. Data from thismeta-analysis are presented in accordance with thechecklist proposed by the PRISMA Statement [12].ResultsSearch resultsTwo RCTs [13,14] and a subgroup of another two RCTs[15,16], comprising a total of 738 patients with advancedpancreatic cancer, were eligible for inclusion in our meta-analysis. PTP was given for the durationof chemotherapy, that is mostly around 3 months.
Meta-analysis resultsThe crude incidence of sVTE was 2.1 and 11.2% inpatients receiving PTP and in the control group, respect-ively, with a resultant risk ratio of 0.18 (95% CI, 0.083-0.39; P < 0.001) (Fig. 2). The absolute risk difference insVTE was 0.092 (95% CI, 0.127 to 0.057; P < 0.001)(Fig. 3), with an estimate of the number needed to treat(NNT) of 11 patients (95% CI, 8-18; P < 0.001) toprevent one sVTE. Major bleeding events were reportedin 4.1% of patients on PTP compared with 3.3% in controlpatients according to an analysis of two RCTs [13,14].The pooled relative risk for major bleeding was statisti-cally nonsignificant at 1.25 (95% CI, 0.48-3.3, P  1/4 0.65)(Fig. 4).Quality assessment resultsThe quality assessment results of the individual studiesare summarized in Table 1. All rights reserved.Heterogeneity and publication biasThere was mild between-study heterogeneity for riskratio and risk difference analysis for VTE events (I2  1/4 34and 38%, respectively). No heterogeneity was observedfor risk ratio analysis for major bleeding events (I2  1/4 0%).There was no evidence of publication bias detected forrisk ratio or risk difference analysis by either the Begg andMazumdar rank correlation test or the Egger's test ofthe intercept.DiscussionThe results of our meta-analysis indicate that thrombo-prophylaxis in advanced pancreatic cancer patientsreceiving chemotherapy resulted in an 82% reductionin the relative risk of sVTE with NNT of 11. This is incontrast to the finding of a previous meta-analysis thatreported approximately 60 patients were required to betreated with low-molecular-weight heparin (LMWH) toprevent one sVTE among unselected cancer patientsreceiving chemotherapy [17]. The discrepant finding inthe benefit of PTP suggests that high-risk patient selec-tion is of crucial importance to improve outcome. Inaddition, the observed risk reduction in our meta-analysiswas not associated with an increase in major bleeding,implying that PTP with LMWH may be beneficial inreducing VTE events in ambulatory patients withadvanced pancreatic cancer undergoing chemotherapy.We were unable to calculate the event rates as a functionof time on study, as the published PROTECHT andSAVE-ONCO trials did not describe the VTE events indetails [15,16]. Interestingly, symptomatic VTE eventrates on LMWH group continue to decrease comparedwith control group even after the discontinuation ofLMWH in the FRAGEM (9% dalteparin arm vs. 22%control arm at >100 days) and CONKO-004 (5.7% enox-aparin arm vs. 15.1% control arm at 12 months) trials[13,14].
In a real-world analysis of venous thromboembolism riskin patients with cancer receiving chemotherapy, 11.6%(221 out of 1912) of patients with pancreatic cancerdeveloped venous thromboembolism within 3.5 monthsafter starting chemotherapy [18]. Our meta-analysisrevealed a comparable pooled incidence rate of sVTE(11.2%, 40 out of 357) in the control group around thesame time frame. The similarity in these two patientpopulations reflect that the efficacy of LMWH in pre-venting sVTE as evident in our meta-analysis might beapplicable to the real-world pancreatic cancer patients.It is noted that a previous meta-analysis of PTP in solidcancer patients had included a subset analysis of pan-creatic cancer patients [19]. All rights reserved.It is at present unclear whether extended PTP beyondthe period of chemotherapy is of benefit in patients withadvanced pancreatic cancer. In CONKO-004 trial, it wasfound that the beneficial effect of short-term PTP(semi-therapeutic dose of enoxaparin for 3 months) onsVTE decreased by three-fold over time (relative risk of0.13 at 3 months vs. 0.38 at 12 months) [14]. Likewise,FRAGEM trial showed that beyond the period of anti-coagulation, the relative risk for sVTE increased byeight-fold over time (relative risk of 0.048 at 100 daysvs. 0.39 at 12 months) [13]. These findings suggest thatextended PTP might be useful in reducing sVTE eventsat 12 months.
Our study has several limitations that should be acknowl-edged. The studies included used different anticoagu-lants as well as differing dose regimens (Table 1); it isunclear regarding the equivalency of the efficacy andsafety of therapeutic dose vs. those of semi-therapeutic orprophylactic dose of anticoagulants with regard tosVTE prevention.In summary, our results indicate that targeted PTPresulted in an 82% reduction in the relative risk ofsVTE without increasing major bleeding events, andthat 11 patients were needed to be treated to preventone sVTE during chemotherapy. Although these find-ings are encouraging to deploy PTP in patients withadvanced pancreatic cancer receiving chemotherapy,uncertainties remain as to its survival benefit, optimalduration of PTP, type and dose of LMWH, quality oflife and costs of care. Therefore, adequately poweredrandomized phase III studies are warranted to addressthese questions.AcknowledgementsN.M.T. and E.G. did the conception and design, datacollection and analysis, and writing of the manuscript.T.H.O. did the conception and critical comments on themanuscript.
