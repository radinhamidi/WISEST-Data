Thyssen. E-mail: AbstractBackground Rosacea is a common skin disease characterized by facial erythema, telangiectasia, papules and pus-tules. Helicobacter pylori infection has been suggested to play a role in the etiopathogenesis of rosacea.Objective To systematically review and meta-analyse the relationship between rosacea and infection with Helicobacterpylori.Methods A literature search was performed using PubMed, EMBASE and Web of Science. Data extraction andanalyses were performed on descriptive data. Study quality was assessed using the Newcastle-Ottawa Scale. Random-effects models with DerSimonian-Laird methods were utilized to estimate pooled odds ratios (ORs), with 95% confi-dence intervals (95% CIs). Heterogeneity of results was assessed using I2 statistics.Results A total of 454 articles were identified and 42 full-text articles were chosen for further review. Fourteen studieswere included in the quantitative meta-analysis, comprising a total of 928 rosacea patients and 1527 controls. The over-all association between Helicobacter pylori infection and rosacea was non-significant (OR 1.68, 95% CI 1.00-2.84,P = 0.052), but analysis restricted to C-urea breath test showed a significant association (OR 3.12, 95% CI 1.92-5.07,
P < 0.0001). Effect of eradication treatment on rosacea symptoms was assessed in seven studies, but without signifi-cant effect (RR 1.28, 95% CI 0.98-1.67, P = 0.069).Conclusion This meta-analysis found weak associations between rosacea and Helicobacter pylori infection as well asan effect of Helicobacter pylori therapy on rosacea symptoms, albeit that these did not reach statistical significance.Whether a pathogenic link between the two conditions exists, or whether Helicobacter pylori infection represents a proxyfor other factors remains unknown.Received: 2 April 2017; Accepted: 9 May 2017Declaration of interestsDr. Egeberg has received research funding and/or consultancy honoraria from Pfizer and Eli Lilly, and honorariaas consultant and/or speaker from Pfizer, Eli Lilly, Novartis, Galderma and Janssen Pharmaceuticals. IntroductionRosacea is a common, sometimes hereditary, chronic inflamma-tory facial dermatosis that predominantly affects adult women1.It is clinically characterized by transient or persistent facial ery-thema, telangiectasia, papules, pustules and/or phymatouschanges1. The estimated prevalence ranges from 1 to 20%, butrosacea is much less common in dark-skinned individuals1.Aggravating factors include consumption of alcohol, caffeine,smoking and ultraviolet exposure1.Although the underlying aetiology of rosacea is not yet fullyunderstood, several studies have suggested a relationship withHelicobacter pylori infection and small intestinal bacterial over-growth (SIBO)2-7.Helicobacter pylori, a spiral shaped, Gram-negative, micro-aerophilic bacteria, is predominantly found in human gastricmucosa and has been linked to gastrointestinal (GI), dermato-logical and neurological diseases.8-10 In textbooks, experts havehad different opinions about the relationship of Helicobacterpylori and rosacea that have varied between coincidental, plausi-ble or undecided11-13.The prevalence of Helicobacter pylori varies between studypopulation and applied diagnostic methods. As much as one-third of Northern European and Northern American popula-tions, and up to 50% of people in Southern Europe, SouthAmerica and Asia are colonized with Helicobacter pylori14.Importantly, previous studies have compared the prevalence ofHelicobacter pylori infection in rosacea patients and controlsand also investigated effects of Helicobacter pylori treatment onrosacea symptoms and severity3,4,15-19. In a recent study by Salehet al.,20 138 (92%) rosacea patients with a positive Helicobacterpylori stool antigen who underwent Helicobacter pylori eradica-tion treatment had a negative stool antigen at the end of thetrial. As the relationship between rosacea and Helicobacterpylori remains unresolved, we performed a systematic reviewand meta-analysis to examine whether the two conditions areassociated.Methods
Selection of studiesTwo authors (A-H.R.J. and R.G.) systematically searched thePubMed, EMBASE and Web of Science databases using the fol-lowing search terms: PubMed (Rosacea[Mesh] OR Rosacea)AND (Helicobacter[mesh] OR helicobacter infections[mesh]OR helicobacter*), Embase (rosacea.af AND helicobact*.af),Web of Science (ts=rosacea AND ts=helicobact*). Our searchwas limited to English-language and human-only studies, pub-lished between 1 January 1990 and 1 January 2016. The searchyielded 252 non-duplicate results. Titles/abstracts were read todetermine eligibility for inclusion in the systematic review. To beincluded, original studies fulfilled the following inclusion crite-ria: case-control, cross-sectional, cohort or nested case-controldesign; evaluation of Helicobacter pylori infection in conjunc-tion with rosacea; and analyses that compared rosacea patientswith control groups.Data extractionThe Meta-Analysis of Observational Studies in Epidemiology(MOOSE) guidelines were used to guide the analysis21. The sys-tematic review and data extraction were performed indepen-dently by two screeners (A-H.R.J. and R.G.), and any differenceswere adjudicated by A.E. and J.P.T. For each study included, werecorded the study year, country in which the study populationlived, setting in which the study took place, study design, num-bers of case and control subjects, age, sex and data collectionprocesses including method for identifying the presence Heli-cobacter pylori. The Newcastle-Ottawa Scale22 was used to assessstudy quality by categorizing into three dimensions: selection,comparability and exposure for case-control studies. The selec-tion, comparability and exposure dimensions contain four, two
and three items, respectively. A star system was used as a semi-quantitative assessment of study quality. A study was awarded amaximum of one star for each numbered item within the selec-tion and exposure categories. A maximum of two stars wereawarded for comparability. Studies with a score of 0-5 were cat-egorized as lower quality, whereas studies with scores of 6 orabove were categorized as higher quality. The primary outcomewas the association of rosacea with Helicobacter pylori infectionfor each study. As a secondary outcome, the effect of treatmentof Helicobacter pylori infection was assessed in some of theincluded studies. Data extraction was performed by A-H.R.J. Foreach of the studies, the type of medication and duration of itsuse were recorded, as well as previous treatments. Finally, theeffect of treatment was registered.Statistical analysisWe assessed between-study heterogeneity using I2 tests, wheresubstantial heterogeneity was considered with I2 values >50%.We used random-effects (DerSimonian and Laird) models tocompute the pooled odds ratio (OR) of association between rosa-cea and Helicobacter pylori infection. Funnel plots were visuallyinspected to asses for publication bias. All analyses were per-formed using StatsDirect version 3.0.190 (StatsDirect Ltd., Che-shire, UK). P-values of <0.05 were considered significant, andresults were reported with 95% confidence intervals (95% CIs).
ResultsStudy selectionFrom the initial 454 search results, 42 articles were chosen forfull-text review. Among these, 28 studies were excluded, becausethey were review articles, duplicates or single case reports(Fig. 1). Fourteen studies were included in the final analysis,including a total of 928 rosacea patients and 1527 controls. Allstudies were published between 1998 and 2015; six were fromSouthern Europe23-28, two from Northern Europe4,29, two fromthe United States19,30, and one study from Egypt31, Nepal32,Korea33 and Brazil2, respectively. Seven of the included studiesalso examined the effect of therapy on rosacea-related skinsymptoms. Four of these studies were from Southern Eur-ope23,24,27,28, one study was from Northern Europe4, one fromEgypt31 and one from Korea33. Association between Helicobacter pylori infection androsaceaHelicobacter pylori infection was found in 520 (56%) of 928 ofrosacea patients and 748 (49%) of 1527 controls. The combinedOR of Helicobacter pylori infection in rosacea patients com-pared to controls was 1.68, 95% CI 1.00-2.84, P = 0.052(Fig. 3). Subgroup analysis revealed a clear trend towards astronger association between Helicobacter pylori and rosacea instudiesusingC-ureabreathtest(C-UBT)toestablishHelicobacter pylori infection (fixed effects OR 3.12, 95% CI1.92-5.07, P < 0.0001). A positive association between rosaceaand Helicobacter pylori was found in studies from all regionsthat were studied, but it was non-significant in any region whenanalysed separately (Northern Europe: OR 1.37, 95% CI 0.18-10.68; Southern Europe: OR 1.51, 95% CI 0.54-4.22; other studylocations: OR 1.72, 95% CI 0.81-3.67). The effect of treatmentof Helicobacter pylori infection on skin symptoms was assessedin seven studies. A non-significant tendency towards improve-ment of rosacea-related skin symptoms was observed in patientswho underwent such therapy (RR 1.28, 95% CI 0.98-1.67,P = 0.0685) (Fig. 4).DiscussionMain findingsIn this systematic review and meta-analysis, we found a non-sig-nificant association between rosacea and Helicobacter pyloriinfection. Studies using the C-UBT for the diagnosis of Heli-cobacter pylori infection, which is known to have greater diag-nostic value compared to serology8,18,34, found a strongerassociation between the two conditions compared with studiesusing serological tests. As a secondary finding, a non-significantimprovement of skin symptoms in rosacea was observed aftertreatment of Helicobacter pylori.
InterpretationImproved hygiene and increased use of antibiotics, as well aschanging demographics with a tendency towards smaller fami-lies, represent explanatory factors for the reduced prevalence ofHelicobacter pylori infection over the past decades in the indus-trialized parts of the world35. Collectively, these datasuggest that there is a positive relationship between rosacea andGI diseases and that Helicobacter pylori infection could be oneof several causative factors, or a proxy for associated lifestyle fac-tors, presence of other pathogenic bacteria or underlying inflam-mation, respectively.Importantly, emerging epidemiological evidence suggests thatrosacea may be associated with neurological diseases, includingParkinson's disease44,45 and Alzheimer's dementia46. In an olderGerman case-control study, including 70 patients with Parkin-son's disease and 22 controls, rosacea was found in 13 (18.6%)patients47. While the underlying cause remains to be elucidated,there could be a link between the gut, skin and brain. Indeed, inpatients with Parkinson's disease, the entire GI tract appears tobe affected by this disease and the gut may be where the diseaseoriginates48. Additionally, persistent SIBO and Helicobacterpylori colonization have also been suggested to contribute in thedevelopment of GI dysfunction in Parkinson's disease49. Never-theless, while these hypotheses are intriguing, further research isrequired to determine whether a place for rosacea exists in thegut-skin-brain axis.
Strengths and limitationsData from several independent study populations and from dif-ferent parts of the world were included in this meta-analysis,making the results relatively generalizable. Both presence ofHelicobacter pylori infection and validation of attempted eradi-cation therapy were examined and various diagnostic methodsevaluated. Still, this meta-analysis had important limitations.Overall, a low number of patients and studies were included.Moreover, several studies did not mention whether the patientsor controls had previously received treatment for Helicobacterpylori infection, potentially resulting in falsely low Helicobacterpylori infection rate, and lastly, no information on previousantibiotic therapy for rosacea was given. One weakness of thisstudy is that it could not analyse different strains of Helicobacterpylori some of which are more virulent than others and that theeffect of eradication therapy was not examined. In the study bySzlachcic50, it was determined that Helicobacter pylori withCAG-A toxin was associated with rosacea. This form is more vir-ulent in general and is more associated with gastric cancer thanother Helicobacter pylori.ConclusionIn conclusion, our meta-analysis found evidence of a weak, butnon-significant, association between rosacea and Helicobacterpylori infection. As a secondary finding, a non-significant ten-dency towards improvement of rosacea-related skin symptomswas observed following Helicobacter pylori therapy. It remainsunknown whether Helicobacter pylori infection could representa proxy for growth of other intestinal bacteria or underlyingimmune alterations. Future studies should therefore examineother factors than Helicobacter pylori colonization to controlfor possible confounding, including the presence of subtypes ofHelicobacter pylori.References
