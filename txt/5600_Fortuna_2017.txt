REVIEWDrug-induced oral lichenoid reactions: a real clinicalentity? A systematic reviewGiulio Fortuna1,2,3& Massimo Aria4 & Julie H. Schiavo5Received: 27 June 2017 /Accepted: 21 August 2017# Springer-Verlag GmbH Germany 2017
AbstractPurpose Drug-induced oral lichenoid reactions (DIOLRs)have been extensively reported in the literature, but the valid-ity of the causality relationship between any drug and the orallichenoid lesions (OLLs) still remains questionable. Wesought to determine whether this causality relationship reallyexists, whether a resolution of the oral lesions upon withdraw-al occurs, and what the most common alleged offending med-ications are.Methods Nine electronic databases from January 1966 toDecember 2016 were systematically searched to identify allrelevant studies selected with specific inclusion criteria (a clin-ical and histopathological diagnosis of DIOLRs, and clearlystatement on the systemic offending medication). Searchedterms included but not limited to oral lichen planus/orallichenoid lesions/oral lichenoid reactions, the adverse effectsof medication, and drug-induced. Statistical analyses conducted.Results The search retrieved a total of 817 articles, of whichonly 46 were included into a qualitative synthesis: 40 casereports/series and 6 studies. The causality assessment was doneonly in 14.8% of cases with the C-D-R protocol. The Naranjoalgorithm was not reported in the majority of cases (98.2%).Culprit medication was withdrawn in 68.5% of the cases,obtaining a partial or complete resolution without treatmentin 16.7% of cases and with treatment in 27.7% of cases. Themedian number of culprit medication(s) described was 1 withthe most frequent ones being Methyldopa (20.37%), Interferon(IFN)-alpha (11.11%), and Imatinib and Infliximab (9.26%).Conclusion This systematic review demonstrated that there isno strong scientific evidence to support the causal relationshipbetween any drug and oral lichenoid lesions; therefore, in allreviewed cases, we must question whether the DIOLRs rep-resent a real and separate clinical entity. Further and morethorough investigations using one of the available algorithmsfor adverse drug reaction are warranted.

However, different al-gorithms, such as the WHO-UMC system [21], Gardner meth-od, Jones algorithm, or Kramer questionnaire, currently vali-dated in the scientific literature to evaluate ADRs [22], werealso considered if used in the included papers. Additionally, weevaluated the complete or partial resolution of the oral lesionsupon withdrawal without treatment and the most common al-leged offending medication(s).As a secondary endpoint, we sought to evaluate the clinicaland histopathological features of DIOLRs.Materials and methods
Selection strategyThe present study followed the Preferred Reporting Items forSystematic Reviews and Meta-Analysis (PRISMA) guidelines[23], illustrating the outcomes of the literature searches andarticle selection process (Fig. 1). We performed a computerizedliterature search for articles published in nine databases fromJanuary 1966 to December 2016: Ovid MEDLINE, Embase,CINAHL, Scopus, Web of Science, Cochrane Libraries,ProQuest, ClinicalTrials.gov, and Google Scholar. Thesearches were concentrated on the two main topics of orallichen planus/oral lichenoid lesions/oral lichenoid reactionsand the adverse effects of medication, and drug-induced. Wesearched these concepts using controlled vocabulary particularto each database and keywords. MeSH terms included, but notlimited to, Lichen Planus, Oral, Lichenoid lesions, Lichenoidreactions, Lichenoid eruptions AND Mouth, and Drug RelatedSide Effects and Adverse Reactions. The Ovid MEDLINEsearch was altered for each database. Citations and abstractsof retrieved articles were exported into RefWorks, and dupli-cates were removed. The complete search strategies for thethree main database Ovid MEDLINE, Embase, and Scopusare included in the Appendix.In addition, all references retrieved from all included arti-cles were further examined manually to identify other relevantarticles that were not detected by the initial search.
Selection criteriaAfter eliminating duplicates, two investigators (GF and JHS)independently evaluated titles, abstract and full text of all theincluded articles. Any disagreement was discussed and re-solved by consensus between the two investigators.The systematic review included the following: (i) articles(case reports, case series, cohort studies, and case-control)published between January 1966 and December 2016 in theEnglish language, (ii) cases with clinical features of DIOLRswith or without cutaneous and/or other mucous membranesinvolvement, (iii) only cases where diagnosis of DIOLRs wasbased on a histopathological examination (hematoxylin andeosin stain (H&E)) via an oral biopsy with or without a directimmunofluorescence (DIF) analysis, (iv) cases where the cul-prit medication(s) was/were clearly stated, and (v) cases inwhich oral lesions appeared during or after administration ofsystemic medications only.We excluded randomized and non-randomized trials be-cause they are interventional in nature and cross-sectionalstudies as they are performed at a single point in time or timeinterval, so unsuitable to establish a possible causative rela-tionship. We also excluded case(s) with (i) oral involvementbut no oral biopsy was taken, (ii) oral and cutaneousinvolvement with only a cutaneous biopsy or where thelocation of the biopsy was not clearly specified (skin viz.oral), (iii) only cutaneous lesions, (iv) oral lichenoid reac-tions induced by dental materials, and (v) preexisting OLP/OLLs.Data extraction
Studies that met the inclusion criteria were independentlyreviewed by two investigators (GF and JS) who extractedand tabulated in an Excel sheet the following information:year of publication, country of publication, age, gender,number of patients (only for case series), culprit medication,personal history of allergy, family history of allergy, pastmedical history (PMH), concurrent medications at the timeof DIOLRs onset, latency period (defined as the intervalbetween the intake of the suspected offending medicationand the development of oral lesions), clinical features oforal lesions, presence of cutaneous and/or other mucosallesions, histopathological features with H&E, direct immu-nofluorescence (DIF) analyses, withdrawal of the allegedoffending medication, partial resolution upon withdrawalwithout treatment, complete resolution upon withdrawalwithout treatment, type of treatment, partial resolution uponEur J Clin Pharmacolwithdrawal with treatment, complete resolution upon with-drawal with treatment, C-D-R protocol, Naranjo algorithm,time for complete or partial resolution of the oral lesions,and follow-up. The duration of follow-up was either record-ed as mentioned by the authors or calculated on the basis ofthe initial date the patient was in complete clinical remissionand discontinued systemic therapy to the last date of obser-vation reported in the reviewed paper.Statistical analysis
Descriptive statistics were calculated on 54 patients withDIOLRs described in 40 case reports/series, includingmean +- standard deviation for age, medians with interquar-tile range for timing of latency period, partial/complete res-olution of oral lesions, and last follow-up, and frequencydistribution for offending and concurrent medication distri-bution, clinical characteristics, and algorithms used for ad-verse drug reactions.Mann-Whitney U test was used to determine the differenceof medians of latency period and time partial/complete resolu-tion of oral lesions between males (M) and females (F), where-as Spearman's correlation (r) was used to identify any corre-lation between age and latency period and between age andtime partial/complete resolution of oral lesions. Conversely,Kruskal-Wallis analysis was used to evaluate the differenceof medians between age, latency period, and time partial/complete resolution of oral lesions by number of concurrentmediation(s).Lastly, a new software called Bibliometrix ( was used to analyze the frequency of themost used words to describe the clinical and histopathologicalfeatures of DIOLRs.ResultsLiterature searchAs shown in Fig. 1, we selected 155 full-text articles out of810 screened. If a suspect of DIOLRs arises, we mustalso include the timing, i.e., the first onset of symptoms, al-though these might not necessarily coincide with the onset ofOLLs; however, the simplistic timing from introduction or
withdrawal of medication to the unpleasant reaction mightnot be sufficient alone to prove conclusively such causalityrelationship; other parameters of timing must be considered aswell as the pattern of an adverse event and further investiga-tions, such as blood test or biopsies [2].For this reason, in order to ascertain the causality assessmentin any suspected drug-induced adverse reaction, the use the C-D-R protocol or one of the many internationally acceptedalgorithms/protocols for ADRs, such as APS [22], should bemandatory [29], although they both have drawbacks. Indeed,the C-D-R protocol in some cases can be unethical and poten-tially dangerous, because there might not be a valid pharmaco-logical alternative, and there might be a risk of anaphylacticreactions. Additionally, in DIOLRs, oral reactions may takemonths to resolve upon discontinuation of the alleged offendingmedication [4, 18], making even more difficult to interpreterany possible causative relationship. On the other hand, thesestandardized protocols/algorithms for ADR are usually unableto produce a precise and reliable quantitative estimation of re-lationship likelihood, although they are still used as a commonroutine procedure in pharmacovigilance [21].Despite this, of course, we cannot and must not neglect theuse of one of the standardized algorithms, such as APS [22],along with specific guidelines published by the AmericanSociety of Health-System Pharmacists [30] on how to monitorand report an ADR. This would reduce the risk of publishingnew cases of DIOLRs based on previous reports that did notfollow any algorithm/protocol for ADRs.We concur with previous authors [29] that the analysis ofcases of can no longer be based on case reports, reports ofsmall series, and analytical reports, where a meta-analysiscannot be performed. Ideally, we need robust and well-constructed prospective cohort study with a large sample ofOLLs cases vs an age- and sex-matched cohort in order todetermine whether there is a true causal relationship betweendrug ingestion and OLLs onset or whether this association ismerely coincidental [29].However, case reports/case series and/or retrospective stud-ies can still add important information on DIOLRs and shouldnot be neglected, only if well-conducted and well-document-ed, in order to be able to draw a definitive conclusion of acertain association between any medication and OLLs.
LimitationsOur review has several limitations, mainly related to the factthat our analysis is only based on case reports, since the in-cluded studies presented with incomplete data for an accurateanalysis regarding drugs and OLLs. Because of their nature,those case reports are unable per se of depicting an accurateclinical overview of this entity. Over the past decades, we have very often based our causalityassessment of DIOLRs on previous cases, assuming that theywere reported following a scientific methodology, but many ofthem clearly were not, or based on cases published in the derma-tological literature, where oral manifestations might not alwaysbe present and/or an oral biopsy was not always performed.Rebus sic stantibus, we cannot claim that DIORLs--as anew and separate nosological entity--does exist or that doesnot, because of the lack of a scientific methodology applied byclinicians over the past 50 years in diagnosing DIOLRs.
