AbstractHerpes zoster is an acute inflammatory condition which can have a significant impact on quality oflife. Antiviral therapies are effective, but do not meet patients' expectations of symptomatic relief.Acupuncture and moxibustion have been used for herpes zoster; this systematic review evaluatedtheir efficacy and safety. Nine English and Chinese databases were searched from their inceptionsto March 2016. Randomized controlled trials evaluating the combination of acupuncture plus mox-ibustion in adult herpes zoster were included. Outcomes included pain intensity and duration,quality of life and adverse events. Meta-analysis was performed using RevMan software (version5.3). Nine studies (945 participants) were included. Studies were of low to moderate methodologi-cal quality based on risk of bias assessment. Pain intensity (visual analogue scale) was loweramong those who received acupuncture plus moxibustion compared with pharmacotherapy (onestudy; MD 28.25 mm, 95% CI 212.36 to 24.14). The clinical significance of this result is yet tobe established. Some benefits were seen for other pain and cutaneous outcomes, and globalimprovement in symptoms. Mild adverse events were reported in the intervention groups. Acu-puncture plus moxibustion may improve pain and cutaneous outcomes, although current evidenceis limited by the number of studies and methodological shortcomings.
K E Y W O R D Sacupuncture, herpes zoster, moxibustion, review, meta-analysis, systematic, varicella1| INTRODUCTIONHerpes zoster is the result of reactivation of the varicella zoster virus,usually acquired during childhood (Dworkin et al., 2007). The acutestage of herpes zoster is characterized by severe pain and unilaterallesions, with significant impact on the patient's quality of life (Gateret al., 2014; Johnson et al., 2010). Three clinical phases are seen: prodro-mal phase (the occurrence of pain before lesions erupt); acute phase(vesicles develop, postulate, and crust); and the healing phase (disap-pearance of lesions crusts) (Volpi, Gross, Hercogova, & Johnson, 2005).Lesions are typically limited to one dermatome, appear in clusters, andare accompanied by erythema (Dworkin et al., 2007; Gross et al., 2003).As many as 90% of adults are at risk of developing herpes zoster(Ozaki, Miwata, Matsui, Kido, & Yamanishi, 1990). Age is a key risk factor for developingherpes zoster (Volpi et al., 2005), due to decreasing varicella zostervirus specific cell-mediated immunity with advancing age (Gershonet al., 2010). Other risk factors include Caucasian ethnicity (Gershonet al., 2010), female gender (Gershon et al., 2010), family history (Ansar,Farshchian, Ghasemzadeh, & Sobhan, 2014; Hicks et al., 2008), diabe-tes (Heymann et al., 2008), and mechanical trauma (Gershon et al.,Herpes zoster is usually self-limiting in immunocompetent patients(Volpi et al., 2005), with the rash resolving within 3-4 weeks (Carrasco,Straten, & Tyring, 2000). Management with antiviral therapies is rec-ommended for patients over 50 years, immunocompromised patients,and patients with zoster involving more than one dermatome (Dworkinet al., 2007; Gross et al., 2003). Clinical practice guidelines recommendantiviral therapies to hasten healing time (acyclovir, valacyclovir, famci-
clovir, and brivudin) (Dworkin et al., 2007; Gross et al., 2003), and painmanagement treatments such as paracetamol/acetaminophen, opioidanalgesics, and tricyclic antidepressants and anticonvulsants for signifi-cant pain (Dworkin et al., 2007; Volpi et al., 2005).Antiviral therapies can reduce healing time, however a recentCochrane review (Chen et al., 2014), found they were not effective inreducing the incidence of post-herpetic neuralgia (PHN), a commonsequelae of herpes zoster. Gater et al. (2014) evaluated patient satis-faction with treatment for herpes zoster using the Treatment Satisfac-tionQuestionnaireforMedication(TSQM,versionII).Patientsatisfaction was highest on the "side effects" domain and lowest on the"effectiveness" domain of the TSQM. The authors highlighted this asan area of unmet clinical need.Acupuncture has been shown to be an effective treatment forsome types of pain (Lee & Ernst, 2011), and various skin conditions(Chen & Yu, 2003), and may provide benefit during the acute stage ofherpes zoster (Ursini et al., 2011). Moxibustion involves the burning ofthe herb Artemesia vulgaris L. close to the skin, usually near acupunc-ture points. A systematic review of moxibustion for pain found moxi-bustion reduced pain associated with osteoarthritis and may reducepain in scleroma and herpes zoster (Lee, Choi, Kang, Lee, & Ernst,2010). Acupuncture and moxibustion are recommended in Chinesemedicine clinical practice guidelines for treating the acute symptoms ofherpes zoster (Liu et al., 2013), and are frequently used in combinationin clinical practice. This review examined the efficacy and safety of thecombination of acupuncture with moxibustion in the acute stage ofherpes zoster.2| METHODSFive English (PubMed, Embase, CINAHL, CENTRAL, and AMED) andfour Chinese (CBM, CNKI, CQVIP, Wanfang Data) databases weresearched from inceptions to February 2014, with an update search runin March 2016. Search terms were grouped according to condition
(herpes zoster, zoster, varicella zoster virus, and variants), intervention(acupuncture, moxibustion, and variants), and study design (random-ized, controlled, placebo, and variants).Resulting citations were exported to reference management soft-ware for review. Titles and abstracts were screened to identify relevantcitations, and full text retrieved where further review was required.Studies were eligible if they: (a) were randomized controlled trials, (b)included adults (18 years) with acute stage herpes zoster, (c) eval-uated the combination of acupuncture plus moxibustion, and (d) usedguideline recommended treatments (antiviral therapy and/or analge-sics) (Dworkin et al., 2007; Gross et al., 2003; Volpi et al., 2005), notreatment or sham/placebo as the comparator. As diagnosis is usuallyclinical, no additional requirements for diagnosis were applied. Studieswhich evaluated the combination of acupuncture plus moxibustionalone, or as adjunct to guideline recommended treatments wereeligible.
Studies were excluded if the condition being treated was PHN, com-plications of herpes zoster (Ramsay Hunt syndrome, zoster opthalmicus,zoster sine herpete, visceral or disseminated zoster, bacterial infections),or included immunocompromised patients (eg HIV, cancer, diabetes,pregnant, or breastfeeding). Studies where the comparator was herpeszoster vaccine or a Chinese medicine therapy were also excluded.The primary outcomes were pain intensity, measured on visualanalogue scale (VAS), McGill pain score, or other rating scales (e. g.,verbal rating scale), and time to resolution of pain. Secondary outcomesincluded measures of lesion healing (time to resolution of rash, time tocessation of new lesion formation, time to crust formation); healthrelated quality of life (HRQoL), measured on the Zoster Brief PainInventory, Initial Zoster Impact Questionnaire, or other quality of lifemeasures; incidence of PHN; therapeutic effective rate (TER), definedas a lesion improvement by 30% or more, and significant reduction inpain, according to a Chinese guideline (State Administration of Tradi-
tional Chinese Medicine, 1994).Characteristics of studies, details of interventions and comparators,outcomes and results were extracted into a pre-defined spreadsheet.Quality assessment was made using the Cochrane Collaboration's riskof bias tool independently by two researchers (KW, WZ). If disagree-ment was seen in assessments, this was resolved through discussionwith a third researcher (MC).Data were analysed using RevMan software (The Nordic CochraneCentre) Continuous data were presented as mean difference (MD) anddichotomous data were presented as risk ratio (RR), with 95% confi-dence intervals (CI). A random effects model was used for all analyses.Statistical heterogeneity was considered present if p < .10 in the Chisquare test for heterogeneity, and the I2 statistic was greater than 50%.Statistical heterogeneity was explored through sensitivity analysis forstudies assessed as low risk of bias for sequence generation, and furthersub-group analysis was planned according to comparator type and use
of local or distal acupuncture points. If 10 or more studies were includedin a meta-analysis, we planned to explore publication bias through visualinspection of funnel plots. This was not possible, as none of the meta-analyses included 10 studies. The protocol for this review was publishedin PROSPERO (Registration no. Seven stud-ies did not report any information on adverse events.6| DISCUSSIONThe findings from this systematic review suggest potential benefit fromacupuncture and moxibustion in reducing pain intensity, improving
rash healing, and reducing the incidence of PHN, although few studiesreported these outcomes. Meta-analysis of five studies showedimprovement in lesions and significant reduction in pain based on thetherapeutic effective rate. Therapeutic effective rate has not been vali-dated, and should be interpreted with caution.Acupuncture and moxibustion were well tolerated by patients,with few mild side effects reported. Bleeding and hematoma are themost common adverse events with acupuncture (Park, Lee, Choi, Kim,& Choi, 2010; White, Hayhoe, Hart, & Ernst, 2001; Witt et al., 2011),resulting from needles puncturing the capillary vessels (Liang, 2005).Due care minimizes the risk of this occurring. Although no adverseevents were reported related to moxibustion in the included studies,appropriate distance between burning moxibustion cigar and acupunc-ture points should be maintained to avoid empyrosis (Liang, 2005).The findings in relation to incidence of PHN require further inves-tigation. There is no consensus on the definition of PHN, but pain per-
sisting three months after the resolution of the rash is generallyaccepted as the clinical definition and is used for research purposes(Dubinsky, Kabbani, El-Chami, Boutwell, & Ali, 2004). The differingfindings seen when PHN was assessed at one month and three monthsmay relate to the definition used. Pain from acute stage herpes zosterhas been reported to take up to 61 days to resolve (Ahn et al., 2001).In this review, fifty cases of pain were reported one month after rashresolution, while six cases (all in the comparator group) were reportedthree months after resolution of the rash. This follows the trend forzoster related pain to resolve over time, and is not likely to be an indi-cator of the efficacy of acupuncture plus moxibustion.The pathogenesis of acute pain in herpes zoster is the inflamma-tory response and necrosis of the varicella zoster virus infected sensorynerve (Dworkin et al., 2007). Acupuncture and moxibustion are bothimportant analgesic therapies in the acute stage of herpes zoster (Liuet al., 2013). The analgesic effect of acupuncture has been well-
researched. Acupuncture promoted cortical endocrine involvementthrough evoking endogenous opiates, such as b-endorphin, encephalin,dynorphin, and endomorphin (Lin & Chen, 2008). Other research sug-gests acupuncture could produce anti-inflammatory effects via theautonomic nerve system (ANS) in animal models (Sekido, Ishimaru, &Sakita, 2003). The mechanism of the anti-nociception effect of electro-acupuncture is still unclear. Recent animal studies showed that electro-acupuncture may alleviate inflammation by suppressing certain pain-related molecular, protein and mRNA pathways (Du, Fang, Liang, &Fang, 2014; Fang, Du, Liang, & Fang, 2013; Liang, Fang, Du, & Fang,Antiviral therapy (with or without analgesic therapy) is recom-mended as first line therapy for immunocompetent patients over 50years of age (Dworkin et al., 2007; Volpi et al., 2005; Gross et al.,2003). Recently released guidelines suggest antiviral therapy may beinitiated in patients under 50 years (Werner et al., 2017). The recom-mended dose of antiviral drug valacyclovir is one gram three times daily
for seven days, and for oral acyclovir is 0.8 g five times a day for sevendays (Gross et al., 2003). Only one study included in this review usedthe guideline recommended dosage (Lin & Zhao, 2012), while the dos-age of the other studies included in this review was considerably lessthan those recommended in guidelines. When acupuncture plus moxi-bustion was compared with guideline recommended dose of antiviraltherapy, benefits were seen in the time to resolution of pain, time tocrust formation, and time to cessation of new lesions. The study alsoused a random number for group allocation, reducing bias fromsequence generation. In light of this, this study may provide the bestevidence for treatment effects of acupuncture plus moxibustion.Achieving adequate analgesia is a key aim of pharmacological man-agement (Dworkin et al., 2007). Recent guidelines recommend analge-sic treatment follow the WHO pain ladder (Werner et al., 2017).Titration can be considered if analgesia is not achieved (Dworkin et al.,2007), or basic analgesia can be supplemented with tricyclic antide-
pressants or anticonvulsants (Werner et al., 2017). Hence, clinical prac-tice uses an individualized approach to pain management. As standarddoses of pain management medications were used in included studies,it is possible that the findings are not directly translatable to clinicalpractice. Two studies used the anticonvulsant carbamazepine as anal-gesic therapy for managing acute zoster pain (0.1 g per day) (Liu, 2010;Lu, 2004), which was also lower than the guideline recommended dos-age (0.4-1.2 g per day) (Gross et al., 2003). None of the included stud-ies described referring to clinical practice guidelines, and no Chineseguidelines for conventional medical management of herpes zostercould be identified to verify the dosage of antiviral and anticonvulsanttherapies. Finally, several studies (Chen, 2012; Lin & Zhao, 2012;Wang, 2011; Yang, 2014) used topical application of antiviral therapy,which is contrary to clinical practice guidelines. Therefore, the clinicalrelevance of the findings of this review are uncertain.Several studies included in this review used vitamin B medications
or derivatives, which are not recommended in clinical practice guide-lines. In China, the prescribing practice for acute stage of herpes zosteroften combines vitamin B1 and B12 for repairing infected nerves andregulating the function of synaptic transmission (Chen, Wu, & Jiang,2006; Zhang & Huang, 2007). It is unclear whether the addition of vita-min B1 or B12 alters the efficacy of valacyclovir, and results should beinterpreted in light of this.Many of the studies included in this review had methodologicalflaws which limits interpretation of the results. There was a lack ofdetail about the randomization process, blinding and outcome assess-ment. These factors are likely to influence the reliability of the results.With the exception of the VAS, few outcomes in herpes zosterresearch have been standardised or validated. With a complex inter-vention like acupuncture, the selection of objective outcomes becomeseven more important to minimise bias in the results.7| CONCLUSIONSeveral studies have evaluated the combination of acupuncture plusmoxibustion in the acute stage of herpes zoster. While single studieshave produced some positive results, overall there is insufficient evi-dence from high quality studies reporting on objective outcomes. Thelack of detail relating to some outcomes such as time to resolution ofpain or rash limited evaluation of the evidence. Further research com-paring acupuncture plus moxibustion to guideline recommended dosesof pharmacotherapy, which report definitions used for outcomes andwhich use the accepted definition for PHN are needed.ACKNOWLEDGMENTSThe authors appreciate the assistance of Wenyou (Iris) Zhou inassessment of risk of bias.CONFLICT OF INTERESTNo conflict of interest.REFERENCESAhn, H.
