The possible role of human papillomavirus infection in thedevelopment of lichen sclerosusAnna K. Hald1, MD and Jan Blaakaer2, DMSc1Aarhus University, Aarhus, Denmark, and2Department of Obstetrics andGynaecology, Odense University Hospital,Odense, DenmarkCorrespondenceJan Blaakaer, DMSCSkaering Egager 208250 EgaaDenmarkE-mail: Conflicts of interest: None.AbstractLichen sclerosus (LS) is a chronic inflammatory skin disease of unknown originpredominantly affecting the anogenital area that causes pruritus and pain and is associatedwith an increased risk of malignancy. In some cases, LS vanishes after application ofimiquimod, raising the question whether human papillomavirus (HPV) may have anetiopathogenic role in anogenital LS. The databases MEDLINE and Embase weresystematically searched using the PRISMA guidelines. Twenty-seven papers were includedthat reported the prevalence of HPV in LS and in LS associated with neoplasia. HPV wasidentified in 0-80% (median 22%) of all LS cases. The prevalence of HPV was higheramong male patients with LS (median 29%) than among female patients (median 8%).HPV16 was the most prevalent genotype, but the distribution of genotypes indicates thateven low-risk HPV can cause LS. The diverging detection rates are probably due to small
sample sizes in the reviewed papers and different detection methods. Factors possiblyunderestimating the prevalence of HPV are a selective search for high-risk HPV, DNAdestruction in fixed tissue, focally residing HPV, and possibly a clearing of HPV before thetime of biopsy. Seventy-five percent of sexually active people acquire HPV during theirlifetime, thus HPV alone is not a cause of LS. Genetic and immunological host factors andviral factors other than type are likely to contribute. Future studies should include patientswith a short duration of symptoms, and biopsies should be multiple and fresh.IntroductionLichen sclerosus (LS) is a chronic inflammatory skin diseasepredominantly affecting the anogenital area in both sexes.Symptoms range from none to pruritus and pain, with a possibleprogression to scars and anatomic disfigurement and 5%increased risk of squamous cell carcinoma (SCC) in the genitalarea.1 It is more common in women than in men, with aratio between 10 : 1 and 6 : 1, and is estimated to affectbetween 1/300 and 1/1000 in both sexes. LS occurs at all agesbut with a bimodal age incidence peaking in prepubertalchildren, middle-aged men, and postmenopausal women.2
The exact etiology of LS remains unknown, although severalmechanisms have been sought. Cases of familial occurrencehave been reported, suggesting a genetic contribution, but stud-ies have been unable to establish the inheritance pattern.3Because of the prepubertal and postmenopausal peaks in inci-dence seen in women, hormonal factors have been investigatedas a contributing factor to the development of LS but without con-clusive results. A significant association between HLA-DQ7 anti-gen and LS has been demonstrated in one study,4 and anotherstudy5 found 22% of women with LS to have another autoim-mune disorder (most frequently thyroid disease). However, arecent publication questions the causative role of autoimmunefactors.6 In some studies, the lymphogenic infiltrate in LS biop-sies has been shown to contain monoclonal rearranged T-cellreceptors, advocating for a specific local immune reaction to anantigen.7 Several infectious factors, including Borrelia Burgdor-feri, Epstein-Barr virus, and human papillomavirus (HPV), havebeen investigated as external triggers,8 but the exact cause of
LS has not been identified, suggesting a multifactorial etiology.This lack of a causal link complicates targeting of the treat-ment, and for that reason only symptomatic treatment is avail-ableatpresent.Currentfirst-linetreatmentistopicalcorticosteroids that relieve symptoms in 75-90% of patients.1Although spontaneous remission is seen, for most cases thetreatment is lifelong.Interestingly, Vignale et al.9 found that the immunomodula-tory drug imiquimod (ordinarily used to treat HPV-inducedcondylomas and cutaneous neoplasms) could be an effectivetreatment of LS. They reported clinical improvement in twoHPV-positive female patients with LS after adding imiquimod for3 months to topical corticoid treatment. We had the same expe-rience at the Department of Obstetrics and Gynaecology in Aar-hus University Hospital, Denmark, in three patients with vulvarLS in whom the symptoms of LS vanished after application ofimiquimod (J. Blaakaer, unpublished data). This led us to sus-pect that HPV could be directly linked to the development ofLS, followed by a hypothetical immune response triggered byviral antigens. If this is the case, HPV vaccine could possiblyprevent LS. Thus, the purpose of this article is to summarize allthe current published data on the prevalence of HPV in LS inorder to evaluate whether HPV could possibly play a role in thedevelopment of LS.Materials and methodsSourcesA systematic literature search up to September 23, 2016, wasperformed. the prevalence of HPV in histologically verified LS, (ii) studieson the prevalence of HPV in LS associated with invasive genitalcancer, carcinoma in situ, or intraepithelial neoplasia of themale or female genitalia. Preferably, the papers should provideinformation on the distribution of specific genotypes of HPV inLS, although this information was not always available. Articlesnot providing information on specific HPV genotypes were alsoincluded because of the low number of articles on the subject.Included studies had to provide clear information on HPVdetection and genotyping methods. Meta-analyses and reviewswere not included.A total of 774 articles were identified through thedatabase search previously described. Here, HPV-16 and -18 account for 86% of the HPVgenotypes, whereas HPV-6, -11, -31, -33, -45, -52, and -58were not identified (Fig. 2).DiscussionThis review demonstrated a prevalence of HPV in LS patientsranging from 0 to 80%. The diverging detection rates are proba-bly due to the small sample sizes in most of the reviewedpapers and the use of different techniques for detection of HPV.The data presented in this review are solely on genital LS, asthere are no published papers on HPV prevalence in extrageni-
tal LS. Though cutaneotropic HPV and mucosotropic HPV aredifferent types, one could assume that the results on HPV ingenital LS are transmissible to extragenital LS, as host factorsare similar and viral factors other than type (such as viral loadand viral integration) are likely to also be active.Human papillomavirus is the most prevalent sexually trans-mitted genital infection in the world, with a lifetime risk of infec-tion of around 75%,37 raising the question whether detection ofHPV in a given case is causal or incidental. In women, theInternational Journal of Dermatology 2017a 2017 The International Society of Dermatologyestimated global prevalence of HPV in the general population is11.7%,38 compared to a prevalence of 12.4% in Europeanmen.39 This meta-analysis underlines that if only papers pub-lished after 2000 are considered, the HPV prevalence in men issignificantly higher (28.5%). Comparing these prevalences ofHPV in the general population to the median prevalence of HPVin LS demonstrated in this review (overall, 22%; female only,
8% and male only, 29%), the present results seem less notable.The apparent greater correlation between HPV and LS in mencould reflect different etiopathogenesis in men and women orthat the included men belong to a high-risk population. Very lit-tle information is available on the included patients, both menand women, as most papers only report age and pathologicaland clinical signs of LS. It would be highly relevant to explorethe sexual behavior of these patients, including age at first inter-course, number of sexual partners, anal intercourse, and otherrisk factors of acquiring HPV infection to evaluate whether theyhave a high-risk behavior. Only Zhang et al.34 report the num-ber of sexual partners.In contrast to the apparent rise in HPV prevalence in the gen-eral population, some papers12,20,31,33,35 demonstrated a dimin-ishing prevalence of HPV in LS in time. Originating from thesame region and using similar detection methods, Ansink et al.35collected tissue blocks from the period 1952 to 1982, Kagieet al.12 from 1984 to 1993, and van der Avoort et al.31,33 from1992 to 2009 and found prevalences of HPV in LS of 37%, 7%,
and 0%, respectively. A similar pattern is observed in the papersfrom the United States11,21 and partly in the papers fromItaly,14,19,24 while the papers from England21,26,27 and Spain23,29do not show this association. Overall, study populations aresmall, and this possible association could be by chance.Hypothetically,HPVinfectioncouldtriggeranimmuneresponse giving rise to LS and afterward clear before the timeof biopsy, resulting in HPV-negative cases. LS has often beenpresent for many years prior to the development of neoplasia,and furthermore there is an estimated delay of diagnosis of LSof 5 years, a period during which HPV could have resolved.On the other hand, one could argue that patients with genitalLS might be at higher risk for HPV infection as a consequenceof prolonged immunosuppressive treatment or that the alteredimmune response seen in LS itself favors HPV infection. Inother words, HPV infection might be the secondary phe-nomenon to LS. Only one paper divided cases into early- and
late-stage LS,34 thereby considering what comes first as well asthe possible clearing of HPV before time of biopsy. It is argu-able whether these early-stage patients actually represent anearly stage, as they required surgery for their LS, but there isno information on the duration of symptoms. HPV was notdetected in the patients in this study, either in early-stage orlate-stage LS patients or in controls, raising uncertainty aboutthe extraction and detection process. The authors validated thedetection process by performing two runs, and in both runs pos-itive and negative controls were normal, indicating sufficient pro-cessing. However, the study only tested for hrHPV and largerstudies on early-stage and preferably untreated LS is needed todetermine what comes first - HPV or LS.Another possible reason for the absence of HPV in somecases is that HPV can reside focally. Thus, the prevalence ofHPV could be underestimated if the biopsy was taken in anarea without HPV. Of the included papers, only Regauer et al.16address this issue by taking topographically oriented biopsiesand analyzing up to nine different areas within a single LS
lesion.All papers except two8,18 examined fixed tissue. One paperused smear and urine as a source of DNA,18 though this is notsuitable material to detect HPV DNA in epithelial cells. Thequality of DNA extracted from formalin-fixed and paraffin-embedded (FFPE) tissue is the main limiting step in HPV detec-tion because formalin fixation may cause extensive damage tothe DNA.40 The steps taken prior to fixation cannot be takeninto account, as most studies collected stored tissue blocks. Byamplifying the human b-globulin gene, the DNA quality is veri-fied in the papers. PCR has been recognized as the most sensi-tive method, but the sensitivity and specificity of PCR varya 2017 The International Society of DermatologyInternational Journal of Dermatology 2017depending on primer sets, size of the amplified PCR product,and DNA polymerase.41 Applied consensus broad-spectrum pri-mers like MY09/MY11, GP5+/6+, VPH, and CPI/II (Tables 1and 2) amplify a rather big fragment and therefore demand abigger piece of intact DNA, whereas the broad-spectrum primer
SPF10 amplifies a much smaller fragment, increasing the oddsof amplifying possibly damaged DNA in FFPE tissue. Hence,the quality of the available tissue and the applied detectionmethod contribute to the diverging detection rate of HPV in thisreview.hrHPV 16 was the most prevalent genotype observed in thisreview. The identified distribution of genotypes also partlydepends on the applied HPV typing methods used becausesome studies only analyze for a limited number of HPV geno-types10,11,16,28,30 or primarily focus on the oncogenic geno-types.34 In LS without concomitant neoplasia, a mixture of lrand hrHPV is represented (Fig. 2), suggesting that even lrHPVcould trigger the immune response seen in LS. Because of theselective search for hrHPV in some studies, the prevalence ofHPV in LS is possibly underestimated in this review.HPV-16 and -18 were the only common genotypes occurringin LS associated with neoplasia (Fig. 2). This contradicts thehypothesis suggested by Bilenchi et al.42 that lrHPV plays an
oncogenic role in LS. Our finding indicates that HPV-16 and -18are more likely to contribute to the carcinogenic process inthese cases, similarly to the HPV-dependent pathway of genitalcancer or perhaps accelerate the carcinogenic mechanism ofAs a result of the widespread vaccination against HPV, onecould expect a decline in the incidence of LS as an interestingside effect, if indeed HPV antigens are a necessary cause ofreview, approximately 63% of LS could be prevented using thebivalent vaccine (Cervarix) as implemented in the Danishchildhood vaccination programme at age 12. This could beextended further to a coverage of 78% and 88% by vaccinationwith the quadrivalent (Gardasil) and the nonavalent vaccine(Gardasil 9), respectively. Unfortunately, the vaccine is only apart of the childhood vaccination program in girls, and many ofthe patients with childhood LS develop the disease prior to theage of 12. It is well established that children can acquireanogenital HPV infection in a nonsexual manner, for instancethrough vertical transmission or close contact.18 Evidence todate suggests that immunity lasts at least 10 years,43 but it is
unknown whether immunity could last into adulthood and possi-bly protect against adult LS.The paradox of treating a possibly infection-induced diseasewith immunosuppressive medicine has been addressed in multi-ple studies, and treatment of LS with topical corticosteroids inthe prescribed manner is generally considered safe regardingsteroid side effects and opportunistic infections.44 However, vonKrogh et al.15 argue that prolonged use of topical corticosteroids(mean: 7 weeks) could potentially reactivate latent or subclinicalHPV infection. They detected an increase in HPV positivity andhistopathological and clinical signs of HPV in four patients withLS after dermovat therapy, though not significant. Similarly,Bilenchi et al.42 report two cases of HPV reactivation after appli-cation of tacrolimus in anogenital LS. Both papers consider it asa reactivation, indicating that the patients had been infectedearlier, consistent with HPV being present prior to LS. Overall,this is in line with the paper published by Virginale et al.9 thatfound an improvement in symptoms of LS after application ofthe virucide agent imiquimod. Data are scarce and inconclusive
but implicate a pattern of HPV involvement.As previously discussed, at least 75% of individuals acquireHPV at some point during their lifetime. The fact that 75% ofthe population do not develop LS nor do the partners ofpatients with LS, indicates that factors other than HPV infec-tion have to contribute to the development of LS. Geneticand immunological host factors and viral factors other thantype (such as viral load and viral integration) are likely to alsobe active. This review demonstrated that HPV in LS rangesfrom 0 to 80%, but larger studies are needed to clarifywhether this association is a chance occurrence or whetherHPV may really have an etiopathogenic role in anogenital LS.Future studies should preferably include patients with a shortduration of symptoms, addressing the problem of possibleclearing of HPV in older lesions. Biopsies should be fresh,multiple, and topographically oriented to circumvent the possi-ble HPV DNA destruction in FFPE tissue and focally residingHPV. Furthermore, studies should focus on histologic change
